10. FX, Commodities and Alternative Investments

Beyond stocks and bonds sits a sprawling world: the currency market that dwarfs every other, commodities you can only sensibly touch through futures, and the "alternatives" — real estate, private equity, hedge funds, crypto — that dominate institutional portfolios and finance-job postings alike. By the end of this section you'll be able to read an FX quote, hedge a currency exposure, compute a cap rate, and decode a 2-and-20 fee structure.

The FX market: the biggest market you've never seen a building for

The foreign exchange (FX) market turns over roughly $7.5 trillion per day (BIS 2022 survey). For scale: that's more in a week than global stock markets trade in a year. There's no exchange floor — it's a decentralized, 24-hour-a-weekday network of bank dealers, an over-the-counter market like the swaps you met in Section 9 (Derivatives).

Currencies trade in pairs, and the quote convention trips up everyone at first. EUR/USD = 1.10 means one euro costs 1.10 US dollars. The first currency (EUR) is the base currency — the thing being priced, always one unit. The second (USD) is the quote currency — the units on the price tag. So when EUR/USD rises from 1.10 to 1.20, the euro got stronger and the dollar got weaker, even though "USD went up" is what your eye wants to read.

Price moves are measured in pips (0.0001 for most pairs). On a standard €100,000 position, one pip is worth 0.0001 × 100,000 = $10 — which tells you why FX traders care about the fourth decimal. Most volume is spot (exchange now, settle in two days), but a huge share is forwards and swaps — the derivative machinery from Section 9 applied to money itself.

What moves currencies? Mostly the forces you met in Section 5 (Fiscal Policy, Debt & Trade): interest-rate differentials (capital flows toward higher yields, set by the central banks of Section 4), inflation differentials (high inflation erodes purchasing power and, eventually, the exchange rate), and trade and capital flows (persistent deficits need financing). Layer on risk sentiment — in a panic, money runs to the dollar, yen and Swiss franc — and you have most of the story.

Rate differentials also power the most famous FX strategy: the carry trade. Borrow yen at 0.5%, convert, invest in Australian assets at 4.5%, and pocket the 4-percentage-point gap — as long as exchange rates sit still. That's the catch. Carry trades earn small steady gains and then, occasionally, lose catastrophically when everyone unwinds at once: a 5% yen rally erases more than a year of carry in days. Traders call it "picking up nickels in front of a steamroller," and the August 2024 yen-carry unwind — which briefly knocked global equities down several percent — showed the steamroller is real.

How companies live with FX

For a multinational, currency risk isn't a trading opportunity — it's weather. It comes in two flavors. Transaction exposure is risk on actual cash flows: you've agreed to pay or receive foreign currency at a future date, and the rate can move before it does. Translation exposure is an accounting effect: a US parent must restate its German subsidiary's euro results in dollars, so reported revenue swings with the exchange rate even if the business itself never changes. A subsidiary earning €50M shows up as $55M at 1.10 but only $50M at parity — a $5M "decline" with zero operational cause. (You'll see this flagged as "constant-currency growth" when you read filings in Section 11.)

Transaction exposure is the one companies actively hedge, usually with the forwards from Section 9. The logic: give up the upside to delete the downside, because a manufacturer's edge is manufacturing, not currency speculation.

Worked example: a European exporter hedges dollar revenue

A German machine-tool maker signs a contract to receive $5,000,000 in six months. Its costs are in euros, so a weakening dollar (EUR/USD rising) directly shrinks its margin. It sells the dollars forward at today's six-month forward rate of 1.10, locking in $5,000,000 ÷ 1.10 = €4,545,455, guaranteed.

Six months later, suppose spot has moved to 1.20. Unhedged, the $5M would convert to only $5,000,000 ÷ 1.20 = €4,166,667. The hedge preserved €378,788 — likely more than the entire profit margin on the machines. If instead spot had fallen to 1.00, the unhedged value would have been €5,000,000 and the hedge "cost" €454,545 of foregone upside. That's the deal: certainty in both directions.

Your edge

Corporate FX desks and treasury analytics teams are heavy consumers of data skills: forecasting exposures from ERP data, computing hedge ratios, simulating rate scenarios. "Treasury analyst" is one of the most underrated data-adjacent entry points into finance.

Deep dive: where forward FX rates come from

Forward rates are not a forecast — they're arbitrage arithmetic, exactly like the futures pricing in Section 9. If US one-year rates are 4% and euro rates are 2%, the one-year forward must satisfy F = S × (1 + rUSD) ÷ (1 + rEUR) = 1.10 × 1.04 ÷ 1.02 ≈ 1.1216. Why: holding dollars for a year earns 4%; holding euros earns 2% plus whatever the currency does. If the forward sat anywhere else, you could borrow one currency, lend the other, lock the forward, and print riskless profit. This relationship is called covered interest parity, and it's one of the most reliably enforced identities in finance. Note the implication: the higher-yielding currency trades at a forward discount — the forward market "gives back" the carry.

Commodities: futures curves, oil and the strange case of gold

Commodities — oil, gas, wheat, copper, gold — are the only asset class you could theoretically dump in your driveway. That's exactly why almost nobody holds them physically: storage, insurance and transport are expensive, and a barrel of oil pays no dividend while it sits there. So financial investors get exposure through the futures contracts from Section 9: standardized, liquid, no warehouse required.

But futures introduce a subtlety that catches even professionals: the shape of the futures curve drives your returns as much as the commodity's price does. Plot the futures price against contract maturity. If longer-dated contracts cost more than spot, the curve is in contango — normal when storage is costly. If they cost less, it's in backwardation — typical when the physical commodity is scarce right now.

Here's why it matters. Futures expire, so a long-term investor must roll: sell the expiring contract, buy the next one out. In contango you repeatedly sell the cheap near contract and buy the expensive far one, and as each contract ages it tends to slide down the curve toward spot. That systematic drag is negative roll yield — an oil ETF can lose money over a year in which spot oil is flat, purely from rolling. In backwardation the same mechanics work in your favor. If you've built a data pipeline with scheduled jobs, the analogy is apt: roll yield is a recurring cost (or credit) baked into the schedule, independent of what the underlying data does.

Two market notes. Oil is the macro commodity: benchmarks WTI (US, NYMEX) and Brent (global, ICE) trade in contracts of 1,000 barrels, and the price feeds straight into the inflation dynamics of Section 3. In April 2020, WTI futures famously settled at negative $37 — storage was full, and holders paid to escape taking physical delivery. That episode is the cleanest proof you'll find that a futures price is about delivery logistics, not just abstract value.

Gold is the odd one out. It has almost no industrial use in proportion to its price and generates no cash flow, yet central banks hold about 35,000 tonnes of it. The honest framing: gold is the anti-currency — a monetary asset that no government can print. It tends to rise when real interest rates fall (cash pays less, so gold's zero yield hurts less) and when trust in monetary policy wobbles. You can't value it with the discounting tools of Section 1; you can only reason about its supply, its demand and its 5,000-year incumbency as the asset people run to.

Real estate and REITs: leverage plus a yield you can compute

Real estate is the world's largest asset class by value, and its defining feature is leverage. Nobody buys a $500,000 building with $500,000 of cash; they put down $100,000 and borrow $400,000. Now a 10% rise in the property's price ($50,000) is a 50% gain on your equity — and a 20% fall wipes your equity to zero. Same asset, radically different risk, purely because of the financing. Keep that multiplier in mind for Section 16 (Risk Management); it's the engine of most property busts, 2008 included.

The valuation workhorse is beautifully simple. A property's net operating income (NOI) is rent minus operating costs (maintenance, insurance, property tax, management — but not the mortgage). The cap rate is:

Cap rate = NOI ÷ Price
NOI = annual rent − operating costs (excluding mortgage) · Price = purchase price · result = unlevered yield

Worked example

A rental property costs $500,000, brings in $42,000 of annual rent and costs $12,000 a year to run. NOI = 42,000 − 12,000 = $30,000, so the cap rate is 30,000 ÷ 500,000 = 6.0%. Compare that to the 10-year Treasury from Section 8: if Treasuries yield 4%, the property offers a 2-percentage-point spread as compensation for illiquidity, vacancies, leaky roofs and tenant risk. If Treasuries rose to 6%, that spread vanishes — which is exactly why property prices sag when rates climb.

Because buildings are lumpy and illiquid, markets built a liquid wrapper: the REIT. A REIT is a listed company that owns portfolios of properties — apartments, warehouses, cell towers, data centers — and, in exchange for paying no corporate tax, must distribute at least 90% of taxable income as dividends. You get diversified property exposure with stock-market liquidity: buy VNQ or a single REIT in seconds. The trade-off: because REITs trade like the stocks of Section 7, they crash like stocks too — in 2008 they fell alongside equities even as "real estate diversification" was the reason people held them. Liquidity has a price: it imports equity-market volatility.

Private equity and venture capital: illiquidity as a business model

Private equity (PE) buys entire companies off the public market — or ones that were never on it. The signature move is the leveraged buyout (LBO), which is the real-estate leverage trick applied to a whole business. One worked paragraph: a PE firm buys a company generating $125M of EBITDA for 8× EBITDA = $1,000M, funding it with $400M of its own equity and $600M of debt loaded onto the company. Over five years the firm pushes EBITDA to $150M and uses the company's cash flow to pay debt down to $300M. It exits at the same 8× multiple: $1,200M, minus $300M of debt, leaves $900M of equity — 2.25× the money in, roughly a 17.6% annualized return. Notice the sources of profit: some operational improvement, but a lot of leverage and debt paydown. That's the LBO in one breath.

Venture capital (VC) plays the opposite statistical game. Instead of leveraging stable cash flows, it buys lottery tickets governed by a power law. A stylized fund makes twenty $1M investments: thirteen go to zero, five limp back their $1M each, one returns 5×, and one returns 30×. Total: $40M on $20M invested — a respectable 2× — and the single 30× winner returned more than everything else combined. For a data scientist this should feel familiar: it's an extreme-skew distribution where the mean is meaningless without the tail, and "median startup outcome" is a rounding error above zero. VC diligence is therefore tail-hunting, not average-estimating.

Both run on the same fund plumbing. Investors — pensions, endowments, sovereign funds — are limited partners (LPs); the firm managing the money is the general partner (GP). LPs commit capital that the GP calls down over several years as it finds deals. Fees follow the famous 2-and-20: roughly 2% annual management fee plus 20% of profits — the profit share is called carried interest — often only above a hurdle rate of ~8%. The cash-flow rhythm produces the J-curve: early years are all outflows (capital calls, fees) and markdowns, then exits start returning cash and the curve climbs.

Common pitfall: smooth ≠ safe

Private funds report returns from appraised valuations, updated quarterly by the GP itself, not from market prices. Stale, managed marks make the return series look eerily smooth — low "volatility," low "correlation" with stocks. Much of that is measurement artifact, not economics: if you only reprice your house once a year, its price chart is smooth too. Practitioners call this volatility laundering. When you build risk models in Section 16, de-smoothing private-asset returns is a real analytical task.

Hedge funds: the strategy zoo and the fee arithmetic

A hedge fund is best defined by what it's allowed to do that a mutual fund isn't: short selling, heavy leverage, derivatives, concentration. The name is historical; many hedge funds hedge nothing. The main strategy families:

Now the fee arithmetic, because it's brutal and interview-relevant. Take 2-and-20 with the common simple convention: management fee first, then 20% of what remains. A fund earning 10% gross nets its investor (10% − 2%) × 0.80 = 6.4%. Compound that for a decade on $100: gross wealth grows to $259, net wealth to $186. The manager's take is the gap — $73, which is 46% of the $159 of gross profit. To justify itself net of fees, a hedge fund can't merely match the market; it needs persistent, large outperformance — exactly the thing Section 17 (Market Efficiency) says is rare.

One more filter for reading hedge-fund marketing: survivorship bias. Hedge fund indices are built from funds that voluntarily report; funds that blow up stop reporting and vanish from the history, and new funds often "backfill" only their good early years. Studies put the resulting overstatement of average returns at several percentage points a year. You already know this bug from ML — it's training on a dataset where the failures were silently dropped. Always ask what happened to the funds that aren't in the sample.

Crypto as an asset class: how an analyst frames it honestly

We'll cover blockchain mechanics in Section 19 (FinTech); here, treat crypto purely as a portfolio asset. Three facts dominate. First, volatility: bitcoin's annualized volatility has historically run 60–80%, versus roughly 15–20% for the S&P 500 — drawdowns of 70–80% have happened repeatedly. Second, custody risk: owning crypto means controlling cryptographic keys, and custody failures — lost keys, hacked wallets, or the exchange itself collapsing, as FTX did in 2022 — can produce a 100% loss with no market move at all. That failure mode simply doesn't exist for a Treasury bond in Section 6's custodial plumbing.

Third, and analytically deepest: no cash flows. A stock has earnings, a bond has coupons, a building has rent — so the discounting machinery of Section 1 and Section 13 (Valuation) can produce an intrinsic value to argue about. Bitcoin pays nothing, so its value rests entirely on scarcity (a hard-coded 21M supply cap) plus what the next holder will pay. That doesn't make it worthless — gold has traded on the same basis for millennia — but it changes the epistemics: there is no model that says bitcoin is "cheap at $40,000," only supply, demand, and narrative. The honest analyst's framing: a high-volatility, custody-fragile, cash-flow-free speculative asset whose diversification benefit is unstable — its correlation with tech stocks, near zero in the 2010s, jumped sharply in the 2022 tightening cycle, exactly when diversification was needed. Sized accordingly (small, survivable), it's a position; sized on faith, it's a lottery ticket with a login page.

Why alternatives at all — and the Part II instrument map

After all these fees, lockups and custody nightmares, why do pensions and endowments pour trillions into alternative investments? One word: correlation. Portfolio risk depends not just on each asset's volatility but on how assets move together — combine assets with correlation below 1 and the portfolio's volatility drops below the weighted average of the parts, a free lunch you'll derive properly in Section 15 (Portfolio Theory). Real assets that track inflation, macro funds that profit in chaos, farmland that ignores the Nasdaq: each imperfect correlation is a diversification credit. Two caveats keep you honest: correlations tend to spike toward 1 in a crisis (2008 taught that painfully), and some of the advertised low correlation is the appraisal smoothing you saw above. The pitch is real, but smaller than the brochure.

Check your understanding

1. EUR/USD rises from 1.10 to 1.25. Who is hurt?

EUR/USD rising means each euro costs more dollars. Anyone who must pay euros — like the importer — now needs more dollars to settle the same invoice. Euro receivers and euro-asset holders gain, and the European tourist finds America cheaper.

2. An oil ETF rolls its long futures position monthly while the curve is in steep contango and spot oil stays flat all year. The ETF most likely…

In contango the next contract is always pricier than the expiring one, and each contract tends to slide down toward spot as it ages. That negative roll yield produces losses even when spot goes nowhere.

3. A hedge fund returns 10% gross. Under 2-and-20 (management fee taken first, then 20% of the remainder), the investor nets about…

(10% − 2%) × 0.80 = 6.4%. The 2% management fee comes off the top, then the manager keeps 20% of the remaining 8%.

4. A building generates $30,000 of NOI and is priced at $600,000. Its cap rate is…

Cap rate = NOI ÷ price = 30,000 ÷ 600,000 = 5.0%. (20% is the inverted ratio — price over NOI gives the "multiple," 20×, not the yield.)

5. The core risk of a carry trade (borrow yen at 0.5%, invest in Australian dollars at 4.5%) is that…

Carry accrues slowly, but the FX loss when the low-rate funding currency rallies can arrive all at once — especially in a crowded unwind, as in August 2024. Steady nickels, occasional steamroller.

Key takeaways

  • FX quotes price the base currency in units of the quote currency: EUR/USD = 1.10 means one euro costs $1.10, and a rising quote means a stronger euro.
  • Currencies move on rate differentials, inflation gaps and capital flows; the carry trade harvests the rate gap and periodically gets crushed when it unwinds.
  • Companies hedge transaction exposure with forwards — trading upside away to lock certainty — while translation exposure is an accounting effect you'll meet in filings.
  • Commodity investors live on the futures curve: contango bleeds negative roll yield, backwardation pays positive roll yield, regardless of what spot does. Gold is the cash-flow-free anti-currency.
  • Real estate = leverage plus a computable yield: cap rate = NOI ÷ price, judged as a spread over Treasuries; REITs make property liquid at the cost of stock-like volatility.
  • PE and VC run on GP/LP fund mechanics, 2-and-20 with carry, and a J-curve; VC returns follow a power law where one winner carries the fund, and smooth private "returns" partly reflect stale appraisals.
  • Fee drag compounds: 10% gross becomes 6.4% net under 2-and-20 — $259 vs $186 on $100 over a decade — and survivorship bias inflates the track records you're shown.
  • Crypto is a high-volatility asset with custody risk and no cash flows to discount; alternatives in general earn their place only through genuine (not appraisal-smoothed) low correlation — the bridge to Section 15.
