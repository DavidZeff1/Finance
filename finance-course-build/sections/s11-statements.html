11. Financial Statements: The Language of Business

Every company on Earth describes itself with the same three documents, and every analyst job you might want assumes you can read them fluently. By the end of this section you will be able to walk a single transaction through all three statements and explain why the balance sheet can never, ever fail to balance.

Why statements exist, and where to find them

A company is a black box that takes in money and (hopefully) produces more money. Financial statements are the API documentation for that box — a standardized, periodic report of what happened inside. They exist because the people who supply capital (shareholders, lenders) are usually not the people running the business, and they need a trustworthy way to check on their money. Regulators, tax authorities, employees, suppliers, and competitors read them too, but the primary audience is investors and creditors.

There are three statements, and they answer three different questions. The income statement asks: did we make a profit this period? The balance sheet asks: what do we own and owe right now? The cash flow statement asks: where did the cash actually go? A period report, a snapshot, and a reconciliation — think of them as a diff, a database dump, and a transaction log.

To keep the reports comparable, companies follow accounting rulebooks. US companies use GAAP; most of the rest of the world uses IFRS. They agree on the big ideas and differ in details (inventory methods, lease treatment, when development costs can be capitalized), so cross-border comparisons need care but not fear. An independent accounting firm performs an audit and signs an opinion that the statements fairly present reality — a useful check, though auditors verify process more than they hunt fraud.

For US public companies, everything is free. The 10-K is the audited annual report; the 10-Q is the quarterly version. Both live on EDGAR, the SEC's filing database (sec.gov/edgar). One career note before we dive in: this is the single most job-critical section of this course for analyst roles. Interviews for equity research, investment banking, credit, and FP&A all test exactly the material on this page — especially the three-statement linkage.

The accrual revolution: earned, not received

Here is the single biggest aha in accounting. Statements are not built on cash. They are built on accrual accounting: revenue is recorded when it is earned (you delivered the goods or service), and expenses are recorded when they are incurred (you consumed the resource) — no matter when money changes hands. The goal is to measure economic performance in the right period, not to track the bank account.

Meet our running example company: Cascade Roasters, Inc., a coffee roaster with $1,000M of annual revenue (we will use its full numbers in a moment). In December, Cascade ships $120k of beans to a hotel chain that pays 60 days later. Under accrual rules, December's income statement shows $120k of revenue recognition, and the balance sheet shows a $120k account receivable — an IOU asset. The bank account shows nothing until February.

Worked example: accrual and cash telling opposite stories

Two gyms open in January. Gym A sells 100 annual memberships at $1,200 each, all paid up front: $120,000 of cash lands in January, but it has only earned one month of service, so January revenue is $10,000 — the other $110,000 sits on the balance sheet as deferred revenue, a liability (it owes 11 months of gym).

Gym B bills companies $10,000 for January corporate memberships, payable in March. January revenue: $10,000. January cash: $0. Same January performance on the income statement; wildly different bank accounts. Accrual profit and cash flow are two different signals about the same business — you need both.

Expenses mirror this. If Cascade prepays a year of insurance, the cash leaves now but the expense hits the income statement one-twelfth per month. If employees earn December wages paid in January, December bears the expense. The machinery that makes this work is the subject of the next three subsections.

Now the vocabulary. COGS covers only the direct cost of what was sold; subtracting it gives gross profit. Operating expenses (opex) are the overhead of being a company: S&M (sales & marketing), R&D (research & development), and G&A (general & administrative — executives, lawyers, HR). Subtract those and you get EBITDA, beloved by bankers because it strips out financing and accounting-policy noise.

Then come the non-cash charges. Depreciation expenses a machine's cost over its life instead of all at once; amortization does the same for intangibles — together, D&A. Subtracting D&A gives EBIT, then interest and tax take their cuts, leaving net income. Note the ordering encodes a priority queue of claims from Section 6: suppliers and employees get paid inside COGS and opex, lenders take interest, the government takes tax, and shareholders get whatever residual remains.

The balance sheet: an invariant that never breaks

The balance sheet is a snapshot at one instant — midnight on the last day of the period. Its whole structure is one identity:

Assets = Liabilities + Equity
what the company owns = who financed it (lenders) + who owns the residual (shareholders)

An asset is a resource with future value; a liability is a promise to outsiders; equity is whatever is left for the owners. Why does it always balance? Because of double-entry bookkeeping: every transaction is recorded twice, in a way that preserves the identity. In CS terms, Assets = Liabilities + Equity is an invariant, and every transaction is an atomic commit that must leave the invariant intact. Borrow $50? Cash (asset) +50 and debt (liability) +50. Buy a $30 roaster with cash? One asset up 30, another down 30 — net zero. Earn $10 of profit? Assets are up 10 and equity is up 10. There is no legal sequence of entries that can break it; if a balance sheet does not balance, someone made an arithmetic error, full stop.

The statement is sorted by liquidity. Current assets (cash, receivables, inventory) convert to cash within a year; non-current assets like PP&E are the long-lived machinery. Same split on the right: accounts payable and short-term debt are current liabilities; bonds and long-term loans are non-current. The gap between the two short-term sides is working capital — the money trapped in the gears of daily operations. Remember it: it is the villain of the cash flow story below.

Two more residents you will meet constantly. Goodwill appears when a company overpays (relative to identifiable net assets) in an acquisition — it is the accounting placeholder for "brand, team, synergies," and it gets written down when acquisitions sour. And equity itself is often called book value — the accountant's estimate of net worth, which can differ wildly from the stock market's estimate (Section 7): the market prices future cash flows, while the book records historical costs.

The cash flow statement: profit is an opinion, cash is a fact

Accrual accounting is powerful, but it runs on estimates: when revenue is earned, how long a roaster lasts, which receivables will actually be paid. Cash is the one number with no judgment in it — hence the analyst proverb, profit is an opinion, cash is a fact. The cash flow statement reconciles the two, sorted into three buckets.

CFO — cash flow from operations. Almost every company builds this with the indirect method: start from net income, then repair the accrual distortions. Add back non-cash expenses (D&A reduced profit but no cash left the building). Then adjust for working capital changes: if receivables grew, you booked revenue you have not collected — subtract it; if inventory grew, you spent cash on beans you have not sold — subtract; if payables grew, suppliers financed you — add. For Cascade in a steady year: net income 181.7 + D&A 50 ± working capital 0 = CFO 231.7.

CFI — cash flow from investing. Dominated by capex, plus acquisitions and purchases or sales of securities. Cascade spends 80 on new roasting lines: CFI −80. CFF — cash flow from financing: money moving between the company and its capital providers — borrowing and repaying debt, issuing shares, buybacks, and dividends. Cascade pays a 30 dividend and raises nothing: CFF −30. Net change in cash: 231.7 − 80 − 30 = +121.7, so cash climbs from 100 to 221.7. That ending number is the same "cash" line on the balance sheet — first glimpse of the linkage.

FCF = CFO − capex
free cash flow: cash the business generates after maintaining and growing its asset base — the raw material of valuation (Section 13)

Free cash flow for Cascade is 231.7 − 80 = 151.7. Why do analysts obsess over it instead of net income? Because growth consumes cash even while it manufactures profit. A company doubling every year must buy inventory and extend credit ahead of the revenue: profits look great, working capital devours the bank account. Plenty of profitable companies have died mid-growth-spurt because they ran out of actual money.

Common pitfall

"Net income up, so cash must be up" is the classic beginner error — and its mirror image matters too. Amazon ran near-zero accounting profit for years while gushing operating cash; many dot-com era firms showed rising profits while cash bled out through receivables. When profit and CFO diverge for several periods in a row, that gap is the first thing to investigate (Section 12 turns this into formal ratios).

How the three statements connect

The statements are not three files; they are three views of one database, joined on shared keys. Learn these four joins and you can rebuild any of them from the other two:

  • Net income → equity and CFO. The income statement's bottom line flows into balance-sheet equity as retained earnings (net income minus dividends), and the same number is the first line of the cash flow statement.
  • Capex → PP&E and CFI. Cash spent on equipment leaves through CFI and lands on the balance sheet as PP&E.
  • Borrowing → cash and debt via CFF. Raise debt and CFF brings cash in while the liability side records the obligation.
  • Ending cash closes the loop. The cash flow statement's final number is the balance sheet's cash line.

Worked example: the classic interview question

"Walk me through $10 of extra depreciation" — asked in nearly every banking and research interview. At a 21% tax rate: Income statement: D&A +10, so EBIT −10, tax −2.1, net income −7.9. Cash flow statement: start −7.9, add back the non-cash +10, so CFO +2.1 — cash actually rises because of the tax shield. Balance sheet: cash +2.1 and PP&E −10, so assets −7.9; retained earnings −7.9 on the other side. Assets down 7.9 = equity down 7.9. Balanced. Now try it live below.

Reading a real 10-K in 15 minutes

A 10-K runs 100–300 pages, and most of it is boilerplate. Analysts triage. Here is a 15-minute routine that extracts perhaps 80% of the value:

  • Minutes 1–3: the three statements. Revenue trend, margins, CFO versus net income, debt level, cash. You now know the shape of the business.
  • Minutes 4–8: MD&A. Management explains, in prose, why the numbers moved. Skim for what changed year over year and how they describe demand, pricing, and costs.
  • Minutes 9–11: segment data. A "cloud company" that earns 70% of profit from legacy licenses is a different investment than its headline suggests. Segments reveal the real mix.
  • Minutes 12–14: footnotes, selectively. Revenue recognition policy, debt maturities, and anything the auditor flagged as a critical audit matter. Footnotes are where inconvenient facts go to hide in plain sight.
  • Minute 15: risk factors, diff only. The section is mostly legal boilerplate — but compare it against last year's filing and read what is new. Companies quietly disclose emerging problems here first. (A CS reflex that serves you well: never read the file, read the diff.)

Red flags preview

Keep a mental alarm list while you skim: net income growing while CFO shrinks for multiple periods; receivables or inventory growing much faster than revenue; frequent "one-time" charges that recur every year; auditor changes; heavy reliance on non-GAAP metrics that always look better than the GAAP ones. Section 12 turns these into quantitative ratio tests.

Your edge

Every filing on EDGAR is machine-readable — modern filings carry XBRL tags, and the SEC publishes bulk financial-statement datasets. While most analysts read one 10-K at a time, you can parse thousands: screen for the receivables-versus-revenue divergence above across the whole market, or diff risk-factor text programmatically. Statement literacy plus data skills is precisely the combination Section 18 builds toward.

Deep dive: why revenue recognition is where fraud lives

Most accounting scandals are, at bottom, revenue-timing scandals, because "earned" involves judgment. Under the ASC 606 standard, a company must identify its contractual performance obligations and recognize revenue as each is satisfied — but multi-year contracts, bundled products, and bill-and-hold arrangements leave room to be aggressive. Classic tricks: channel stuffing (shipping unordered product to distributors at quarter-end and booking it as sales), recognizing multi-year contract value up front, and booking barter or round-trip transactions as revenue.

The tell is almost always on the other statements: fictional revenue creates receivables that never convert to cash, so days sales outstanding stretches and CFO decouples from net income. Enron, one of the largest frauds in history, reported rising profits while operating cash flow told a much uglier story. This is why the linkage matters — a lie on one statement must leave fingerprints on the other two, because the invariant always has to hold.

Check your understanding

1. Cascade delivers $200k of coffee to a client in December; the client pays in February. Under accrual accounting, when does Cascade record the revenue?

Accrual accounting recognizes revenue when it is earned — at delivery. Until payment arrives, the $200k sits on the balance sheet as an account receivable.

2. Depreciation increases by $10 (tax rate 21%), nothing else changes. What happens to the company's cash?

Net income falls $7.9 (10 less 2.1 of tax saved), but the full $10 is added back on the cash flow statement since no cash left. Net effect: +10 − 7.9 = +$2.1 — the tax shield.

3. Why can a legitimate balance sheet never fail to balance?

The identity is an invariant. Every transaction is a balanced entry — like an atomic commit that must leave the invariant intact — so no sequence of valid entries can break it.

4. A fast-growing retailer reports record net income, but CFO has been negative for four straight quarters. The most likely explanation is:

Working capital consumes cash during growth: inventory is bought and credit extended before revenue is collected. Profit is an opinion; cash is a fact — and this divergence is normal for fast growers but deadly if financing dries up. (Faster depreciation would raise CFO, not lower it.)

5. A company reports CFO of $500M and capex of $180M. Its free cash flow is:

FCF = CFO − capex = 500 − 180 = $320M: the cash left over after operating the business and maintaining its asset base. Capex is very much a cash outflow, just reported in CFI rather than CFO.

Key takeaways

  • Three statements, three questions: the income statement measures period profit, the balance sheet snapshots what is owned and owed, the cash flow statement tracks actual cash. US GAAP and IFRS are the two rulebooks; audited 10-Ks and 10-Qs are free on EDGAR.
  • Accrual accounting records revenue when earned and expenses when incurred — so reported profit and the bank balance can tell opposite stories in any given period.
  • The income statement is a cascade — revenue → gross profit → EBITDA → EBIT → net income — and the margin at each checkpoint tells you where the money leaks.
  • Assets = Liabilities + Equity is an invariant enforced by double-entry bookkeeping: every transaction is a balanced entry, so the sheet cannot fail to balance.
  • Profit is an opinion, cash is a fact: CFO starts from net income, adds back non-cash charges, and subtracts the working capital that growth traps. FCF = CFO − capex.
  • The statements link through shared lines: net income feeds retained earnings and the top of the CFS; capex flows through CFI into PP&E; the CFS's ending cash is the balance sheet's cash.
  • Read 10-Ks by triage — statements, MD&A, segments, footnotes, then the diff of the risk factors — and treat a persistent profit-versus-CFO divergence as the number-one red flag.
  • For analyst interviews, the three-statement walk-through (e.g., $10 of depreciation) is the single most-tested skill in this course. Drill it until it is reflex.
