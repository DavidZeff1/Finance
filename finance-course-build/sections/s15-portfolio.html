15. Portfolio Theory: Diversification, CAPM and Factors

Individual assets are noisy; portfolios are engineered systems whose risk you can shape with covariance math. This section gives you the core machinery — diversification, the efficient frontier, CAPM, and factor models — and it is the most "data science in disguise" material in the whole book.

Measuring risk and return: mean and standard deviation

Portfolio theory starts by compressing an asset's future into two numbers. The expected return E[r] is the mean of the return distribution — what you'd earn on average across many repetitions. Volatility (σ) is the standard deviation of returns — how widely outcomes scatter around that mean. If a stock has E[r] = 8% and σ = 18%, a typical year lands somewhere in the −10% to +26% band, and roughly one year in three lands outside it.

Convention: both numbers are annualized so everything is comparable. Means scale linearly with time, but standard deviations scale with the square root of time, because variances — not standard deviations — add across independent periods. A stock with 1.2% daily volatility has annualized volatility of about 1.2% × √252 ≈ 19% (252 is the number of US trading days in a year). You already know this rule: it's the same √n that governs the standard error of a sample mean.

σannual = σdaily × √252  ·  E[r]annual ≈ E[r]daily × 252
variance adds over independent periods, so σ grows with √time — the same √n as a standard error

Why does everyone use standard deviation rather than, say, drawdowns or tail quantiles? Convenience. If returns were normally distributed, mean and σ would describe the whole distribution, and everything in this section becomes clean linear algebra. That assumption is good enough to build powerful tools — and quantifiably wrong in the tails.

Common pitfall: the normal assumption

Real markets have fat tails. Under a normal distribution with σ = 1% daily, a −7% day is a 7-sigma event that should happen roughly never in the life of the universe; the S&P 500 has had many. Treat everything in this section as a first-order model that works well in the middle of the distribution. Section 16 (Risk Management) deals honestly with the tails.

Diversification: the only free lunch

Here is the one result in finance that gives you something for nothing. Combine two assets and the portfolio's expected return is just the weighted average — no surprise. But the portfolio's risk is generally less than the weighted average of the two risks, and the discount is controlled entirely by correlation (ρ). For a two-asset portfolio with weight w in asset A:

σp2 = w2σA2 + (1−w)2σB2 + 2w(1−w)ρσAσB
σp = portfolio volatility · w = weight in A · ρ = correlation between A and B · the last term is 2w(1−w) × covariance(A, B)

Read the three terms as a variance decomposition: A's own variance, B's own variance, and a cross term that ρ can shrink or even flip negative. At ρ = +1 the formula collapses to (wσA + (1−w)σB)2 — a perfect square, risk is the plain weighted average, diversification does nothing. As ρ falls, the cross term shrinks and portfolio risk drops while expected return is unchanged. At ρ = −1 you can pick w = σB ÷ (σA + σB) and drive risk to exactly zero.

Worked example

Stocks: E[r] = 8%, σ = 18%. Bonds: E[r] = 3.5%, σ = 6%. Correlation ρ = 0.1. Put 60% in stocks. Expected return: 0.6 × 8 + 0.4 × 3.5 = 6.2%. Variance: 0.36 × 324 + 0.16 × 36 + 2 × 0.6 × 0.4 × 0.1 × 18 × 6 = 116.64 + 5.76 + 5.18 = 127.58 (in %²), so σp = 11.3%. The naive weighted average of volatilities is 0.6 × 18 + 0.4 × 6 = 13.2% — correlation just handed you 1.9 points of risk reduction for free.

Analogy: model ensembling

This is exactly why ensembles beat single models. Average k models with uncorrelated errors and the error variance drops by 1/k; if the errors are correlated, the benefit caps out at the shared component. Diversification is ensembling for money: idiosyncratic noise averages away, the correlated part survives.

That surviving part has a name. Systematic risk is the market-wide component: recessions, rate shocks, pandemics hit everything at once. Idiosyncratic risk is company-specific: a botched product launch, an accounting scandal. Hold N stocks in equal weights and the portfolio variance tends toward σ²(1/N + (1 − 1/N)ρ̄), where ρ̄ is the average pairwise correlation. The 1/N term — the idiosyncratic part — dies fast; the ρ̄σ² floor never moves.

That decay curve is the empirical answer to "how many stocks do I need?": 20–30 well-spread holdings capture most of the diversification benefit, which is why concentrated single-stock positions (say, all your net worth in your employer's equity) carry risk the market never pays you for. That last clause is the punchline of this whole section, and CAPM will make it precise.

The efficient frontier

Now flip from analyzing one mix to choosing among all of them. Sweep the weight w from 0 to 1 (and consider every combination when there are many assets) and plot each portfolio as a point in risk–return space: σ on the x-axis, E[r] on the y-axis. The cloud of achievable portfolios is the feasible set; with many assets it looks like a bullet lying on its side.

Two landmarks matter. The leftmost tip of the bullet is the minimum-variance portfolio — the lowest-risk mix that exists. And the upper edge of the bullet is the efficient frontier: for every level of risk, the portfolio with the highest expected return. Any point inside the bullet is dominated — some frontier portfolio offers more return at the same risk, or the same return at less risk. A rational mean-variance investor therefore lives on the frontier; the only remaining question is where on it, which is a matter of risk appetite.

A result that surprises everyone the first time: with the stock/bond numbers above, the minimum-variance mix is about 7% stocks, 93% bonds, with σ ≈ 5.8% — less than the 6% volatility of bonds alone. Adding a riskier asset lowered total risk, because low correlation lets stock wiggles partially offset bond wiggles. Risk is a property of the portfolio, not a sum over its parts. This is Harry Markowitz's 1952 insight, and it won a Nobel Prize. Play with it yourself:

Adding a risk-free asset: the capital market line

The frontier gets a dramatic upgrade the moment you add a risk-free asset — think 3-month Treasury bills, whose return rf is known in advance and whose volatility is essentially zero. Any blend of the risk-free asset with a risky portfolio P plots on a straight line from (0, rf) through P, because mixing with a zero-variance asset scales both the excess return and the risk linearly.

So which risky portfolio should you draw your line through? The one giving the steepest line — the line that just grazes the frontier. Its point of tangency is the tangency portfolio, and the line itself is the capital market line (CML). The slope of any such line is the portfolio's Sharpe ratio:

Sharpe = E[rp] − rfσp
excess return per unit of risk — the slope of the line from rf through your portfolio; the tangency portfolio maximizes it

The punchline is called two-fund separation: every mean-variance investor should hold the same risky portfolio — the tangency portfolio — and adjust risk only by moving along the line. Conservative? Put 40% in the tangency portfolio, 60% in T-bills. Aggressive? Borrow at (near) the risk-free rate and put 130% in — that's leverage, and it extends the CML past the tangency point. Risk appetite changes your position on the line, never the risky mix itself. In the widget above, the dashed line through your mix is exactly this line — maximizing its slope is the whole game.

CAPM: beta, the security market line, and priced risk

Two-fund separation has a radical implication. If everyone holds the same tangency portfolio, then in equilibrium that portfolio must be the whole market — every asset held in proportion to its market value. The CAPM (Capital Asset Pricing Model, Sharpe 1964) follows: the only risk the market pays you for is an asset's contribution to market risk, measured by beta (β).

Your edge: beta is just OLS

β is literally the slope coefficient from regressing the asset's excess returns on the market's excess returns: β = Cov(ri, rm) ÷ Var(rm). Concretely: if Cov(ri, rm) = 0.000486 on daily data and Var(rm) = 0.000324, then β = 1.5 — the stock moves 1.5% for a 1% market move, on average. The regression's R² tells you how much of the stock's variance is systematic; the residual variance is the idiosyncratic part that diversification kills. And the intercept has a famous name: alpha. When a fund manager claims alpha, they are claiming a positive regression intercept — always ask for the standard error.

E[ri] = rf + βi × ERP
ERP = equity risk premium = E[rm] − rf · βi = Cov(ri, rm) ÷ Var(rm)

Worked example: with rf = 4%, ERP = 5% and β = 1.2, the model says E[r] = 4 + 1.2 × 5 = 10%. Plotting E[r] against β gives the security market line (SML). An asset priced to return more than the line demands is undervalued (positive alpha — buyers should bid its price up); one below the line is overvalued. Note the distinction from the CML: the CML lives in σ-space and holds only for efficient portfolios; the SML lives in β-space and, under CAPM, holds for every asset — because only the systematic slice of risk is priced. Volatility you can diversify away earns you nothing.

How does CAPM hold up empirically? Its core qualitative claim — diversifiable risk is not rewarded, co-movement with the market is what's priced — has aged well. Its quantitative claims have not: measured over decades, the relationship between beta and average return is flatter than the model predicts (low-beta stocks do too well, high-beta stocks too poorly), and beta alone leaves large, persistent return differences unexplained. Yet CAPM remains everywhere in practice because it is simple and disciplined: it is the standard engine for the cost of equity inside Section 13's DCF and WACC machinery, and beta is still the default first coordinate of any risk model.

Factors: return sources beyond beta

Those "large, persistent return differences" got names. A factor is a characteristic that sorts assets into groups with reliably different average returns. Researchers build them the way you build features: compute a signal per stock, rank the universe, go long the top slice and short the bottom, and measure the spread. The canonical set:

FactorSignal (the "feature")Classic finding
ValuePrice relative to fundamentals (low P/B, P/E — see Section 12)Cheap stocks have outperformed expensive ones long-run
SizeMarket capitalizationSmall caps have outperformed large caps (weakest premium)
MomentumTrailing ~12-month return, skipping the last monthRecent winners keep winning for months
QualityProfitability, earnings stability, low leverageProfitable, stable firms outperform junk
Low volatilityTrailing volatility or betaBoring low-vol stocks beat what CAPM predicts

Fama and French formalized the first extensions in 1992–93; their three-factor model (market, size, value) plus momentum and later profitability became the standard benchmark for judging any fund. The modern regression is your CAPM regression with more columns: ri − rf = α + βmkt·MKT + βsize·SMB + βval·HML + … + ε. Whatever alpha survives that regression is much rarer than CAPM alpha — most "stock-picking skill" turns out to be repackaged factor exposure.

Factor investing — marketed as smart beta — packages these tilts into cheap, rules-based funds. For a data person this is home turf, so bring your ML instincts, especially the paranoid ones. A factor is a feature engineered on a finite, autocorrelated sample; the field has published hundreds ("the factor zoo"), and most fail out-of-sample — textbook overfitting via multiple hypothesis testing. The half-dozen above survive because they replicate across countries, decades and asset classes and have plausible economic mechanisms: either compensation for real risk (value stocks crash hardest in recessions) or persistent behavioral patterns (Section 17) that are costly to arbitrage away. Section 18 shows you how factor research is actually run — and how backtests lie.

Passive vs active: Sharpe's brutal arithmetic

Active management tries to beat the market; passive investing buys the whole market via an index fund and accepts the market return. In 1991 William Sharpe pointed out that the contest is settled by arithmetic, not opinion. Every share is held by someone, so passive investors and active investors together hold the market portfolio. Passive investors earn the market return by construction. Therefore active investors, in aggregate, also earn exactly the market return — before costs. After their higher fees and trading costs, the average actively managed dollar must underperform the average passive dollar. No model, no assumptions about efficiency — just accounting.

The data cooperates. S&P's SPIVA scorecards (spglobal.com/spiva) find that roughly 90% of US large-cap funds underperformed the S&P 500 over the trailing 15 years, with similar figures across most categories and countries. Skilled managers exist, but identifying them in advance is the hard problem: past outperformance barely persists, and much of what looks like alpha is factor exposure you could buy for a few basis points.

Worked example: what a 1% fee really costs

Invest $100,000 for 30 years. At 7% you end with 100,000 × 1.0730 ≈ $761,000. Pay a 1% annual expense ratio and compound at 6% instead: ≈ $574,000. The fee looked like 1% but consumed 25% of your final wealth, because — as Section 1 warned — costs compound exactly like returns. The fee-drag widget in Section 19 lets you feel this interactively.

Real world

This arithmetic reshaped an industry: index funds and passive ETFs went from a curiosity in 1976 (Vanguard's first index fund was mocked as "Bogle's folly") to holding roughly half of US fund assets today, and fee competition has pushed broad-market index funds below 0.05% per year. For most portfolios, the highest-confidence "alpha" available is simply refusing to pay unnecessary fees.

Deep dive: the n-asset case is one line of linear algebra

Everything above generalizes instantly. Stack the weights into a vector w and the pairwise covariances into the covariance matrix Σ: portfolio variance is σp2 = wTΣw, and expected return is wTμ. The efficient frontier is the solution set of "minimize wTΣw subject to wTμ = target" — a quadratic program with a closed form when short sales are allowed.

The catch is estimation, not optimization. For n = 500 assets, Σ has n(n+1)/2 = 125,250 free parameters, estimated from noisy, non-stationary returns — and the optimizer greedily exploits every estimation error, loading up on whatever spurious correlations your sample contains ("error maximization"). Practitioners fight back with regularization you already know: shrinkage estimators (Ledoit–Wolf pulls the sample covariance toward a structured target), factor models that reduce Σ to a low-rank-plus-diagonal form, and constraints on weights. Naive mean-variance optimization on raw sample estimates is the finance equivalent of fitting a 125,000-parameter model without regularization and shipping it to production.

Check your understanding

1. You hold a 50/50 mix of two assets and their correlation falls from +0.8 to +0.1 (volatilities unchanged). What happens?

Expected return is a weighted average of the assets' returns, so correlation doesn't touch it. But ρ multiplies the cross term 2w(1−w)ρσAσB in the variance, so lower ρ means lower portfolio risk at the same return — the free lunch.

2. Which kind of risk does holding 30 stocks instead of 1 essentially eliminate?

Company-specific surprises average out across ~20–30 holdings (the 1/N term in portfolio variance). Market-wide shocks hit every holding at once — that systematic floor is why the decay curve in Figure 15.1 flattens near 15% instead of falling to zero.

3. Risk-free rate 3%, equity risk premium 6%, stock beta 1.5. What expected return does CAPM assign?

E[r] = rf + β × ERP = 3% + 1.5 × 6% = 12%. Note that only systematic exposure (β) enters — the stock's total volatility is irrelevant under CAPM.

4. Portfolio A: E[r] = 9%, σ = 20%. Portfolio B: E[r] = 6%, σ = 8%. Risk-free rate 2%. Which has the better risk-adjusted performance, and why can a leveraged investor prefer it even while wanting more risk?

Sharpe(A) = (9−2)/20 = 0.35; Sharpe(B) = (6−2)/8 = 0.50. Borrowing to hold 2.5× portfolio B gives σ = 20% with E[r] = 2% + 2.5 × 4% = 12% — same risk as A, three points more return. The slope of the line is what matters, not the point you start at.

5. Why must the average actively managed dollar underperform the average passively managed dollar after costs?

This is Sharpe's arithmetic: it needs no assumption about market efficiency or skill. Aggregate active holdings equal the market minus passive holdings, so gross returns match; net returns then differ only by costs, which are higher for active.

Key takeaways

  • Risk and return are summarized as mean and standard deviation of returns, annualized with the √time rule — a normal-distribution convenience that understates tail risk (Section 16).
  • Diversification is covariance math: portfolio variance is w²σA² + (1−w)²σB² + 2w(1−w)ρσAσB, so lower correlation cuts risk at zero cost in expected return.
  • Roughly 20–30 holdings eliminate most idiosyncratic risk; the systematic (market) component is a floor nothing diversifies away.
  • Rational investors hold portfolios on the efficient frontier; adding a risk-free asset collapses the choice to one tangency portfolio plus a lending/borrowing dial along the capital market line, whose slope is the Sharpe ratio.
  • CAPM prices only systematic risk: E[r] = rf + β × ERP, with β the OLS slope against the market and alpha the regression intercept. It powers cost-of-equity estimates (Section 13) even though the empirical beta-return line is flatter than it predicts.
  • Factors — value, size, momentum, quality, low-vol — are engineered features with historically positive premia, wide error bars, and a serious overfitting problem; judge any manager's alpha against a multi-factor regression.
  • Sharpe's arithmetic guarantees the average active dollar loses to passive after fees; about 90% of US large-cap funds trail their index over 15 years, and a 1% fee compounds into ~25% of final wealth over 30 years.
