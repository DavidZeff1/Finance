12. Ratio Analysis: Reading a Company Like a Pro

In Section 11 you learned to read the three financial statements — the raw tables. Ratios are the queries you run against those tables, and by the end of this section you'll be able to size up a company's profitability, safety, efficiency, and price tag in about ten minutes.

Why ratios: the great normalizer

Raw financial numbers are almost meaningless on their own. "This company earned $1.25 billion last year" — is that good? For a startup, spectacular. For Walmart, a catastrophe. The number only becomes information when you divide it by something: profit per dollar of sales, profit per dollar of assets, debt per dollar of earnings. Division strips out size, and once size is gone you can compare a corner grocer to a global chain, or this year's company to the same company a decade ago.

Two golden rules govern every ratio you will ever compute. Rule one: always compare against peers. A 2.5% net margin is dreadful for a software firm and perfectly healthy for a supermarket — every industry has its own gravity. Rule two: always compare against history. A company whose margin slid from 8% to 5% over three years is telling you a story that a single snapshot never could. A ratio in isolation, with no peer and no trend, is close to worthless.

And one rule about what ratios are for: they generate questions, not answers. If inventory turnover suddenly halves, the ratio doesn't tell you why — maybe demand collapsed, maybe management is deliberately stocking up before a price increase, maybe a new warehouse just opened. The ratio's job is to point your flashlight at the right paragraph of the 10-K. Analysts who treat ratios as verdicts get burned; analysts who treat them as anomaly detectors get promoted.

Your edge: this is feature engineering

You already do this for a living. Raw statements are the source tables; ratios are derived features — normalized, scale-invariant, comparable across entities and time, exactly like converting raw counts to per-capita rates or click totals to click-through rates before feeding a model. Quant equity funds literally use these ratios as model inputs (Section 18). The whole section is one big feature_pipeline.py for companies.

Throughout this section we'll follow two deliberately opposite companies: SteadyMart, a grocery chain ($50 billion of revenue, thin margins, warehouses full of inventory), and CloudNine, a SaaS firm ($2 billion of revenue, fat margins, no inventory at all). Same ratios, wildly different profiles — and that contrast is the fastest way to build intuition.

Profitability: margins, ROA, ROE, ROIC

Profitability ratios come in two families: profit per dollar of revenue (margins) and profit per dollar of capital (returns). The margins form a ladder down the income statement from Section 11:

  • Gross margin = (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue. What's left after the direct cost of the product itself. SteadyMart: (50,000 − 37,500) ÷ 50,000 = 25%. CloudNine: (2,000 − 400) ÷ 2,000 = 80% — serving one more customer costs a SaaS firm almost nothing.
  • Operating margin = Operating income ÷ Revenue. After SG&A, R&D, depreciation. SteadyMart: 2,000 ÷ 50,000 = 4%. CloudNine: 400 ÷ 2,000 = 20%.
  • Net margin = Net income ÷ Revenue. The bottom line. SteadyMart: 1,250 ÷ 50,000 = 2.5%. CloudNine: 300 ÷ 2,000 = 15%.

Now the returns family. ROA (net income ÷ total assets) asks how much profit each dollar of stuff produces: SteadyMart 1,250 ÷ 20,000 = 6.25%; CloudNine 300 ÷ 3,000 = 10%. ROE (net income ÷ equity) asks how much each dollar of the owners' money earns: SteadyMart 1,250 ÷ 8,000 = 15.6%; CloudNine 300 ÷ 2,400 = 12.5%. Notice something strange: CloudNine crushes SteadyMart on every margin, yet SteadyMart's ROE is higher. Hold that thought — the DuPont decomposition below explains it.

The most important return of all is ROIC, because it ignores financing tricks and measures the business engine itself:

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested capital = EBIT × (1 − t) ÷ (Equity + Debt − Cash)
NOPAT = net operating profit after tax · t = tax rate · invested capital = all funding actually at work

SteadyMart: NOPAT = 2,000 × (1 − 0.23) ≈ 1,540; invested capital = 8,000 + 7,500 − 1,000 = 14,500; ROIC ≈ 10.6%. CloudNine: NOPAT ≈ 308; invested capital = 2,400 + 200 − 1,200 = 1,400; ROIC ≈ 22%. Here CloudNine's quality shows: it needs barely any capital to run.

Key idea: ROIC vs the cost of capital is THE test

Every dollar a company invests has a price — the cost of capital, the return its lenders and shareholders demand (formalized as WACC in Section 14). A company creates value only when ROIC exceeds that hurdle. ROIC of 10.6% against an 8% cost of capital creates value; ROIC of 7% against 9% destroys value, and growing faster just destroys it faster. This single comparison is the spine of valuation in Section 13.

DuPont: taking ROE apart like an engineer

ROE is the headline number shareholders watch, but it hides how the return was earned. In the 1910s an analyst at DuPont noticed you can factor it into three ratios that each tell a separate story — multiply revenue/revenue and assets/assets into the definition and the algebra telescopes:

ROE = Net incomeRevenue × RevenueAssets × AssetsEquity = Margin × Turnover × Leverage
margin = pricing power · asset turnover = asset efficiency · leverage = balance-sheet amplification

The classic contrast is a supermarket versus a luxury brand. A supermarket earns roughly 2% net margin but flips its asset base 3× a year and runs 3× leverage: 2% × 3.0 × 3.0 = 18% ROE. A luxury handbag maker earns a 30% margin but turns its assets only 0.5× a year with modest 1.2× leverage: 30% × 0.5 × 1.2 = 18% ROE. Identical destination, opposite routes — one is a volume machine, the other a pricing machine.

Our two companies replay the same story. SteadyMart: 2.5% × 2.50 × 2.50 = 15.6%. CloudNine: 15% × 0.67 × 1.25 = 12.5%. That's why the "worse" business had the higher ROE: it compensates for razor-thin margins with relentless turnover and a leveraged balance sheet. The decomposition also flags risk — an ROE built on the leverage lever can be inflated at will by borrowing, and it evaporates just as fast in a downturn. Two firms with the same ROE are not the same investment.

Liquidity and solvency: can it pay its bills, and can it survive?

Liquidity asks whether the company can cover the next twelve months of bills; solvency asks whether the whole capital structure is sustainable. Lenders — the people you met in Section 8 (Fixed Income) — live and die by these ratios, and they hard-code them into loan contracts as covenants.

The short-term pair: the current ratio (current assets ÷ current liabilities) and the stricter quick ratio, which throws out inventory because you can't always sell it quickly at full price. SteadyMart: current = 4,700 ÷ 4,500 = 1.04, quick = (1,000 + 700) ÷ 4,500 = 0.38. CloudNine: both = 1,600 ÷ 400 = 4.0. On paper SteadyMart looks scary — but a grocer converts inventory to cash daily, so a low ratio is normal for the industry. Golden rule one again: thresholds are industry-relative. The gap between the two ratios is essentially working capital tied up in inventory.

Common pitfall: liquidity ≠ solvency

A company can be liquid but insolvent (plenty of cash today, hopeless debt load long-term) or solvent but illiquid (valuable assets, no cash this quarter — the classic bank-run shape from Section 6). Firms almost always die of illiquidity first: the cash runs out before the balance sheet formally fails. Check both, always.

Efficiency and the cash conversion cycle

Efficiency ratios measure how fast a company spins its working capital. Three clocks matter. Inventory turnover (COGS ÷ inventory) counts how many times a year the shelves are cleared: SteadyMart does 37,500 ÷ 3,000 = 12.5 turns, i.e. goods sit for DIO = 365 ÷ 12.5 ≈ 29 days. DSO (receivables ÷ revenue × 365) counts how long customers take to pay: SteadyMart's shoppers pay at the register, so DSO ≈ 5 days; CloudNine invoices enterprises on slow terms, so DSO = 400 ÷ 2,000 × 365 = 73 days. DPO (payables ÷ COGS × 365) counts how long the company takes to pay its own suppliers: SteadyMart stretches to about 39 days.

Chain the clocks together and you get the single most underrated ratio in finance, the cash conversion cycle:

CCC = DIO + DSO − DPO
days your cash is trapped = days goods sit + days customers owe you − days you owe suppliers

SteadyMart: 29.2 + 5.1 − 38.9 = −4.6 days. Read that timeline carefully: it buys groceries on day 0, sells them around day 29, collects by day 34 — and doesn't pay its supplier until day 39. Customers' cash arrives before the supplier's bill leaves. CloudNine, despite its beautiful margins, runs 0 + 73 − 54.8 ≈ +18 days: every dollar of new sales temporarily consumes cash while invoices age.

Real world: Amazon's negative-CCC superpower

For most of its history Amazon has run a CCC around minus 20 to 30 days: customers pay by card instantly, inventory turns fast, and suppliers wait two to three months. Every incremental sale therefore generates float — an interest-free loan from suppliers that grows automatically with revenue. That float helped fund Amazon's expansion for decades with less need for outside capital. A negative CCC means growth feeds cash instead of eating it, which is why analysts treat it as a structural superpower.

Market ratios: what the price tag says

Everything so far used only the statements. Market ratios add one outside ingredient — the stock price from Section 7 — and ask: what are investors paying for all this? SteadyMart trades at a market cap of 20,000; CloudNine at 9,000.

  • P/E = price ÷ earnings. SteadyMart: 20,000 ÷ 1,250 = 16×. CloudNine: 9,000 ÷ 300 = 30×. The market pays almost twice as much per dollar of CloudNine's profit — it's pricing in growth and that 22% ROIC. Best for stable, profitable firms; meaningless when earnings are negative or temporarily depressed.
  • P/B = price ÷ book value. SteadyMart 2.5×, CloudNine 3.75×. Most useful for banks and insurers, whose assets are financial and marked close to reality; least useful for firms whose real assets (brands, code) never hit the balance sheet.
  • EV/EBITDA = enterprise value ÷ EBITDA. EV adds net debt to market cap, so the ratio is capital-structure-neutral — ideal for comparing firms with different debt loads, and the workhorse of buyout analysis. SteadyMart: (20,000 + 6,500) ÷ 2,800 ≈ 9.5×. CloudNine: (9,000 − 1,000) ÷ 460 ≈ 17.4×.
  • FCF yield = free cash flow ÷ market cap. SteadyMart: 1,150 ÷ 20,000 = 5.75%; CloudNine: 350 ÷ 9,000 ≈ 3.9%. The hardest multiple to fake, because it's built on cash (remember Section 11: cash is the ground truth).
  • Dividend yield = dividends ÷ price. SteadyMart pays 500, so 2.5%; CloudNine pays nothing, reinvesting everything at its 22% ROIC — which, given the ROIC-vs-cost-of-capital test, is exactly what it should do.

A crucial reframe before Section 13: a multiple is not a valuation, it's shorthand for one. "30× earnings" is only cheap or expensive relative to the growth, ROIC, and risk behind it. Valuation is the discipline of checking whether the multiple is justified; ratios just tell you which multiples deserve the interrogation.

Earnings quality and red flags: a forensic mini-checklist

Every ratio above trusts the reported numbers. But Section 11 taught you that accounting runs on accruals — management judgment calls about when revenue is earned and costs are incurred. Earnings quality is the question of how much judgment is propping up the bottom line, and a short forensic checklist catches most of the classic games:

  • Net income growing while operating cash flow shrinks. Profits are an opinion; cash is a fact. When the two diverge for several quarters, the "profit" is increasingly made of accruals — book revenue that hasn't been collected, or costs that haven't been recognized. This single check would have flagged Enron years early.
  • Receivables growing faster than revenue. If sales rise 10% but receivables rise 30%, the company is either extending desperate credit terms or channel stuffing — borrowing next quarter's sales to make this quarter's number. Watch DSO trend upward.
  • Serial "one-time" charges. A restructuring charge every single year is not one-time; it's a recurring cost that management wants you to exclude. Compare "adjusted" earnings to plain GAAP earnings — a persistently widening gap is a tell.
  • Aggressive capitalization. When a company chooses to capitalize everyday costs, expenses vanish from the income statement into the balance sheet, inflating today's profit. WorldCom capitalized $3.8 billion of routine network fees this way — for a while, it worked.
  • Inventory outrunning sales. Stock piling up faster than revenue usually precedes markdowns and margin pain — the turnover ratio sniffs it out before management admits it.

Notice that every flag is itself a ratio or a comparison of growth rates — CFO ÷ net income, ΔAR ÷ Δrevenue, adjusted ÷ GAAP earnings. Forensic accounting is ratio analysis with a suspicious mind. None of these flags proves fraud; each one earns the company a spot on your "read the footnotes" list.

Deep dive: when ratios break

Every ratio has failure modes, and knowing them separates users from tourists. Negative equity: a company that has bought back more stock than it ever earned (Home Depot and Starbucks have both been here) can have negative book equity, making ROE and debt/equity mathematically meaningless — the denominators flip sign. Use ROIC and net debt/EBITDA instead. Negative earnings: P/E is undefined for loss-makers; analysts fall back on EV/Sales or forward earnings estimates, each with its own hazards. EBITDA skepticism: EBITDA ignores capital expenditure entirely, which is why Warren Buffett quips that managements touting EBITDA are hoping you'll forget that depreciation is a real cost — a telecom that must replace its network every seven years cannot pay creditors with EBITDA. Point-in-time denominators: balance-sheet items are snapshots, so a retailer's year-end inventory (measured after the holiday sell-down) understates its true average; careful analysts use averages of beginning and ending balances. The pattern: whenever a ratio looks absurd, check whether a denominator is small, negative, or unrepresentative before drawing any conclusion.

Check your understanding

1. Firm A has 10% net margin, 1.5× asset turnover, 1.0× leverage. Firm B has 2% margin, 2.5× turnover, 3.0× leverage. Both show 15% ROE. Which statement is most accurate?

DuPont shows the levers: A earns its 15% with no leverage at all, while B multiplies a thin margin by 3× borrowed money (2% × 2.5 × 3.0 = 15%). B's ROE would collapse fastest in a downturn, when leverage cuts both ways.

2. A company earns 7% ROIC while its cost of capital is 9%, and it's reinvesting heavily to grow revenue 20% a year. What is it doing to shareholder value?

Value is created only when ROIC exceeds the cost of capital. Earning 7% on money that costs 9% loses 2 cents per dollar invested, so pouring more capital in at that spread just scales up the loss.

3. A retailer has a cash conversion cycle of −8 days. What does that mean?

Negative CCC means DPO exceeds DIO + DSO: money comes in before it goes out. Growth then generates interest-free float rather than consuming cash — the Amazon-style superpower.

4. Net income has grown 15% a year for three years, but operating cash flow has fallen each year and DSO keeps climbing. The most likely explanation?

Rising DSO plus profits diverging from cash means revenue is being recognized well ahead of collection — possibly channel stuffing. (Note the third option has it backwards: depreciation lowers net income, not operating cash flow.)

5. You want to compare two similar manufacturers, one debt-free and one financed 60% with debt. Which multiple compares their operating businesses most cleanly?

P/E is distorted by capital structure — the levered firm's earnings are reduced by interest while its equity is smaller. EV/EBITDA prices the whole business (equity plus net debt) against profit measured before financing costs, making the two comparable.

Key takeaways

  • Ratios normalize away size — they are engineered features derived from raw statements. Always compare against peers and against the company's own history, and treat every ratio as a question generator, not a verdict.
  • Margins (gross → operating → net) measure profit per dollar of sales; ROA, ROE, and ROIC measure profit per dollar of capital. ROIC versus the cost of capital is the single test of whether a company creates value.
  • DuPont factors ROE into margin × turnover × leverage: a 2%-margin supermarket and a 30%-margin luxury house can post identical ROE through opposite levers — and ROE built on leverage is the fragile kind.
  • Lenders live on liquidity and solvency ratios: quick ratio near 1, net debt/EBITDA under ~3×, and interest coverage above ~4–5× are the classic comfort zones, all industry-dependent.
  • The cash conversion cycle (DIO + DSO − DPO) counts the days cash is trapped in operations; a negative CCC means suppliers finance the business and growth generates float instead of consuming cash.
  • Market multiples are shorthand valuations: P/E for stable earners, P/B for banks, EV/EBITDA across capital structures, FCF yield when you want the number hardest to fake.
  • Earnings quality checks are ratios too: cash flow diverging from net income, receivables outrunning revenue, serial one-time charges, and aggressive capitalization are the four flags that catch most accounting games.
