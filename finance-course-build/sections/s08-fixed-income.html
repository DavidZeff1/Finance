8. Fixed Income: Bonds and Interest Rates

The bond market is bigger than the stock market, and it is where interest rates — the price of money itself — actually get set. By the end of this section you will be able to price a bond from scratch, explain why prices and yields move in opposite directions, read a yield curve, and estimate exactly how much a rate move will cost a bondholder.

Bond anatomy: a loan cut into pieces

A bond is a loan that has been sliced into standardized, tradable pieces. When you buy a bond you are the lender; the issuer is the borrower. Because the payments are contractually fixed in advance, this whole asset class is called fixed income.

Three numbers define the contract. The face value (or par value, typically $1,000) is what you get back at the end. The coupon is the interest payment — a 5% coupon on $1,000 face pays $50 a year (US bonds actually pay half every six months; we will use annual payments to keep the math clean). The maturity is when the principal comes back. That is it: a bond is a fully specified cash-flow schedule, known on day one. The name "coupon" is literal — paper bonds once had detachable coupons you clipped and mailed in to collect interest.

Who issues them? Four big families:

  • Treasuries — debt of the US federal government (see Section 5 for why it borrows). T-bills mature within a year, notes run 2–10 years, bonds 20–30. Treated as the closest thing to a risk-free benchmark, so everything else is quoted relative to them.
  • Corporates — companies borrowing for factories, acquisitions, or buybacks. Riskier than Treasuries, so they must pay more (we quantify how much in the credit subsection).
  • Munis — state and local government bonds, often federally tax-exempt, which is why their quoted yields look low.
  • Agencies — government-sponsored entities such as Fannie Mae, mostly issuing bonds backed by pools of home mortgages.

Now the surprise for stock-market-centric readers: globally, bonds outstanding total roughly $140 trillion, versus roughly $110–120 trillion for all stocks. Why? Every company has exactly one class of common stock but may have dozens of bond issues alive at once; governments — the biggest borrowers on Earth — issue bonds but no equity at all (the US Treasury market alone is around $27 trillion); and mortgages, car loans, and credit-card balances get bundled into bonds too. Almost all of this trades over the counter through dealer banks rather than on an exchange like the NYSE — the plumbing from Section 6 in action.

Pricing a bond: it is just discounted cash flows

Here is the good news: you already learned the entire pricing model in Section 1. A bond is a known list of future cash flows, and the price of any known cash-flow list is the sum of its present values. No new theory required — only a discount rate.

P = Σt=1…n C ÷ (1 + y)t  +  F ÷ (1 + y)n
P = price · C = annual coupon in dollars · F = face value · y = market yield per year · n = years to maturity

The discount rate y is the yield to maturity (YTM): the going market rate for money of this maturity and riskiness. Crucially, y is set by the market every minute, while C, F, and n are frozen in the contract. Keep that asymmetry in mind — it is about to explain everything.

Worked example: a 3-year, 5% coupon bond at three different yields

Face value $1,000, so the cash flows are $50, $50, $1,050 at years 1, 2, 3.

Market yield 5% (equal to the coupon): 50÷1.05 + 50÷1.05² + 1,050÷1.05³ = 47.62 + 45.35 + 907.03 = $1,000.00. Exactly par.

Market yield 7%: 50÷1.07 + 50÷1.07² + 1,050÷1.07³ = 46.73 + 43.67 + 857.11 = $947.51. Below par.

Market yield 3%: 48.54 + 47.13 + 960.90 = $1,056.57. Above par.

Same bond, same cash flows — three prices. Only the discount rate moved.

Your edge

Bond pricing is a five-line function: loop over t, accumulate cf / (1+y)**t, done. Every readout in this section's widget — price, duration, the whole price–yield curve — falls out of that one loop. When a finance person says "we ran the bond math," they mean someone vectorized this in NumPy.

The seesaw: why price and yield move in opposite directions

This is the single most important fact in fixed income, and once you see it you cannot unsee it: when market yields rise, existing bond prices fall — and vice versa. Not sometimes. Always, mechanically, by arithmetic.

The intuition needs no formula. Suppose you own that 5% coupon bond, and new bonds of identical maturity and risk start being issued with 7% coupons because rates rose. Nobody will pay $1,000 for your $50-a-year bond when $70-a-year is on the shelf next to it. To find a buyer, your bond's price must drop until its fixed cash flows deliver the buyer a 7% return — which is precisely $947.51 for the 3-year bond above. The cash flows are bolted down by contract; price is the only part that can move. So when the required yield goes up, price is forced down, and when yields fall, old higher-coupon bonds become prized and trade above face value.

Analogy: the seesaw

Picture a playground seesaw with price on one seat and yield on the other. Push yield up, price sinks; push yield down, price rises. They are two views of the same object: yield is just the price restated as an annualized return. Saying "bonds sold off today" and "yields rose today" is the same sentence.

The relationship between coupon and market yield sorts every bond into one of three states:

One trap to defuse early: there are three different "yields" floating around, and news headlines mix them freely. The coupon rate is frozen at issue ($50 ÷ $1,000 = 5%, forever). The current yield is coupon over today's price ($50 ÷ $947.51 = 5.28%). The YTM (7% here) is the full internal rate of return, counting both the coupons and the guaranteed drift from $947.51 back up to $1,000 at maturity. YTM is the one that matters for comparing bonds; the other two are partial views.

Key idea

A bond's cash flows never change; its price does. Yield up ⇒ price down. Buy at a discount and the market yield exceeds the coupon; buy at a premium and it is the reverse. Everything else in fixed income is a footnote to this seesaw.

The yield curve: the market's forecast in one line

Take one issuer — the US Treasury — and plot the yield of its bonds against their maturities, from 3-month bills out to 30-year bonds. That plot is the yield curve, and it is arguably the most-watched chart in finance, because it compresses the market's entire forecast of growth, inflation, and central-bank policy into a single line.

Why does its shape carry information? A 10-year yield has to compete with the alternative of rolling over short-term bills for ten years. So long yields embed the market's expected path of the short rates the Fed controls (Section 4), plus a term premium — compensation for locking up money longer and eating more price risk while you wait. Three canonical shapes result:

  • Normal (upward-sloping): long yields above short. The default state — steady growth expected, term premium doing its job.
  • Flat: little difference across maturities. A transition state — the market thinks the rate cycle is near a turning point.
  • Inverted: short yields above long. The market is saying today's policy rate is unusually high and will be cut — and central banks cut aggressively mostly when the economy is heading into trouble.

That last shape has a famous track record: an inversion of the 10-year-minus-2-year (or 10-year-minus-3-month) Treasury spread has preceded every US recession of the past half-century, typically by 6 to 24 months — the pattern behind the recession indicators in Section 3. It is a strong signal, not a law of physics: the lead time is wildly variable, and the deep 2022–2024 inversion was followed by an unusually long wait, reminding everyone that "preceded every recession" is not the same as "is always followed promptly by one."

Duration and convexity: how hard does the seesaw swing?

The seesaw tells you the direction of a price move; duration tells you the size. Play with the widget above and you will notice the pattern yourself: stretch maturity from 10 to 30 years and the price curve gets dramatically steeper. Long bonds are the far end of the seesaw plank — same push, much bigger swing.

The cleanest way to see why: Macaulay duration is the weighted-average arrival time of the bond's cash flows, where each date is weighted by the present value it delivers. To a data person, that is just a center of mass. Picture the discounted cash flows as weights sitting on a plank: the small coupons barely matter, and the giant final payment drags the balance point out toward maturity. For our 10-year, 5% bond at a 7% yield the balance point sits at 7.94 years. The farther out your money's center of mass, the longer it stays exposed to compounding at the wrong rate — and the harder the price reacts.

For day-to-day use, traders quote modified duration (Macaulay ÷ (1 + y)), because it plugs straight into the most-used rule of thumb in fixed income:

ΔP ÷ P ≈ −Dmod × Δy
ΔP ÷ P = percent price change · Dmod = modified duration · Δy = change in yield (in percentage points)

Worked example: pricing a rate shock

Our 10-year, 5% bond at a 7% yield: price $859.53, modified duration 7.42 (the widget's default readout). Yields jump one percentage point to 8%. Predicted move: −7.42% × $859.53 ≈ −$63.78, so about $795.75. Exact repricing gives $798.70. The linear rule nailed the damage to within $3 — and note which way it missed: the true price fell less than predicted.

That systematic miss is convexity. The price–yield curve bows toward the origin, so the straight-line duration estimate always sits below it: for a plain bond, losses come in smaller than the linear forecast and gains come in bigger. Duration is the first derivative of price with respect to yield; convexity is the second — a Taylor expansion you have met many times, wearing a suit. It is why the rule of thumb is trustworthy for small moves (a few basis points) and only approximate for big ones.

Real world: 2022, the worst bond year in modern history

Duration stopped being an abstraction in 2022. Bonds had been issued for years at rock-bottom coupons, which makes durations long (small coupons = the plank's weight is almost all at the far end). Then the Fed hiked from near 0% to over 4% in nine months. Run the rule: broad bond-index duration around 6.5 and yields up roughly 2.5 points ⇒ ≈ −16% before coupon income. The Bloomberg US Aggregate index finished 2022 down about 13% — its worst year since records began in 1976 — and long-maturity Treasuries lost around 30%, a stock-crash-sized loss in the "safe" asset.

Silicon Valley Bank was the corporate casualty of the same arithmetic: it parked deposits in long-duration Treasuries and agency mortgage bonds, rates rose, and the mark-to-market hole grew to roughly the size of its entire equity. Depositors noticed and ran. There was no exotic credit bet involved — just unhedged duration. Section 16 dissects the risk-management failure in full.

Common pitfall

Duration measures sensitivity to a small, parallel shift in yields — it is a local linear model, not a crystal ball. For big moves you need the convexity term; for twists in the curve's shape (short yields up, long yields down) a single duration number can badly mislead.

Credit: getting paid for the risk of not getting paid

Everything so far assumed the cash flows arrive. For Treasuries that is a working assumption; for everyone else there is default risk, and the market charges for it.

The pricing of that risk starts with a credit rating — a letter grade on repayment odds from Moody's, S&P, or Fitch. The scale runs from AAA (a fortress) down through the alphabet to D (already in default). One line matters more than all others: BBB− and above is investment grade (IG); BB+ and below is high yield — universally nicknamed junk. The line is regulatory as well as reputational: many pension funds and insurers are barred from holding junk, so a downgrade across it (a "fallen angel") triggers forced selling.

The price of credit risk is the credit spread: the bond's yield minus a same-maturity Treasury yield. A BBB corporate yielding 5.5% while the 10-year Treasury pays 4% carries a 150-basis-point spread. Spreads breathe with the market's mood, which makes them a superb fear gauge: US high-yield spreads run roughly 3–4 points in calm times, blew out past 20 points in the 2008 crisis, and spiked above 10 in March 2020 before the Fed intervened. When spreads gap wider, the bond market is repricing the probability of a wave of defaults — often before equity markets fully react.

Default is also not a total loss. Bondholders stand ahead of stockholders in bankruptcy (the payoff hierarchy from Section 7), and the recovery rate on senior unsecured corporate bonds has historically averaged around 40 cents on the dollar. So a rough model of a fair spread is expected loss: default probability × (1 − recovery), plus a cushion for risk aversion and illiquidity — a one-line expected-value calculation you could code in your sleep.

Money markets: the plumbing where cash lives

Below one year to maturity, fixed income stops feeling like investing and starts feeling like infrastructure. The money market is where corporations, funds, and governments park and borrow cash by the trillions, overnight to a few months.

The instruments are refreshingly simple. T-bills pay no coupon at all: you buy at a discount and collect face value — pay $980.39 today for $1,000 in six months and you have locked in 2% over the period (about 4% annualized). Commercial paper is the corporate version — unsecured IOUs blue-chip firms roll over routinely to fund payroll and inventory.

The strangest-sounding and most important one is repo (a repurchase agreement): I sell you a Treasury today and commit to buying it back tomorrow at a slightly higher price. Squint and it is an overnight loan collateralized by the Treasury, with the price difference as interest. Trillions of dollars of repo roll every night; it is how dealers finance their inventories, and it anchors SOFR, the benchmark rate that replaced LIBOR in most floating-rate contracts. The Fed itself steers short-term rates partly through repo operations (Section 4). When people say the financial system's plumbing froze in 2008, the repo and commercial-paper markets are literally what they mean.

Finally, money-market funds are where all of this gets packaged for everyone else: mutual funds holding T-bills, repo, and commercial paper, engineered to hold a $1.00 share price so they feel like a bank account with a yield. That $1.00 is a promise, not a law.

Real world: breaking the buck

In September 2008, the Reserve Primary Fund — a money-market fund holding Lehman Brothers commercial paper — marked its shares at $0.97 when Lehman failed. "Breaking the buck" by three cents sounds trivial; it triggered a $300+ billion institutional run on money funds within days, froze the commercial-paper market that companies use to make payroll, and forced the US Treasury to guarantee money funds outright. The lesson: the most boring corner of finance is the one nobody can function without.

Deep dive: real-world bond math conventions

Everything above used annual coupons for clarity, but US convention is semiannual: a 5% coupon pays $25 every six months, and quoted YTMs are semiannual rates doubled (a "bond-equivalent yield"). The mechanics are identical — halve the coupon and rate, double the period count. Our 10-year, 5% bond at a 7% quoted yield becomes 20 payments of $25 discounted at 3.5% per half-year, giving $857.88 rather than $859.53. Same seesaw, slightly different arithmetic.

Two other conventions you will hit in practice. First, prices are quoted per $100 of face ("95.75" means 95.75% of par) and clean — excluding interest accrued since the last coupon, which the buyer pays on top (the "dirty" price is what actually changes hands). Second, day-count conventions (30/360 for corporates, actual/actual for Treasuries) decide exactly how that accrued interest is computed. None of this changes the concepts; all of it changes the fourth decimal place, which is precisely the sort of thing an analytics team gets paid to reconcile.

One elegant special case worth knowing: a zero-coupon bond (like a T-bill, or Treasury STRIPS at longer maturities) has its entire cash flow at maturity, so its Macaulay duration equals its maturity — the balance-point picture with one single weight at the far end of the plank. Zeros are therefore the purest, most rate-sensitive bonds you can hold.

Check your understanding

1. Market interest rates rise from 4% to 6%. What happens to the price of an existing fixed-coupon bond?

The cash flows cannot change, so the price must. Nobody pays par for a 4% coupon when new bonds pay 6%; the price drops until the old bond's fixed payments yield 6% from the lower purchase price.

2. A bond with a 6% coupon trades at $1,080 (above its $1,000 face). What do you know about its yield to maturity?

Premium price means coupon > market yield. Paying $1,080 for cash flows built on $1,000 also guarantees a capital loss at maturity, dragging the all-in return (YTM) below the 6% coupon rate.

3. The 2-year Treasury yields 5.0% while the 10-year yields 4.2%. Historically, what has this configuration signaled?

That is an inverted curve. Long yields sit below short ones only when the market expects short rates to be cut a lot — and deep cuts historically arrive with recessions. Inversions have preceded every US recession of the past half-century, with variable lead times.

4. A bond fund has a modified duration of 6. Yields rise by 0.5 percentage points. Approximately what happens to the fund's value?

ΔP ÷ P ≈ −D × Δy = −6 × 0.5% = −3%. Duration converts a yield move into a percent price move — and thanks to convexity the true loss would be slightly smaller than 3%.

5. High-yield credit spreads jump from 350 to 800 basis points in a month. What is the bond market saying?

The spread is the market's charge for default risk over Treasuries. Widening from 3.5 to 8 points means investors demand far more compensation to hold risky corporate debt — a classic fear signal that often precedes trouble showing up in equities.

Key takeaways

  • A bond is a tradable loan with contractually fixed cash flows: coupons plus face value at maturity. Globally, bonds outstanding exceed the value of all stocks.
  • Price = sum of discounted cash flows, exactly as in Section 1. The cash flows are frozen; the market yield — and therefore the price — is not.
  • The seesaw: yields up ⇒ prices down, always. Coupon above market yield ⇒ premium; below ⇒ discount; equal ⇒ par. YTM is the all-in return; the coupon rate never changes.
  • The yield curve plots Treasury yields across maturities and encodes the expected path of Fed policy. Inversions have preceded every US recession in half a century, with 6–24-month (and sometimes longer) lead times.
  • Duration is the center of mass of discounted cash flows and the price-sensitivity dial: ΔP ÷ P ≈ −D × Δy. Convexity makes real losses slightly smaller and gains slightly bigger than that linear estimate.
  • Low coupons and long maturities mean high duration — the arithmetic behind 2022's record bond losses and SVB's collapse (Section 16).
  • Credit spreads price default risk over Treasuries; the BBB−/BB+ line splits investment grade from junk, and widening spreads are one of the market's best fear gauges.
  • Money markets — T-bills, repo, commercial paper, money funds — are the system's cash plumbing; when they seize (2008), everything else stops.
