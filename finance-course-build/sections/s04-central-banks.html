4. Central Banks and Monetary Policy

One committee of twelve people in Washington sets a single interest rate, and within months it moves your mortgage, your savings yield, the stock market, and the value of the dollar. By the end of this section you'll understand how that machine works — and be able to read a Fed headline the way a trader does.

What a central bank is (and is not)

A central bank is a bank for banks and for the government — not for you. You can't open an account at the Federal Reserve. Its customers are commercial banks, which hold accounts there filled with reserves, the electronic cash banks use to settle payments with each other. On top of that plumbing role, a central bank issues the currency, supervises banks, acts as lender of last resort during panics, and — the part markets obsess over — conducts monetary policy: steering interest rates to keep the economy on track.

The United States' central bank is the Federal Reserve (the "Fed"), created in 1913. It's deliberately weird in structure: a seven-member Board of Governors in Washington (appointed by the President, confirmed by the Senate, with staggered 14-year terms) plus twelve regional Federal Reserve Banks (New York, Chicago, San Francisco, and so on). Interest-rate decisions are made by the FOMC (Federal Open Market Committee): the seven governors, the New York Fed president, and four of the other eleven regional presidents on a rotating basis — twelve votes in all, eight scheduled meetings a year.

Congress gave the Fed a dual mandate: stable prices and maximum employment. In practice "stable prices" means inflation around 2% per year, measured by the PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures) index. The two goals often conflict: cooling inflation usually means slowing the economy, which costs jobs. Monetary policy is the art of trading one against the other, in real time, with noisy data.

Why does everyone insist central banks be independent — free to set rates without political approval? Because politicians face elections, and cheap money feels great for about a year before the inflation bill arrives. The classic cautionary tale: in the early 1970s, Fed chair Arthur Burns kept rates low under pressure from President Nixon, helping ignite the inflation that took a decade — and a brutal recession under chair Paul Volcker, with rates near 20% — to extinguish. Countries with independent central banks reliably have lower, steadier inflation. Independence isn't decoration; it's the whole trick that makes the 2% promise believable.

The pattern is global. The European Central Bank (ECB) sets policy for the 20 euro-area countries, with a single primary mandate — price stability at 2% — and a Governing Council instead of the FOMC. The Bank of England (BoE, founded 1694, the granddaddy of them all) targets 2% inflation through its Monetary Policy Committee. The Bank of Japan (BoJ) spent three decades fighting the opposite problem — deflation — and pioneered nearly every unconventional tool in the modern kit, including quantitative easing (2001) and negative interest rates (2016), which it finally exited in 2024. Different acronyms, same machine.

Key idea

A central bank doesn't set "the" interest rate on your loans directly. It sets one overnight rate at the very base of the system, and lets arbitrage and expectations propagate it outward — like changing a root constant that every other rate in the economy is computed from.

How banks create money (yes, really)

Here's the fact that surprises almost everyone: most money is created not by the government printing it, but by commercial banks making loans. When a bank approves your $900 loan, it doesn't hand you someone else's savings from a vault. It simply credits your account with $900 — a keystroke. Your deposit is brand new money that didn't exist a moment earlier. Loans create deposits, not the other way around.

This works because of fractional-reserve banking: banks keep only a fraction of deposits on hand and put the rest to work. The textbook version chains it into a loop. Say banks hold 10% of each deposit as reserves. You deposit $1,000. Your bank keeps $100 and lends $900. The borrower spends it; the recipient deposits $900 at another bank, which keeps $90 and lends $810. That gets deposited, and $729 gets lent. Each round is 90% of the last — a geometric series, straight out of the same math as Section 1's compounding.

Total deposits = Initial deposit × 1reserve ratio = $1,000 × 10.10 = $10,000
The 1/reserve-ratio factor is the money multiplier — here 10×, so $1,000 of base money supports up to $10,000 of deposits ($9,000 newly created)

Common pitfall: the multiplier is a teaching device

The clean 1/reserve-ratio story is a useful mental model, not a literal description of 2020s banking. The US cut reserve requirements to zero in March 2020, and banks today are limited by capital rules, risk appetite, and whether profitable borrowers exist — not by a reserve count. In the real world causality mostly runs backwards: banks find creditworthy borrowers first, create the deposits, and obtain reserves afterwards. Keep the multiplier as intuition for how banking amplifies base money; don't use it to forecast.

Money creation has a dark twin: money destruction. Because a bank promises deposits back on demand but has locked the funds into long-term loans — a maturity mismatch — no bank can survive everyone asking at once. If depositors merely believe a bank might fail, they rush to withdraw, forcing it to dump assets at fire-sale prices, which causes the failure they feared. That self-fulfilling stampede is a bank run.

Two defenses exist. Deposit insurance — the FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation) guarantees up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank — removes the reason for small depositors to run at all. And the central bank stands as lender of last resort for solvent banks caught short. Neither is foolproof: in March 2023, Silicon Valley Bank's clients (mostly startups with balances far above the insured cap) tried to pull $42 billion in a single day, coordinating on Twitter and moving money by app. The mechanics of that failure — an interest-rate bet gone wrong — get a full autopsy in Section 16 (Risk Management), and the wider banking system in Section 6.

The policy rate: what the Fed actually sets

Turn on financial news and you'll hear "the Fed raised rates." The rate in question is the federal funds rate — what banks charge each other for overnight, unsecured loans of reserves. It's the shortest, safest lending rate in the economy, which makes it the base of the entire interest-rate stack. The FOMC doesn't decree a single number; it sets a target range, like 4.25%–4.50%, and then uses its tools to pin the market rate inside it. Rate moves are quoted in basis points (bp): a "25 bp hike" lifts the range by 0.25 percentage points.

How does it pin the rate? The old textbook answer was daily open market operations (OMO): buy Treasury securities to inject reserves and nudge the rate down, sell to drain reserves and nudge it up. That's still the right intuition for how reserves enter the system, but since 2008 the Fed has run an "ample reserves" regime — banks hold trillions in reserves, so tiny daily tweaks to reserve supply no longer move the price. Instead the Fed sets the rate administratively, with two floors:

First, IORB (Interest on Reserve Balances): the Fed simply pays banks interest on reserves parked at the Fed. No bank will lend overnight for less than it can earn risk-free by doing nothing — so IORB acts as a floor for banks. Second, the ON RRP (Overnight Reverse Repurchase) facility does the same job for money-market funds and other non-banks that can't earn IORB: they can always park cash at the Fed at the ON RRP rate, so they won't lend below it either. Move those two administered rates and the whole overnight market moves with them — no daily fine-tuning required. (A third backstop, the discount window, lets banks borrow directly from the Fed at a slightly higher rate, capping spikes.)

Your edge

Think of IORB as a risk-free max() bound in an optimization: no rational lender accepts less than the guaranteed alternative. The entire floor system is one arbitrage constraint, enforced by trillions of self-interested dollars — a distributed system with no central scheduler that still converges every night.

Transmission: one rate moves everything (slowly)

The fed funds rate itself applies only to overnight loans between banks. Its power comes from transmission — the chain reaction that carries it into every corner of the economy. Follow the channels:

Consumer borrowing. Banks price loans off their own funding cost. The prime rate — the benchmark for credit cards and many business loans — sits mechanically at fed funds + 3 points, so every hike passes through within days. Credit-card APRs (prime plus a fat margin) topped 21% on average during the 2023 peak. Thirty-year mortgages price off long-term Treasury yields (see Section 8) rather than the overnight rate, but expectations drag them along: when the Fed turned hawkish in 2022, mortgage rates went from below 3% to about 7% in a year. On a $400,000 loan, that's $1,633 a month at 2.75% versus $2,528 at 6.5% — roughly $322,000 more over the life of the loan. That freezes homebuying, which freezes construction, which cuts jobs.

Business investment. Companies greenlight projects when expected returns beat the cost of capital (Section 14). Raise rates and the hurdle rises; marginal factories, data centers, and startups don't get funded. Asset prices. Stocks and bonds are priced by discounting future cash flows (Section 1, Section 13); a higher discount rate mechanically lowers today's value, hitting long-duration growth stocks hardest — one reason 2022 was brutal for tech. The dollar. Higher US yields attract global capital, bidding up the dollar (Section 10): imports get cheaper (disinflationary), US exports get pricier abroad. Expectations. If firms and workers believe the Fed will keep inflation at 2%, they set prices and wages accordingly — belief does half the work.

Now the catch that makes central banking genuinely hard: the lag. Milton Friedman's famous phrase is that monetary policy works with "long and variable lags" — most estimates say 12–18 months for a hike to fully hit inflation and jobs. The Fed is steering by looking through a delayed feed: today's inflation print reflects decisions from over a year ago. Tighten until inflation visibly breaks and you've almost certainly overdone it.

Analogy

It's a thermostat wired to a radiator with a 15-minute delay. Crank the heat until the room finally feels warm and you've guaranteed a sauna. Good central banking, like good control engineering, means acting on the forecast, not the current reading — and accepting you'll sometimes be wrong in public, at scale.

QE and QT: policy by balance sheet

In December 2008 the Fed hit a wall: the fed funds rate reached zero and the economy was still collapsing. You can't meaningfully cut below zero — savers would rather hold cash — a constraint called the zero lower bound. So the Fed reached for a second lever: instead of changing the price of overnight money, change the supply and price of long-term money directly.

That lever is quantitative easing (QE): the central bank buys huge quantities of long-term government bonds and mortgage-backed securities, paying with newly created reserves. Extra demand pushes bond prices up, and — the seesaw you'll master in Section 8 — when a bond's price rises, its yield falls. Lower long-term yields mean cheaper mortgages and corporate debt even when the overnight rate is already at zero. Investors who sold their safe bonds to the Fed then reach for other assets — corporate bonds, stocks — easing conditions further. And the purchase program itself is a loud signal that rates will stay low, which shapes expectations.

The reverse gear is quantitative tightening (QT): the Fed lets bonds mature without buying replacements, so reserves quietly drain out of the system month by month. No dramatic sales — more like slowly releasing pressure from a valve, precisely because everyone remembers how violently markets react to abrupt moves (the 2013 "taper tantrum," when merely talking about slowing QE spiked yields).

The scale is worth staring at. Before 2008 the Fed held under $1 trillion in assets. Three rounds of QE took it to about $4.5 trillion by 2014. Then COVID: in 2020 the Fed bought $120 billion of bonds per month at the peak, and the balance sheet touched nearly $9 trillion in 2022 before QT began shaving it back.

Common pitfall: "QE is just printing money, so hyperinflation is coming"

QE creates bank reserves, not wallets full of cash — reserves sit inside the banking system and don't buy groceries. A decade of massive QE (2009–2019) coincided with inflation stubbornly below 2%. The 2021–2022 inflation burst had QE in the mix, but alongside pandemic supply-chain chaos and trillions in direct fiscal transfers (Section 5) — checks that, unlike reserves, really did land in spendable accounts. Be suspicious of one-variable explanations of inflation.

Deep dive: September 2019, when QT broke the plumbing

The first QT experiment ended with a bang. By September 2019 the Fed had drained reserves from about $2.8 trillion to $1.4 trillion — and discovered, live, that "ample" had quietly become "scarce." On September 17, the repo rate (overnight lending against Treasury collateral) spiked from about 2% to nearly 10% intraday as banks ran short of spare cash on a corporate-tax-payment day. The fed funds rate broke above its target range — the one thing the system is built to prevent.

The Fed responded within hours, injecting tens of billions through repo operations and soon resuming asset purchases to rebuild the reserve buffer. The lesson analysts took away: nobody, including the Fed, knows exactly where the minimum comfortable level of reserves sits — so QT is always a game of approaching an invisible cliff slowly. When you hear "reserves are getting scarce" in 2020s Fed coverage, this episode is the ghost in the room.

Reading the Fed like a market participant

Eight times a year, the FOMC meets for two days and releases a statement at 2:00 p.m. Eastern, followed by the chair's press conference at 2:30. Those ninety minutes are the closest thing markets have to a scheduled earthquake. Detailed minutes follow three weeks later, and four meetings a year (March, June, September, December) come with the Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) — including the famous dot plot, where each of the 19 Fed officials anonymously plots where they think the policy rate should sit at the end of each of the next few years. The median dot becomes the headline: "the Fed projects two cuts next year."

Fed-watchers sort officials into hawks — who lean toward higher rates because they weight the inflation mandate — and doves, who lean toward lower rates to protect employment. Handicapping the committee's hawk–dove balance, and who holds a vote this year, is a legitimate full-time job on trading desks.

Why do markets convulse over a single adjective? Because modern monetary policy works substantially through forward guidance — communication about the future path of rates. Ten-year yields, mortgage rates, and stock valuations depend on the expected path over years, not tonight's rate. So when the Fed's statement drops the word "patient," or the chair says "some time" instead of "considerable time," markets reprice the entire path within seconds. The current setting is almost old news; the path is the product. Traders read expectations straight off fed funds futures — that's what a headline like "markets price a 78% chance of a March cut" means (futures live in Section 9).

Your edge

Traders literally run a diff on the FOMC statement — news wires publish red-lined versions minutes after release, word-level changes highlighted like a pull request. With your background you could build that pipeline in an afternoon: scrape the statements, diff the token streams, timestamp the changes against tick data. That's an event study, and it's a standard quant research pattern you'll meet again in Section 18.

A practical reading list for any Fed event, in the order the market processes it: (1) the rate decision itself — usually fully priced in advance; (2) the statement diff — what changed since last time; (3) the dot plot, if it's a SEP meeting — where the median moved; (4) the press conference — where an unscripted sentence can move markets more than the decision did. If the Fed does exactly what futures predicted and says nothing new, prices barely move; surprise, not action, is what moves markets — a theme Section 17 makes precise.

Real world: one word, trillions repriced

In January 2015 the Swiss National Bank abandoned its currency cap with no warning at all; the franc moved ~20% against the euro in minutes and several brokers went insolvent. That's the extreme case of why central banks now telegraph moves — and why, when a Fed chair merely hints ("we're not even thinking about thinking about raising rates" — Jerome Powell, 2020), it is front-page financial news.

Check your understanding

1. The Fed's dual mandate consists of which two goals?

Congress assigned the Fed price stability (interpreted as ~2% PCE inflation) and maximum employment. The dollar, stocks, and specific loan rates are affected by policy but are not mandate targets.

2. When a commercial bank makes a new loan, what happens to the money supply?

Loans create deposits: the bank credits the borrower's account with new money that didn't exist before. This is how most money in the economy comes into being.

3. How does the Fed keep the federal funds rate inside its target range today?

In the modern ample-reserves "floor system," the Fed pays interest on reserves (IORB) and offers the ON RRP facility to non-banks; arbitrage keeps the market rate pinned near those administered rates. Reserve requirements are zero, and daily fine-tuning OMO is history.

4. The Fed hikes rates by a surprise 100 basis points. Which reaction is most likely, all else equal?

Higher US yields attract foreign capital (stronger dollar) and make existing lower-coupon bonds less attractive (prices fall as yields rise). The inflation effect is real but takes 12–18 months, not a month.

5. What does the dot plot show?

Each of the 19 officials anonymously marks where they think rates should be at future year-ends; the median is quoted as "the Fed's projection." It's a forecast snapshot, not a promise — and market-implied odds come from fed funds futures instead.

Key takeaways

  • A central bank is the bank for banks and the government; the Fed's FOMC sets policy under a dual mandate of ~2% inflation (PCE) and maximum employment, and its independence is what makes the inflation target credible.
  • Most money is created by commercial banks when loans create deposits; the money multiplier (1 ÷ reserve ratio) is useful intuition, but modern banks are constrained by capital and loan demand, not reserve counts.
  • Bank runs are self-fulfilling panics born of maturity mismatch; deposit insurance (FDIC, $250k) and the lender of last resort exist to prevent them — SVB 2023 showed the app-era version.
  • The federal funds rate is an overnight bank-to-bank rate steered through a floor system: IORB for banks, ON RRP for money funds, with rates quoted in basis points (25 bp = 0.25%).
  • Transmission runs through mortgages, credit cards (prime = policy + 3), business hurdle rates, asset prices, and the dollar — with a 12–18 month lag that forces the Fed to steer by forecast.
  • QE buys long-term bonds with new reserves to push long yields down when the policy rate hits zero; QT lets them run off. It is not literal money-printing into wallets, and its inflation effect is contested.
  • Markets trade the expected path of rates, not the current level — so statement wording, dot plots, and press-conference phrasing move prices more than the decision itself.
