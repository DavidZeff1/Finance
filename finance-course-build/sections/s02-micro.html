2. Microeconomics: How Markets Work

Strip away the tickers and the jargon, and every market — the corner coffee shop, the used-car lot, the New York Stock Exchange — runs on the same two forces: buyers who want things and sellers who provide them. This section hands you the supply-and-demand machinery that prices everything, including the stocks and bonds you'll meet later, and shows exactly what happens when that machinery breaks.

Scarcity, opportunity cost, and thinking at the margin

Economics starts from one blunt fact: scarcity. Wants are unlimited; time, money, land, and engineers are not. So every choice forces you to give something up, and economists insist you count that sacrifice as a real cost. The opportunity cost of anything is the value of the best alternative you didn't pick.

Worked example

You earn $120,000 a year and consider a two-year, full-time master's costing $80,000 in tuition. The sticker price is $80,000 — but the economic cost is $80,000 + (2 × $120,000) = $320,000, because you also give up two years of salary. Whether the degree is worth it depends on whether it delivers more than $320,000 of extra lifetime value, not $80,000. Most bad decisions come from ignoring the invisible $240,000.

Opportunity cost is the beating heart of finance. The discount rate you met in Section 1 (Money & the Time Value of Money) is nothing more than the opportunity cost of capital: the return your money could earn in its best alternative use. Every valuation you will ever build is an opportunity-cost calculation in a suit.

The second habit of mind is marginal thinking: rational decisions compare the benefit of one more unit against the cost of one more unit — never totals, never averages. An airline flight costs $90,000 to run whether it carries 150 or 151 passengers, so the average cost per seat is $600. But the marginal cost of passenger 151 — a snack and a little fuel — is maybe $20. Selling that last empty seat for $59 an hour before departure is pure profit, even though $59 looks absurd against a $600 average. Airlines, cloud providers, and market makers all price this way.

Marginal thinking also tells you what to ignore: sunk costs. The $2 million already spent on a failing project is gone whether you continue or quit, so it belongs in zero future decisions — a rule humans violate constantly, as you'll see in Section 17 (Behavioral Finance).

Your edge

Optimizing at the margin is gradient ascent. You don't ask "is this whole position good?" — you ask "does one more step improve the objective?" and keep stepping until marginal benefit equals marginal cost. Firms maximize profit exactly where marginal revenue = marginal cost, for the same reason your optimizer stops where the gradient hits zero.

Demand: what buyers will pay

Start on the buyer side. Each potential buyer has a willingness to pay — the maximum they'd hand over for one unit. Line every buyer up from the most eager to the least, and you get the demand curve: at any price P, it tells you how many units get bought. To a data person, a demand curve is literally every bid in the market, sorted descending — ORDER BY willingness_to_pay DESC. Stock exchanges make this visible: the bid side of an order book is a demand curve you can watch update in real time.

Demand curves slope down — cut the price and more units sell — for three reinforcing reasons. First, substitution: as coffee gets cheaper relative to tea, some tea drinkers defect. Second, the income effect: a lower price stretches every budget, so people can afford more of everything. Third, diminishing marginal utility: your fifth slice of pizza is worth less to you than your first, so you'll only buy it if it's cheap.

Common pitfall: movement vs. shift

When the good's own price changes, you slide along a fixed demand curve — economists call that a change in quantity demanded. When anything else changes — income, tastes, other prices — the entire curve moves. Mixing these up is the classic Econ 101 exam trap and, worse, a real analytical error: "price fell and people bought more" is a movement; "the product went viral and people buy more at every price" is a shift.

The curve shifts when any non-price driver changes. Income matters two ways: for a normal good, richer buyers demand more; for an inferior good, they demand less. Prices of related goods matter too: substitutes (Uber vs. Lyft) pull demand toward whichever got cheaper, while complements (printers and ink) rise and fall together. Add tastes, expectations about future prices, and the sheer number of buyers, and you have the full shifter list.

Here's the finance payoff: the demand curve for a stock shifts on expectations. A single earnings headline changes what buyers believe the future cash flows are worth, so the whole curve jumps in milliseconds — which is why prices gap instead of glide. When you hear "buyers stepped in" on financial TV, translate it as "the demand curve shifted right."

Supply: what sellers must get

Mirror image, seller side. The supply curve tells you how many units producers will offer at each price, and its backbone is marginal cost. A rational seller produces another unit only if the price covers the cost of making that unit — so the supply curve essentially is the industry's marginal-cost curve, sorted ascending.

That sorting explains why supply slopes up. In oil, the cheap wells pump first: Saudi fields might lift a barrel for $10, US shale needs $45, deep-water Arctic projects $70+. At $30 a barrel only the cheap wells run; at $80, everyone drills. Higher prices coax higher-cost producers off the bench — that's the upward slope, and it's why commodity analysts obsess over "cost curves."

Supply shifts when production conditions change: input costs (wages, energy, chips), technology (fracking shifted oil supply right for a decade), taxes and subsidies, sellers' expectations, and the number of sellers. In markets for securities, supply has its own vocabulary: a company's IPO or secondary offering adds shares to the market, an insider lockup expiry releases a wave of potential sellers, and the US Treasury shifts the bond supply curve every time it ramps up issuance to fund a deficit (see Section 5 and Section 8).

Equilibrium: where price gets discovered

Put the two curves in one diagram and they cross exactly once. That crossing is equilibrium: the one price where the quantity buyers want equals the quantity sellers offer. Economists call it the market-clearing price, and traders call finding it price discovery — it is, quite literally, the product an exchange sells.

The magic is that nobody computes it. If the price sits too high, sellers offer more than buyers take — a surplus — and inventories pile up until someone cuts price. Too low, and eager buyers outnumber goods — a shortage — and bidding pushes price up. The system self-corrects like a thermostat, with price as the feedback signal. Play with it below.

Now watch what happens when governments override the thermostat. A price ceiling set below equilibrium creates a permanent shortage: buyers want more than sellers will provide, and since price can't ration the good, something uglier does — queues, waiting lists, lotteries, favoritism, and side payments. Rent control is the textbook case: cap rents 30% below market and you get decade-long waiting lists, "key money" bribes, and landlords who stop maintaining buildings because tenants will queue up anyway. A price floor set above equilibrium does the reverse, producing surpluses — the EU's old butter mountains, or unsold labor when a wage floor binds.

Where legal trade is throttled, a black market forms to finish the job at the true equilibrium price — scalped tickets, smuggled gasoline, off-books apartments. The lesson isn't that all controls are always wrong; it's that price controls never repeal scarcity, they just change who queues and who profits.

Real world: ceilings in finance

Interest rates are prices too — the price of borrowing money. From the 1930s to the 1980s, the US capped the interest banks could pay depositors (Regulation Q). When market rates soared past the cap in the 1970s, savers did exactly what the model predicts: they exited, funneling cash into newly invented money market funds — a legal "black market" for yield that permanently reshaped American finance. Usury caps on loan rates show the flip side: cap the price of credit below what risky borrowers cost to serve, and lenders simply stop lending to them — a shortage called credit rationing.

Deep dive: surplus, deadweight loss, and the price of meddling

Economists measure a market's total win-win using two areas on the diagram. Consumer surplus is the triangle between the demand curve and the price: everyone who'd have paid more than P* pockets the difference. Producer surplus is the triangle between the price and the supply curve. With the simulator's baseline curves (D: P = 100 − 0.5Q, S: P = 20 + 0.3Q, equilibrium at Q* = 100, P* = $50), consumer surplus = ½ × (100 − 50) × 100 = $2,500 and producer surplus = ½ × (50 − 20) × 100 = $1,500. Total surplus: $4,000.

Now impose a $40 price ceiling. Sellers supply only (40 − 20) ÷ 0.3 ≈ 66.7 units, while buyers want (100 − 40) ÷ 0.5 = 120 — a shortage of about 53 units. The trades between Q = 66.7 and Q = 100 would have made both sides better off, but they never happen. Their lost value — ½ × (66.67 − 40) × (100 − 66.67) ≈ $444 — is called deadweight loss: wealth that doesn't go to buyers, sellers, or the government. It simply evaporates. Taxes create deadweight loss the same way, which is why economists judge policies by how much surplus they burn per dollar raised or redistributed.

Elasticity: how hard the market pushes back

Slope direction tells you which way quantity moves when price changes; elasticity tells you how much. The price elasticity of demand (PED) is the ratio of percentage changes:

PED = %ΔQdemanded ÷ %ΔP
|PED| > 1 → elastic (buyers flee) · |PED| < 1 → inelastic (buyers absorb) · PED is negative for normal demand; we compare absolute values

Demand is elastic when close substitutes exist, the good is a luxury, it eats a big share of the budget, or buyers have time to adapt. It's inelastic when the good is a necessity with no substitutes — insulin, a tank of gas this week, electricity tonight. Time matters enormously: gasoline demand barely budges in a month (you still have the same commute) but responds over five years (you buy a smaller car, move closer to work). Supply has an elasticity too, with time as the star: the number of apartments in a city is nearly fixed this year (inelastic) but very responsive over a decade of construction.

Elasticity has a cash consequence — the revenue test. Revenue = price × quantity, and a price hike raises one factor while shrinking the other. Which force wins depends entirely on elasticity: raise price on inelastic demand and revenue climbs; raise it on elastic demand and revenue bleeds.

Worked example: the revenue test

A café raises its latte from $5.00 to $5.50 (+10%). Daily sales drop from 200 to 170 cups (−15%). PED = −15% ÷ 10% = −1.5 → elastic. Revenue goes from 200 × $5.00 = $1,000 to 170 × $5.50 = $935. The price hike cost the café $65 a day.

Now suppose it's the only café inside a secure office campus and sales drop just 2% (200 → 196 cups). PED = −0.2 → inelastic. New revenue: 196 × $5.50 = $1,078. Same 10% hike, opposite result. (Purists compute the percentages against the midpoint of old and new values so the answer doesn't depend on direction; the verdict here is the same.)

Analogy: liquidity is elasticity in disguise

Liquidity — a word you'll hear daily in finance — is essentially the elasticity of an asset's market. In a deep, liquid market like Apple stock, a $10 million buy order barely moves the price: the order book is thick with sellers at every price, so supply to you is highly elastic. In a thin small-cap stock, the same order rips through the book and spikes the price — inelastic supply. When people say a market "dried up" in a crisis, they mean its elasticity collapsed: every trade started moving prices violently. More in Section 6 (The Financial System).

Market structures: from price takers to price makers

So far we've assumed sellers simply accept the market price. Whether they actually can depends on market power — and market power lives on a spectrum defined by how many sellers there are and how substitutable their products look.

At the far left sits perfect competition: many sellers, identical product, zero pricing power. A Kansas wheat farmer can sell unlimited bushels at the market price and exactly zero bushels a cent above it. Competition grinds price down to marginal cost and profits toward zero. The major currency pairs in FX trading come close: your euros are identical to everyone else's.

One notch right, monopolistic competition describes most of the economy you see: many sellers with slightly differentiated products — restaurants, apps, ETF providers. Branding buys a small markup, but substitutes cap it. Then comes oligopoly — a few big players whose moves visibly affect each other — and finally monopoly, one seller with no close substitute, free to restrict output and price far above cost. Regulators tolerate a natural monopoly like a water utility (duplicate pipe networks would be wasteful) but cap its prices in exchange.

Oligopoly is where strategy gets interesting, because each firm's best move depends on rivals' moves — the home turf of game theory. The canonical example is a pricing standoff:

Airline A: price HighA: 8 · B: 8A: 2 · B: 12

Check Airline A's logic. If B prices High, A earns 8 by matching or 12 by undercutting — undercut. If B prices Low, A earns 2 holding High or 4 matching Low — match. Cutting price is best no matter what B does, and B reasons identically. Both land on Low/Low earning 4 each, when High/High would have paid 8 each. That trap — nobody can improve by changing strategy alone — is a Nash equilibrium, and it's the prisoner's dilemma wearing a price tag. It explains why price wars erupt, and why a cartel — sellers colluding to hold prices High/High — is both illegal in most markets and chronically unstable: every member is tempted to secretly defect, which is why OPEC members so often bust their own quotas.

Real world: market power in finance

Finance has textbook oligopolies. Three credit rating agencies — S&P, Moody's, and Fitch — control roughly 95% of the global ratings market, a structure that mattered enormously in 2008 (see Section 17). A handful of exchanges and index providers (S&P Dow Jones, MSCI, FTSE Russell) collect tolls on trillions in assets. And when analysts praise a company's "moat," they mean durable market power — the ability to price above cost for years. That pricing power flows straight into the profit margins you'll dissect in Section 12 (Ratio Analysis).

When markets fail — and why financial regulation exists

Supply and demand is a marvel, but it earns its Nobel Prizes only under conditions: many participants, full information, and prices that capture all costs and benefits. Break those conditions and markets misfire in predictable ways. Three failures matter most — and all three run straight through finance.

Externalities. An externality is a cost or benefit that lands on bystanders. A factory that pollutes a river pays for labor and steel but not for the dead fish downstream, so pollution is overproduced — the price is a lie that omits part of the cost. Vaccines work in reverse: benefits spill onto others, so left alone we underproduce them. Finance's great negative externality is systemic risk: an over-leveraged bank that fails doesn't just hurt itself, it can topple counterparties and freeze credit for everyone — pollution, but the smoke is panic. That's the economic logic behind capital requirements, explored in Section 6 and Section 16 (Risk Management).

Public goods. A public good — national defense, clean air, financial stability itself — is one nobody can be excluded from and one person's use doesn't deplete. Since you get it whether you pay or not, everyone is tempted to be a free rider, and private markets underproduce the good. That's why stability is provisioned publicly, by central banks and regulators (Section 4).

Information asymmetry. The failure that defines finance. Information asymmetry means one side of a deal knows more than the other — and economist George Akerlof showed, with used cars, how that can destroy a market entirely. Suppose 100 cars are for sale: 50 "peaches" worth $12,000 to buyers ($10,000 to their sellers) and 50 lemons worth $4,000 to buyers ($2,000 to sellers). Buyers can't tell which is which, so they'll offer around the expected value: 0.5 × $12,000 + 0.5 × $4,000 = $8,000. But peach owners won't take $8,000 for a $10,000 car — they withdraw. Now the market is all lemons, buyers know it, and the price collapses toward $4,000. Good cars never trade at all. That death spiral is adverse selection.

Now reread that paragraph replacing "used car" with "share of stock." A company's insiders know far more about its true condition than outside investors. Without a fix, rational investors would price every stock like a possible lemon, good companies couldn't raise capital fairly, and markets would shrivel. This is precisely why the SEC exists: mandatory disclosure — audited financial statements (Section 11), quarterly filings, insider-trading bans — is anti-lemon technology. So are credit ratings, warranties, and bank due diligence. Regulation here isn't the opposite of free markets; it's the plumbing that keeps them from unraveling.

One sibling deserves its own name: moral hazard is hidden action rather than hidden quality — behaving more recklessly because someone else bears the downside. Deposit insurance stops bank runs (good) but can tempt banks to gamble with insured money (the hazard), which is why insurance comes bundled with supervision. Adverse selection is about who shows up to the deal; moral hazard is about what they do after signing. Keep the pair handy — they explain half of financial regulation, and a good share of the crises in Section 17.

Key idea

Markets fail exactly where prices stop telling the truth — about costs (externalities), about who pays (public goods), or about quality (asymmetric information). Financial regulation, at its best, is truth-maintenance for prices.

Check your understanding

1. You leave a $120,000 job for a two-year, full-time master's with $80,000 total tuition. Ignoring living costs, the economic cost of the degree is…

Opportunity cost includes the best alternative you give up — here, $240,000 of salary on top of $80,000 tuition. Future raises determine whether the degree is worth $320,000; they don't reduce what it costs.

2. Laptop prices fall and shoppers buy more laptops. On a supply-and-demand diagram, this is…

The good's own price changed, so you slide along a fixed curve — quantity demanded rises. The curve itself only shifts when a non-price factor (income, tastes, other goods' prices, expectations) changes.

3. A utility estimates the price elasticity of demand for electricity at −0.4. If it raises prices 10%, its revenue will…

With inelastic demand (|PED| < 1), the percentage drop in quantity is smaller than the percentage rise in price, so revenue = P × Q increases. That's the revenue test.

4. A city caps rents 30% below the market-clearing level. Standard supply-and-demand analysis predicts…

Below the equilibrium price, quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied — a shortage. Since price can't ration the good, queues, key money, and black-market workarounds do, and landlords have little incentive to maintain units.

5. In Akerlof's used-car market, buyers can't observe quality, so they offer only an average price. The predictable result is…

Owners of above-average cars refuse the average price and withdraw, which lowers average quality, which lowers the price buyers will offer — adverse selection. Disclosure rules, audits, and warranties exist to break this loop.

Key takeaways

  • Scarcity makes every choice a trade-off; the true cost of anything is its opportunity cost — the best alternative foregone. The discount rate is finance's version of this idea.
  • Decide at the margin: compare the benefit and cost of one more unit, ignore sunk costs, and stop where marginal benefit equals marginal cost.
  • Demand slopes down (substitution, income effects, diminishing marginal utility); supply slopes up (rising marginal cost). Own-price changes move you along a curve; everything else shifts it.
  • Equilibrium is a feedback loop: surpluses push prices down, shortages push them up. Price controls don't repeal scarcity — they create queues, quality decay, and black markets.
  • Elasticity measures how hard quantity responds to price. Raise prices on inelastic demand and revenue rises; on elastic demand it falls. Market liquidity is elasticity in disguise.
  • Market power runs on a spectrum from perfect competition (price = marginal cost) to monopoly (price far above it); oligopolists play games, and the prisoner's dilemma explains price wars and cheating cartels.
  • Markets fail on externalities, public goods, and information asymmetry. Adverse selection and moral hazard are the two demons of finance — and disclosure rules, ratings, audits, and capital requirements are the exorcists.
