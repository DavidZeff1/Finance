The role landscape, up close

Finance uses the word "quant" the way tech uses "engineer" — as a blurry umbrella over jobs that share a toolkit but differ wildly in what they do all day. Before you apply to anything, learn the map, because the titles are not interchangeable and one of them is a much better landing spot for a CS-and-data background than the others.

Financial data and its horror list

Data is the raw material of every role above, and financial data has textures a general-purpose data scientist has never touched. Learn the taxonomy first, then learn the ways it will silently ruin your models.

The first family is market data — the price side. Its coarsest form is OHLCV bars (open, high, low, close, volume), one row per day per ticker. Zoom in and you get tick data — every single trade and quote, microsecond-stamped, gigabytes per day. Zoom in further and you reach the order book, the live ledger of every resting buy and sell order at each price. The second family is fundamental data — the numbers from the financial statements. The third is macro data — GDP, inflation, rates. The fourth, and the frontier, is alternative data: satellite images of parking lots, aggregated card spending, app-download counts — anything that hints at reality before the official numbers land.

Where do you get it? The professional world runs on three expensive vendors: Bloomberg (its terminal is a rite of passage), Refinitiv (formerly Reuters), and FactSet. A terminal costs on the order of $30,000 per user per year, which tells you how valuable clean, timely data is. But you can build a serious portfolio for free: yfinance for daily equity prices, FRED for macro series, and EDGAR for the raw SEC filings behind fundamental data. The free stack is genuinely enough to learn on.

Now the part that separates the careful from the doomed. Financial data carries booby traps that inflate backtest results and then detonate in production. Memorize this list; interviewers ask about it precisely because it weeds people out.

Common pitfall: the four data-quality horrors

Survivorship bias. Your dataset only contains companies that survived. The 400 stocks that went bankrupt or delisted are gone, so any strategy tested on today's index constituents looks brilliant — you accidentally only bet on winners you couldn't have known in advance.

Look-ahead bias. Using information that wasn't available yet at decision time — a quarter's earnings on the quarter-end date, when they were actually reported six weeks later. This is the finance name for what you'd call data leakage.

Corporate-action adjustments. Splits, dividends, and mergers make raw prices discontinuous. A 2-for-1 split halves the price overnight with no loss to holders; if you don't use adjusted prices you'll record a fake −50% return.

Point-in-time data. Databases get restated — a company revises last quarter's revenue, and naive vendors overwrite the old number. A point-in-time dataset preserves what was known on each date, so your backtest sees only what a trader could have seen.

Your edge: this is leakage, renamed

You already fight three of these four every time you build a model. Look-ahead bias is train-test leakage. Survivorship bias is a biased sampling frame. Point-in-time discipline is just "compute features from data as of the prediction timestamp." Finance simply has richer, sneakier versions — but your instinct to ask "could this feature actually have been known then?" is exactly the right reflex, and most finance grads never developed it.

Returns math for data people

Here is a rule that will feel wrong until it clicks: you almost never model prices. You model returns. Prices are non-stationary — Apple at $5 and Apple at $200 are different worlds with different variances, and every time-series and ML tool you own assumes some stability. Returns, the percentage changes, are far closer to stationary, so they're what you feed your models.

There are two ways to compute a return, and knowing when to use each is a genuine competence signal. The simple return is the everyday percentage change. The log return is the natural log of the price ratio.

Rsimple = (Pt − Pt−1) ÷ Pt−1     rlog = ln(Pt ÷ Pt−1) = ln(1 + Rsimple)
Pt = price today · Pt−1 = price yesterday · ln = natural logarithm

Why keep both? Log returns add across time; simple returns add across assets. A stock that goes +10% then −10% in simple terms is not flat — it's at 0.99, a −1% loss — but the log returns +0.0953 and −0.1054 sum cleanly to −0.0101, matching ln(0.99). That additivity makes log returns the natural currency for compounding a single series over time. Simple returns, meanwhile, aggregate correctly across a portfolio: a portfolio's simple return is the weighted average of its holdings' simple returns, which is not true for logs. Use logs down the time axis, simples across the holdings axis.

For small daily moves the two are nearly identical — ln(1.001) ≈ 0.0009995 — which is why people get sloppy. But the gap explodes for large moves, and that's exactly when it matters.

Two more essentials. First, annualization. There are about 252 trading days in a US year, so a daily mean return scales by ×252, but daily volatility scales by ×√252 ≈ 15.87, because variance adds with time and standard deviation is its square root. A daily standard deviation of 1% is therefore about 15.9% annualized — the √-of-time rule you'll type a thousand times.

Worked example: annualizing

Suppose SPY's daily log returns have mean 0.0003 and standard deviation 0.011. Annualized return ≈ 0.0003 × 252 = 0.0756, so about 7.6%. Annualized volatility ≈ 0.011 × √252 = 0.011 × 15.87 = 0.1746, about 17.5%. A crude annualized Sharpe-style ratio (ignoring the risk-free rate) is 0.0756 ÷ 0.1746 ≈ 0.43 — realistic for a broad equity index.

Second, returns have famous stylized facts — regularities so universal they show up in every market. Volatility clusters: turbulent days bunch together, so today's volatility predicts tomorrow's even though the return's sign doesn't. Returns have fat tails: crashes and rallies are far more frequent than a normal distribution says, which is why a "6-sigma" day happens every few years, not every million. And returns themselves show near-zero autocorrelation — you cannot predict tomorrow's direction from yesterday's, even though you can predict its magnitude. That combination is the signature of a market that's hard, but not impossible, to beat.

Backtesting done honestly

A backtest answers "how would this strategy have done?" — and it is the most dangerous tool in finance, because it is trivially easy to build one that lies to you. The whole craft is a discipline for not lying to yourself. The honest workflow runs in one direction and never skips a step.

Two steps deserve extra fear. Step 5, transaction costs and slippage, kills more strategies than any other. A signal that trades daily and looks like a goldmine at zero cost can be a slow bleed once you subtract even 0.1% per trade — high-turnover strategies are especially fragile. And step 6 guards against the cardinal sin: overfitting. This is the exact enemy you already know from ML — a model that memorizes the training set. The defense is identical: a strict train/test split, where the out-of-sample period is quarantined and touched exactly once.

Common pitfall: the multiple-testing problem

Here's the subtler trap. Try 100 unrelated strategies on the same data at a 5% significance level, and by pure luck roughly 5 will look "significant" — the finance version of p-hacking. If you test enough moving-average combinations, one will have crushed history for no reason but noise. This is why a real research process demands an economic reason the signal should work before the backtest, and penalizes the number of things you tried. A backtest is a hypothesis test, and running a thousand of them silently destroys your p-values.

Machine learning in finance, honestly

You arrived with ML skills, so here is the blunt truth about where they pay off in finance and where they'll break your heart. The dividing line is signal-to-noise ratio. ML is spectacular when the signal is strong and abundant, and nearly useless when the noise drowns everything — and financial problems span that entire range.

Where ML genuinely works, it works because the problem is really a classification task with rich, labeled, high-signal data. Fraud detection is a near-ideal ML problem — millions of transactions, clear labels, strong patterns. Credit scoring predicts default from applicant features, a decades-old ML staple. Execution optimization — slicing a big order to minimize slippage — has fast feedback and measurable outcomes. And NLP on filings and news extracts structured signals (sentiment, topics, risk-factor changes) from the mountain of text in 10-Ks and headlines. These are the roles hiring your profile right now.

Where ML struggles is the seductive one: point forecasts of prices. Because returns have near-zero autocorrelation and fat tails, the predictable component is a whisper under a roar of noise. A model that gets 51% directional accuracy can be genuinely valuable at scale — but that is a different game from the 95% accuracy you're used to on clean datasets, and beginners routinely mistake an overfit backtest for the former. Respect the noise floor.

Common pitfall: finance-specific feature leakage

Leakage in finance wears clever disguises. Using the close to predict the same day's return — the close is part of the return. Normalizing features using the full dataset's mean and standard deviation before splitting — your scaler peeked at the future. Labeling with a forward return but computing a feature that spans the label window. Filling missing prices by interpolation, which quietly injects future values into the past. Each one turns a mediocre model into a fake genius that dies in production.

Your edge: cross-validation, but time-aware

Your instinct to k-fold cross-validate is right — but plain k-fold shuffles the future into the training set and leaks. Finance uses walk-forward validation: train on 2015–2018, test on 2019, roll forward, repeat. Add a purge/embargo gap so the boundary can't leak. It's the same discipline you know, with the arrow of time enforced — and mentioning it in an interview marks you as someone who's done real financial ML.

The starter stack and a project ladder

You already know most of this toolkit; here it is with the financial dialect attached. pandas is your workhorse — its DatetimeIndex, resample, and pct_change were practically built for price series. numpy handles the vectorized return math. matplotlib draws the equity curves. statsmodels gives you the econometric tools finance loves — OLS with proper standard errors, ARIMA, stationarity tests like the augmented Dickey-Fuller. And scikit-learn covers the classification and regression you already do, as long as you swap its default k-fold for time-aware splits. That's the whole starter stack, and it's free.

Knowledge without a portfolio is invisible to recruiters, so climb this ladder. Each rung adds exactly one hard idea, and by the top you have an artifact that proves you understand honest backtesting — the thing most applicants fake.

Deep dive: why 252, and does the day-count matter?

The 252 comes from roughly 365 calendar days minus about 104 weekend days minus around 9 US market holidays. Other conventions exist — some fixed-income math uses 360, some risk systems use 260 — and the exact figure barely moves annualized returns but does nudge annualized volatility, since it enters as √252 versus √260. The rule: pick one convention, apply it consistently, and document it. Mismatched day-counts are a classic "why don't my numbers tie out?" bug when you join two vendors — a debugging skill you already have from reconciling datasets.

Check your understanding

1. You backtest a stock strategy using only the companies currently in the S&P 500. Which bias does this most directly introduce?

Today's index excludes every company that went bankrupt or was delisted, so you only test on survivors you couldn't have identified in advance — survivorship bias, which systematically inflates results.

2. A daily return series has a standard deviation of 1.2%. Roughly what is the annualized volatility?

Volatility scales by the square root of time: 1.2% × √252 ≈ 1.2% × 15.87 ≈ 19%. (Returns scale by ×252, but volatility by ×√252 because variance adds with time.)

3. Why do quant researchers insist on an economic reason for a signal before running the backtest?

Run 100 tests at 5% significance and about 5 pass by chance alone. Demanding a hypothesis first — and penalizing how many things you tried — is the defense against p-hacking your own backtest.

4. Which financial ML task has the WORST signal-to-noise ratio and is hardest to do well?

Returns are nearly unforecastable (near-zero autocorrelation, fat tails), so directional price prediction lives at the bottom of the ladder — a good model barely clears 51%. The others have far stronger, more learnable signal.

5. A stock rises +25% then falls −20%. What is the correct total simple return, and how do the log returns behave?

1.25 × 0.80 = 1.00, a 0% total simple return — you cannot add simple returns across time. Log returns can be added: ln(1.25) = +0.2231 and ln(0.80) = −0.2231 sum to 0, matching ln(1.00) = 0.

Key takeaways

  • The five quant-adjacent roles form a gradient: data scientist and financial data analyst are your fastest doors in, quant developer and risk quant reward your engineering, and quant researcher is a destination, not an entry point.
  • Financial data comes in market, fundamental, macro, and alternative flavors — and carries booby traps (survivorship, look-ahead, corporate actions, point-in-time) that are just leakage and sampling bias wearing finance costumes.
  • Model returns, not prices: prices are non-stationary, returns aren't. Use log returns down the time axis (they add), simple returns across holdings (they aggregate). Annualize returns by ×252 and volatility by ×√252.
  • Stylized facts are universal: volatility clusters, tails are fat, and direction has near-zero autocorrelation — you can forecast magnitude better than sign.
  • Honest backtesting is a one-way pipeline ending in a single out-of-sample test; the cardinal sins are overfitting, ignoring transaction costs, and the multiple-testing problem that turns 100 tries into 5 fake winners.
  • ML works best where signal is strong and labeled — fraud, credit, execution, document NLP — and worst on point price forecasts, where noise dominates. Guard against finance-specific leakage with walk-forward validation and a purge gap.
  • Climb the five-project ladder on the free pandas/numpy/statsmodels/scikit-learn stack; the honest out-of-sample report at the top is the artifact that proves you won't fool yourself — and hiring managers know it.
