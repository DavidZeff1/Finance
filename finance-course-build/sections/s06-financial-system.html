6. The Financial System: A Guided Tour

Before you study stocks, bonds, or derivatives, you need the map: who the players are, what each one does, and what actually happens in the seconds after you tap Buy. This section is your orientation tour — by the end you will be able to place any headline, job posting, or trade confirmation on the map, and you will have run your own orders through a live order book.

The core job: routing money from savers to users

Strip away the glass towers and the jargon, and the financial system does one job: it moves money from people who have more than they currently need (savers) to people who can put it to productive use (users of capital — firms building factories, governments building roads, households buying homes). In exchange, the users hand back a security or a promise: a share, a bond, a loan agreement — a claim on future cash.

There are exactly two routes the money can take. In direct finance, you buy the claim yourself in a market: you purchase Apple shares or a Treasury bond, and your money flows (roughly) straight to the firm or government. In indirect finance, you hand your money to an intermediary — a bank, a pension fund, an insurer — and it decides who gets funded. Your checking account is indirect finance: the bank pools your deposit with millions of others and lends the pool out as mortgages and business loans.

Why do intermediaries exist at all, when a market could match savers and borrowers directly? Three reasons, all of which should ring a bell from Section 2 (Microeconomics):

Information. Lending suffers from information asymmetry: the borrower knows far more about their repayment odds than you do. A bank employs credit analysts, sees the borrower's cash flows, and screens thousands of applications — it amortizes the cost of due diligence over a huge loan book, the way one trained ML model serves millions of queries.

Maturity transformation. Savers want their money back on demand; borrowers want it for 30 years. A bank performs maturity transformation — it borrows short (your deposit, withdrawable today) and lends long (a 30-year mortgage), betting that not everyone withdraws at once. This is enormously useful and inherently fragile: when everyone does withdraw at once, you get a bank run — the failure mode behind much of Section 17 (Crises).

Diversification. With $1,000 you can fund a slice of one borrower; a fund with $10 billion holds thousands of loans or stocks, so one default barely dents it. Pooling gives small savers diversification they could never build alone — the statistics of this are the heart of Section 15 (Portfolio Theory).

Analogy

Think of the financial system as the internet's routing layer for money. Savers are clients, users of capital are servers, markets are peer-to-peer connections, and intermediaries are load balancers: they pool traffic, hide the messy topology, and add redundancy — at the cost of a fee and a new single point of failure.

Real world: scale

BlackRock alone manages over $11 trillion — more than the GDP of every country except the US and China. When you hear “institutional investors,” picture a handful of asset managers, pension funds, and sovereign funds that collectively own most of the stock market. Retail investors — individuals like you — are the minority of volume in most markets.

The referees: who regulates what

In Section 2 you met market failures: information asymmetry, externalities, monopoly power. Financial regulation is the institutional response to exactly those failures. Securities rules force disclosure (fixing information asymmetry); bank capital rules limit systemic risk (a negative externality — a bank's collapse hurts people who never banked there); antitrust and market-structure rules police concentrated venues. Each US regulator owns one slice:

Notice the pattern: no single regulator sees everything. A bank holding company might answer to the Fed for its balance sheet, the SEC for its brokerage arm, the CFTC for its swaps desk, and the FDIC for its deposits. This fragmentation is a recurring plot device in crisis post-mortems — risky activity migrates to whichever corner is watched least, a phenomenon called regulatory arbitrage.

Buy side vs sell side: the industry's two halves

Practitioners split the industry into two camps, and the split shows up in every job posting you will ever read. The buy side manages money: asset managers, hedge funds, pension funds, PE/VC, insurers, sovereign funds. Its product is investment performance, and it earns fees on the assets it runs. The sell side sells services to the money: investment banks, brokers, market makers, and research desks. Its products are execution, new securities, advice, and analysis, and it earns fees, commissions, and spreads.

The economics differ sharply. Buy-side revenue scales with assets under management — a fund charging 0.5% on $50 billion earns $250 million a year whether markets are open or not. Sell-side revenue scales with activity: deals closed, trades executed, spreads captured. That is why sell-side hiring booms with deal cycles while big asset managers grind on steadily.

Career-wise: the sell side hires analysts into investment banking (building deal models, brutal hours), sales & trading (pricing and executing), and research (writing on companies — the skills of Sections 12–13). The buy side hires portfolio managers, research analysts, and — in force since the 2010s — quantitative researchers and data scientists who build signals from data (Section 18). Sell-side experience is a classic on-ramp to buy-side seats; Section 20 (Career Roadmap) maps the paths in detail.

Your edge

Data roles now live on both sides: market makers and hedge funds run some of the most demanding ML and low-latency engineering anywhere, while banks and asset managers hire heavily for risk analytics, surveillance, and client data. When you read a posting, first ask: buy side or sell side? That single bit tells you the business model, the pace, and how your work gets monetized.

Primary vs secondary markets: issuance and trading

Every security lives two lives. It is born in the primary market, where the issuer sells it and receives the cash. It then spends decades in the secondary market, changing hands between investors while the issuer gets nothing further. When Apple stock trades $10 billion in a day, Apple's bank account doesn't move a cent.

The flagship primary-market event is the IPO. A private company hires investment banks to run underwriting: the banks value the company, write the prospectus (a legal disclosure document filed with the SEC — readable free at sec.gov/edgar), drum up demand from buy-side institutions in a roadshow, set the price, and buy the shares from the company to resell to investors. Bond issuance works the same way but faster and more routine — large firms tap the bond market the way you'd call an API, sometimes raising billions in an afternoon (Section 8).

Worked example: IPO economics

A company sells 10 million new shares at $20: gross proceeds $200 million. The underwriters' fee (the gross spread) is a typical 7%, or $14 million, so the company nets $186 million. If the stock closes its first day at $26, that 30% “pop” means the company arguably left 10M × $6 = $60 million on the table — money captured by the investors who got IPO allocations instead of by the firm. Underpricing is one of the best-documented puzzles in finance.

Why does the secondary market matter so much if issuers get nothing from it? Because liquidity in the secondary market is what makes the primary market possible. You'll only buy a 30-year bond at issue if you know you can sell it Tuesday. Secondary prices also do the economy's homework: they continuously re-value every firm, which tells capital where to flow next — the price-signal machinery from Section 2 operating at millisecond resolution. How stocks and their exchanges work in detail is Section 7.

Common pitfall

“I bought shares, so I gave the company money.” Only in the primary market. Buying AAPL on the secondary market sends your money to whoever sold you the shares — the company was paid once, at issuance, possibly decades ago.

Market microstructure: inside the order book

Now zoom all the way in, to the data structure at the center of every modern market: the order book. It is literally two priority queues. On one side sit bids — resting offers to buy, sorted highest-first. On the other sit asks — resting offers to sell, sorted lowest-first. The gap between the best bid and best ask is the bid–ask spread, and the total size resting at each price level is the book's depth.

You interact with the book through two order types. A market order says “fill me now at whatever prices are available” — it consumes resting orders starting from the best price. A limit order says “fill me only at this price or better” — if nobody's willing, it joins the queue and waits. Matching follows price-time priority: best price wins, and among equal prices, first-come-first-served — a stable sort you could implement in an afternoon, running at nanosecond scale.

Worked example: walking the book

Using Figure 6.3's book, send a market buy for 1,000 shares. You lift 200 @ $100.05, then 400 @ $100.10, then 300 @ $100.15, then 100 @ $100.20. Average fill = (200×100.05 + 400×100.10 + 300×100.15 + 100×100.20) ÷ 1,000 = $100,115 ÷ 1,000 = $100.115 — 6.5¢ per share worse than the best ask, a $65 cost known as slippage. Your own order moved the market: that's price impact, and it's why large investors slice big orders into thousands of small ones.

Who keeps the queues populated? Market makers. They post bids and asks simultaneously, hoping to buy at $99.95 and sell at $100.05 all day — earning the spread as compensation for the risk that prices lurch while they're holding inventory. Most are now HFT firms whose edge is speed: co-located servers, microwave links between cities, FPGA-parsed market data. The good: spreads on liquid US stocks fell from ~12 cents in the 1990s to ~1 cent today. The debated: whether the speed race spends real resources winning races that don't help anyone — Section 17 revisits this, including the 2010 Flash Crash.

Key idea

The spread is the price of immediacy. Market orders pay it; limit orders earn it (in exchange for execution risk — the market may run away without filling you). Every execution strategy in existence is a tradeoff along this line.

A trade, end to end

You tap Buy on your phone. What actually happens? More than you'd think, across three very different timescales.

Milliseconds — routing and execution. Your broker's systems validate the order and route it to an execution venue: a public exchange, or (for most retail orders) a wholesale market maker who pays the broker for the privilege. The venue's matching engine crosses your order against the book, and you get a confirmation before your thumb leaves the screen. Economically, the shares are yours from this instant.

Hours — clearing. Overnight, a clearing house (in US stocks, the NSCC) performs novation: it inserts itself as buyer to every seller and seller to every buyer, so nobody needs to trust a stranger. It also does netting — a firm that bought 5 million shares and sold 4.9 million settles only the net 100,000. Netting routinely shrinks settlement obligations by ~98%, a compression trick any data engineer will appreciate.

One day — settlement and custody. On T+1 (trade date plus one business day, the US standard since May 2024), settlement happens: cash and shares swap at the Depository Trust Company, the central vault where nearly all US securities live as electronic entries. Your broker's custodian records you as beneficial owner. The whole apparatus exists to crush counterparty risk — the risk that the stranger on the other side simply doesn't pay.

Real world: January 2021

When meme-stock volumes exploded, the clearing house demanded billions more in collateral (margin) from brokers to cover the two-day settlement gap that then existed. Several brokers restricted buying in GME because they literally couldn't post the deposit fast enough. The invisible plumbing became front-page news — and the episode accelerated the US move from T+2 to T+1.

Deep dive: where your “free” retail order really goes

Most US retail brokers charge zero commission because of payment for order flow (PFOF): wholesale market makers like Citadel Securities pay the broker a fraction of a cent per share to execute your order internally rather than send it to an exchange. Why pay for it? Retail orders are “uninformed” in the statistical sense — a random retail buy predicts nothing about the next price move, so making markets against retail is safer than quoting on an exchange where sharper counterparties lurk. Wholesalers typically fill retail slightly inside the public quote (price improvement of a fraction of a cent), and brokers must report execution quality. Critics argue the improvement is measured against a quote the wholesalers themselves help set, and that the conflict — your broker is paid by your counterparty — deserves more scrutiny; the EU banned PFOF outright from 2026. Either way, “free” means the spread, not the commission, is where the money moves.

Zoom back out. You now hold the whole map: two channels (markets and intermediaries), a cast of institutions on the buy and sell sides, referees for each arena, a primary market where securities are born, a secondary market where an order book prices them tick by tick, and a clearing-and-settlement pipeline that makes strangers safe to trade with. Check your understanding

1. A bank funds 30-year mortgages using deposits customers can withdraw at any time. This is called…

Borrowing short (deposits) to lend long (mortgages) is maturity transformation — a core service of banks, and the reason bank runs are possible when everyone demands their short-term money at once.

2. A hedge fund analyst and an investment-bank M&A analyst are, respectively…

Hedge funds manage money (buy side); investment banks sell services — deals, underwriting, execution — to the money (sell side).

3. The book shows asks of 200 shares at $100.05 and 400 at $100.10. A market buy for 600 shares fills at an average of…

(200 × 100.05 + 400 × 100.10) ÷ 600 = 60,050 ÷ 600 = $100.0833. It's a size-weighted average, not a simple midpoint — the extra 3.3¢ over the best ask is slippage.

4. You buy 100 shares of AAPL through your broker today. Who receives your money?

Secondary-market trades move money between investors. Apple was paid once — in the primary market, when the shares were originally issued.

5. What does a clearing house (CCP) fundamentally do?

Through novation the CCP guarantees both legs of every trade, and through netting it shrinks what actually has to move at settlement. Central custody is the depository's job (the DTC), not the clearing house's.

Key takeaways

  • The financial system routes money from savers to users of capital via two channels: direct finance (markets) and indirect finance (intermediaries such as banks and funds).
  • Intermediaries exist to solve information asymmetry, perform maturity transformation, and provide diversification — services no individual saver can replicate alone.
  • Buy side manages money and earns fees on assets; sell side sells execution, research, and new issues, and earns on activity. Every finance job sits on one side or the other.
  • Regulators map to market failures: the SEC forces disclosure, the Fed and FDIC contain systemic risk and bank runs, FINRA polices brokers, the CFTC covers derivatives.
  • Issuers get paid only in the primary market (IPOs, bond issues, via underwriters); the secondary market prices those claims continuously and gives them liquidity.
  • The order book is two price-time priority queues; the bid–ask spread is the price of immediacy, and big market orders walk the book, paying slippage.
  • A trade executes in milliseconds but settles at T+1: clearing houses net trades and guarantee both sides, then cash and securities swap at the depository, with custodians keeping the records.
