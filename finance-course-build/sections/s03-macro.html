3. Macroeconomics: The Big Picture

Microeconomics zoomed in on single markets; macroeconomics zooms out to the whole machine — total output, the price level, jobs, and the boom-bust rhythm underneath them. By the end of this section you'll be able to read a GDP release, a CPI print, or a jobs report the way an analyst does: knowing exactly what each number measures, and where it can mislead you.

GDP: the economy's top line

Macroeconomics asks three big questions: how much are we producing, what do things cost, and who has a job? Where Section 2 (Microeconomics) studied one market's supply and demand, macro adds everything up. The master number is GDP — the market value of everything a country produces in a quarter or a year.

Two words in that definition do heavy lifting. Final means we count the loaf of bread but not the flour that went into it — otherwise we'd double-count the wheat, the flour, and the bread. Final goods are the end products. And inside a country means geography, not nationality: a German-owned BMW plant in South Carolina counts in US GDP.

Statisticians measure GDP by adding up who spends the money — the expenditure approach:

GDP = C + I + G + NX
C = consumption · I = investment · G = government purchases · NX = net exports (exports − imports)

For the US (roughly $29 trillion in 2024) the proportions are surprisingly stable. Consumption is about 68% — the American economy is mostly households buying things. Investment (~18%) means new factories, equipment, software, and homes — not buying stocks; in GDP-speak, trading existing assets creates nothing new. Government purchases (~17%) cover roads, teachers, and defense — but not Social Security checks, which merely transfer money to households (it gets counted when they spend it, in C). Net exports run about −3% for the US: a persistent trade deficit, which we'll unpack in Section 5 (Fiscal Policy, Debt & Trade).

One trap will follow you everywhere in macro data: prices change. If GDP rises 6% but everything just got 6% more expensive, nothing real happened. Nominal GDP uses current prices; real GDP strips inflation out using a price index called the GDP deflator. Analysts almost always mean real growth when they say "the economy grew."

Worked example

Nominal GDP rises from $25.0T to $26.5T: +6.0%. The deflator says prices rose 4.0%. Real growth = 1.06 ÷ 1.04 − 1 ≈ 1.9% — close to the quick shortcut of 6% − 4% = 2%. A headline that looked like a boom was actually a sluggish year plus inflation.

Size also isn't standard of living. China's GDP (~$18T) dwarfs Switzerland's (~$0.9T), but divide by population and Switzerland produces about $100,000 per person versus China's ~$13,000. GDP per capita is the number to watch for living standards; total GDP is the number to watch for geopolitical and market weight.

Analogy

GDP is a speedometer, not a health checkup. It tells you how fast the machine is running — nothing about who's in the car or what's happening to the engine. It ignores unpaid work (a parent's childcare counts for zero; the same care purchased from a daycare counts fully), leisure, environmental damage, the informal cash economy, and distribution — GDP per capita can rise while the median household stagnates. Wikipedia adds enormous value and roughly $0 to GDP. Useful gauge; incomplete one.

Growth: why 1% is a big deal

Growth rates look boring — 1.5% versus 3% barely moves a chart for a year or two. But you already know from Section 1 what happens when a rate compounds: the gap goes from rounding error to canyon. Growth is the FV = PV × (1 + r)n machine applied to an entire country.

Run the numbers. Two countries both start at $10,000 of GDP per person. Country A grows 1.5% a year, Country B grows 3%. After 60 years — one working lifetime — A reaches about $24,400 and B about $58,900. Same starting point, and B's citizens end up 2.4 times richer, from a difference you'd barely notice in any single year. The Rule of 72 from Section 1 says it directly: at 1.5%, living standards double every ~48 years; at 3%, every ~24.

Real world: Korea vs. Ghana

In 1960, South Korea and Ghana had roughly similar income per person — both poor, both recently emerged from colonization or war. Korea then compounded at some of the fastest rates ever recorded for three decades. Today Korean income per person is roughly seven times Ghana's. No single year did that; the exponent did.

So what sets the growth rate? In the short run, lots of things — we'll get to cycles below. But in the long run the answer is almost embarrassingly simple: productivity — output per hour worked. A country gets richer per person only if each hour of work produces more stuff. As economist Paul Krugman put it, "productivity isn't everything, but in the long run it is almost everything."

Productivity grows through better machines and infrastructure (capital), better recipes (technology — from crop rotation to spreadsheets to LLMs), better-trained people (human capital), and institutions that make investing worthwhile — courts, property rights, competition. This is why economists get worked up about policies with "small" effects on growth: a policy that permanently adds 0.2 percentage points to productivity growth is worth more, compounded over 50 years, than almost any one-off program you could name.

Inflation: how the CPI is built, and why 2% is the target

Inflation is a sustained rise in the overall price level — which is the same thing as a fall in what each dollar buys. It's measured as the rate of change of a price index, and the most famous index is the CPI (Consumer Price Index).

The construction is beautifully simple in concept. The Bureau of Labor Statistics defines a basket of what a typical urban household buys, sends people out to collect roughly 80,000 prices every month, and averages the price changes using weights from surveys of actual household budgets. Shelter gets about a third of the weight; food about 13%; energy about 7%. Change in the weighted average = inflation.

Your edge

CPI is literally a weighted average — a dot product of budget-share weights and price changes. Two consequences fall out of the math. First, your personal inflation rate differs from the headline whenever your budget weights differ (renters in hot cities live a higher inflation rate than the index). Second, big-weight items dominate: a 5% move in shelter moves the index more than a 20% move in gasoline.

Worked example: a mini-CPI

Say the basket is 35% rent, 15% food, 5% gasoline, 45% everything else. This year rent rises 5%, food 2%, gas 20%, everything else 1%. Inflation = 0.35×5 + 0.15×2 + 0.05×20 + 0.45×1 = 1.75 + 0.30 + 1.00 + 0.45 = 3.5%. Note the 20% gas spike — the thing everyone sees on street signs — contributes only 1 point.

Because food and energy prices whipsaw with weather and geopolitics, analysts also watch core inflation (everything except food and energy) to see the underlying trend. Headline is what households feel; core is what forecasts better.

Where does inflation come from? Three classic stories, and real episodes usually mix them. Demand-pull: total spending outruns what the economy can produce — too much money chasing too few goods (think stimulus checks meeting a reopening economy in 2021). Cost-push: input costs jump and producers pass them on — the 1973 oil embargo quadrupled crude prices and inflation followed. And the monetary story: if the money supply grows persistently faster than output, prices eventually follow — Milton Friedman's famous line that inflation is "always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon" is the extreme version, and it becomes exactly true in every hyperinflation.

So why do the Fed, the ECB, and most modern central banks target roughly 2% inflation rather than zero? Four reasons. Measured inflation overstates true inflation slightly (indexes struggle with quality improvements and substitution), so 2% measured is closer to 0–1% real. A buffer above zero keeps the economy away from deflation. Mild inflation greases labor markets: firms can cut real wages by freezing nominal pay, which is easier than cutting paychecks outright. And it leaves the central bank room to push real interest rates below zero in a crisis — more in Section 4 (Central Banks).

Deflation sounds pleasant — falling prices! — and is anything but. If prices fall steadily, waiting becomes profitable: why buy today what's cheaper next month? Spending stalls, so firms cut prices and jobs, so spending stalls further. Worse, debts are fixed in dollars, so falling prices and wages make every mortgage and business loan heavier in real terms. That self-reinforcing spiral is the deflation trap; Japan spent much of the 1990s and 2000s stuck in a mild version of it, which is why central banks fear 0% far more than 3%.

Inflation time machine

Set an average inflation rate and watch what it quietly does to cash. Try the presets — especially the third one.

At 3% average inflation, in 30 years your $100.00 buys what $41.20 buys today. To match it, you would need $242.73.

Play with the presets and you'll feel the compounding viscerally: at the 2% target, $100 still buys $55 worth of today's goods after 30 years — a slow, planned-for leak. At the 7% America actually averaged across the 1970s, $100 shrinks to about $13 of buying power over 30 years. That's why "cash is safe" is only true in the short run — a theme we'll return to in Section 7 (Equities) and Section 8 (Fixed Income).

And then there's hyperinflation — conventionally, inflation above 50% per month. In Weimar Germany in 1923, prices doubled roughly every three to four days at the peak; workers were paid twice daily and sprinted to spend wages before evening, and banknotes were cheaper than firewood. Zimbabwe in November 2008 hit an estimated 79 billion percent per month — prices doubling roughly every day — until the country simply abandoned its own currency. The mechanism is always the same: a government that can't tax or borrow prints money to pay its bills, everyone races to swap cash for anything real, and money stops doing its job (recall the three functions of money from Section 1 — hyperinflation kills all three). The "Argentina-style 50%" preset above is annual, not monthly — and even that halves your purchasing power every 1.7 years.

Unemployment: one number, many stories

The unemployment rate is the share of the labor force — not the population — that is jobless and actively looking. Both italicized conditions hide bodies. Retirees, full-time students, stay-at-home parents, and people who've given up searching aren't unemployed; they're simply not in the labor force at all.

Concrete version: imagine 100 working-age adults. 60 have jobs, 4 are searching, 36 are out of the labor force. The labor force is 64, so unemployment is 4 ÷ 64 = 6.25%. Now suppose 2 of the searchers get demoralized and stop looking. Nobody found a job — but the labor force shrinks to 62, unemployed drops to 2, and the rate "improves" to 2 ÷ 62 = 3.2%. The headline halved while the economy got slightly worse.

Common pitfall

Never read the unemployment rate alone. Always check the labor-force participation rate next to it — if unemployment fell but participation fell too, discouraged workers may be doing the "improving." Broader measures like U-6 (which adds discouraged workers and part-timers who want full-time work) often tell a darker story than the headline U-3 rate.

Economists also sort unemployment by cause, because each type calls for a different fix. Frictional unemployment is people between jobs — a data scientist who quit Friday and starts interviews Monday. Structural unemployment is a skills or geography mismatch — coal miners in a region where the new jobs are in data centers. Cyclical unemployment is the recession kind: demand collapses everywhere at once and layoffs sweep across industries.

This is why zero unemployment is neither achievable nor desirable. A healthy, dynamic economy always has churn — people quitting bad matches to search for better ones is how workers and jobs find each other, like retries in a matching algorithm. The rate consistent with "everyone who wants a job at prevailing wages can find one reasonably fast" is called the natural rate, roughly 4–5% in the US. Below it, employers bid up wages and inflation pressure builds; above it, cyclical slack is wasting human potential. The Fed is legally tasked with balancing both sides — its "dual mandate," coming in Section 4.

Business cycles: the economy's heartbeat

Zoom out on any real GDP chart and you see a rising trend with a wobble around it. The wobble is the business cycle: an expansion climbs to a peak, activity contracts through a recession to a trough, then recovery begins the next expansion. The cycle is recurring but not periodic — expansions have lasted anywhere from one year to over a decade (post-1945 US expansions average about five years; recessions about ten months). Anyone selling you a fixed-length "cycle clock" is selling astrology.

Who decides when a recession officially happened? In the US it's the NBER (National Bureau of Economic Research), a committee of economists who date peaks and troughs using a dashboard of monthly data — payrolls, real income, industrial production, real spending — not the folk rule of "two negative GDP quarters." The catch: they rule only when the evidence is unambiguous, which means after the fact. The 2020 recession's end (April 2020) wasn't declared until July 2021 — fifteen months later. By the time a recession is official, markets have long since moved on.

That's why analysts obsess over indicator timing. Leading indicators (building permits, new factory orders, stock prices, jobless claims, the yield curve) tend to turn months before the economy. Coincident indicators (payrolls, industrial production) tell you where you are. Lagging indicators (the unemployment rate, CPI inflation) confirm what already happened — unemployment often keeps rising well after recovery begins, because firms rehire cautiously.

The most famous leading indicator deserves its own sentence. The yield curve plots government bond interest rates by maturity; normally long rates exceed short rates. When the curve inverts — short rates above long — bond investors are collectively betting the Fed will be cutting rates in the future, which usually means they expect a downturn. An inverted curve has preceded every US recession of the past six decades, typically by 6–24 months, with only one clear false alarm in the mid-1960s (and a long, hotly debated inversion in 2022–24 that was not followed by a recession on schedule). Why it works, and why it might stop working, is a Section 8 (Fixed Income) story.

AD–AS: one mental model for every shock

You now have the dashboard: output, prices, jobs. What you need is a way to reason about how a shock — an oil spike, a stimulus bill, a pandemic — moves the needles. The workhorse model is aggregate demand and aggregate supply, and it's a deliberate echo of the supply-and-demand cross from Section 2, with the whole economy on the axes: total real output on the x-axis, the overall price level on the y-axis.

Aggregate demand (AD) is total spending — C + I + G + NX again, now drawn as a curve. It slopes down: a lower price level stretches money and wealth further and tends to come with lower interest rates, both of which coax out more spending. Aggregate supply (AS) slopes up in the short run for a very human reason: wages and many prices are sticky — renegotiated slowly — so when selling prices rise before costs catch up, producing more is temporarily profitable. In the long run wages catch up and output settles at the economy's potential, set by the productivity fundamentals from earlier.

Now every macro headline becomes a two-step question: which curve moved, and which way?

ShockCurveOutputPrices
Stimulus checks, tax cuts, rate cutsAD shifts right↑↑
Oil spike, supply-chain breakdown, warAS shifts left↓↑
Financial crisis, confidence collapseAD shifts left↓↓
Pandemic lockdownBoth shift left at once↓↓mixed

Real world: 2020–2022 in one framework

The pandemic hit both curves: AS left (factories and services shut) and AD left (spending froze) — output cratered while prices barely moved at first. Then 2021 reversed the demand side violently: trillions in stimulus plus reopening shifted AD hard right while AS was still crippled by supply chains and, in 2022, an energy shock. Demand up plus supply down is the textbook recipe for the inflation surge to ~9% — and the model also explains why it faded as supply chains healed and the Fed leaned on demand.

The supply-shock case is the nasty one, and it earned its own word in the 1970s: stagflation — stagnation plus inflation. It broke the era's favorite empirical relationship, the Phillips curve: through the 1960s, US data traced a tidy trade-off (low unemployment came with higher inflation, and vice versa), and policymakers treated it like a menu. Then the 1973 oil shock shifted AS left and the dots jumped off the curve — high inflation and high unemployment at once, which the "menu" said was impossible.

Deep dive: why the Phillips curve broke — expectations

Milton Friedman and Edmund Phelps predicted the breakdown before it happened, in 1968. Their argument: the trade-off only works while workers are fooled. Run the economy hot and inflation rises; workers eventually notice, demand raises to match, firms' cost advantage evaporates, and unemployment drifts back to its natural rate — but now with permanently higher inflation baked in. Formally, the expectations-augmented version is:

π = πe − b × (u − u*) + supply shocks
π = inflation · πe = expected inflation · u = unemployment · u* = natural rate · b > 0

The policy punchline: the curve is only a usable trade-off when expectations (πe) stay anchored. That is why modern central banks talk obsessively about "anchored expectations" and why they react so aggressively when surveys and market-implied inflation forecasts start drifting — once πe moves, you're fighting the 1970s again. The bond-market gauges of πe live in Section 8.

Reading macro data like an analyst

Here's the part textbooks skip. In markets, macro data isn't trivia — it's a scheduled stream of events that reprice trillions of dollars within seconds of release. The key mechanic: prices already reflect the consensus estimate before the number drops. So markets don't move on whether the data is good; they move on whether it beats or misses expectations. A "bad" jobs number that's less bad than forecast can spark a rally. (Why prices embed expectations is the subject of Section 17 (Market Efficiency).)

Two analyst habits to steal. First, always ask "versus what?" — a payrolls print of +180,000 means nothing until you know consensus was +200,000 (a miss) or +150,000 (a beat). Second, know the regime: in a "good news is bad news" market, a strong jobs report can sink stocks because it implies the Fed keeps rates high. The same number, opposite reaction, depending on what investors fear more — recession or inflation.

Your edge

If you build models on macro data, respect two data-engineering realities. Releases get revised — payrolls are routinely restated by tens of thousands of jobs over the following two months — so backtesting on today's cleaned-up series quietly leaks the future. Serious quant shops use point-in-time ("vintage") datasets that store what was known on each date. And engineer features from surprises (actual minus consensus), not levels: the level was already priced.

Check your understanding

1. Nominal GDP grew 6% last year while the GDP deflator rose 4%. Real growth was roughly…

Real growth ≈ nominal growth minus inflation: exactly 1.06 ÷ 1.04 − 1 ≈ 1.9%, or 2% with the quick subtraction shortcut. Always strip out prices before calling something "growth."

2. Why do most central banks target about 2% inflation rather than 0%?

Zero leaves no safety margin: a small shock tips you into deflation, where falling prices raise real debt burdens and reward waiting instead of spending. A 2% cushion also absorbs the slight upward bias in measured inflation and greases nominal wage adjustment.

3. The unemployment rate falls from 6.3% to 5.8%. Which scenario is consistent with a weaker economy?

The rate only counts people actively looking. If searchers give up, they exit the labor force and the rate falls even though nobody found a job — which is why analysts read the participation rate alongside the headline number.

4. An inverted yield curve is best described as a ___ indicator.

Inversion has preceded every US recession of the past six decades, typically by 6–24 months — the definition of a leading indicator. Lagging indicators like the unemployment rate only confirm turns after the fact.

5. An oil price spike hits the economy. In the AD–AS framework, this…

Oil is an input cost, so it moves the supply curve — leftward, since production gets more expensive at every price level. That pushes output and prices in opposite directions, the signature of a supply shock and of the 1970s.

Key takeaways

  • GDP = C + I + G + NX measures production, with US consumption ~68% of the total. Use real (inflation-adjusted) and per-capita versions for growth and living standards — and remember GDP ignores unpaid work, the environment, and distribution.
  • Growth compounds: 1.5% vs 3% per year turns identical countries into a 2.4× income gap within one lifetime. Long-run growth per person comes almost entirely from productivity.
  • Inflation is a weighted average of basket price changes (CPI). Central banks target ~2% as a buffer against the self-reinforcing deflation trap. Compounding cuts hard: at the 1970s' ~7%, $100 keeps only ~$13 of purchasing power over 30 years.
  • Hyperinflation (>50% per month) is always a fiscal-printing story — Weimar 1923 and Zimbabwe 2008 destroyed their currencies within months.
  • The unemployment rate counts only active job-seekers: read it with labor-force participation, and remember some (frictional) unemployment is healthy churn.
  • Business cycles are recurring but not periodic. Leading indicators (yield curve, permits, claims) turn before the peak; lagging ones (unemployment, CPI) only confirm. NBER dates recessions long after the fact.
  • AD–AS is the diagnostic: demand shocks move output and prices together; supply shocks split them apart (stagflation). It explains 2020–22 in one picture.
  • Markets price the consensus in advance — data moves markets only by surprising, and revisions mean serious analysis needs point-in-time data.
