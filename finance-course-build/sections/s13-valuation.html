13. Valuation: What Is a Company Worth?

Every price on a stock screen is an opinion; valuation is how you form your own. By the end of this section you will be able to build a discounted cash flow model from scratch, sanity-check it with multiples, and answer the classic interview question "walk me through a DCF" without breaking a sweat.

Price is what you pay, value is what you estimate

In Section 7 (Equities) you saw that a stock's market price is just the last price at which two strangers agreed to trade. That number is a fact. What the company is actually worth — its intrinsic value — is not a fact. It's an estimate, and every serious valuation comes with error bars, whether the analyst admits it or not.

There are two big families of valuation. Intrinsic valuation asks: what cash will this business hand its owners over its lifetime, and what is that stream worth today? That's the discounted cash flow (DCF) approach, and it's just the time-value machinery from Section 1 pointed at an entire company. Relative valuation asks a lazier but often smarter question: what are similar companies selling for right now? That's the world of multiples and comparable companies.

Analogy

Pricing a house works the same way. Intrinsic: estimate the rent it could earn forever and discount it. Relative: check what the three nearly identical houses on the same street sold for last quarter. Neither is "the truth" — a good agent runs both and gets suspicious when they disagree wildly.

Here's the mindset shift that separates professionals from spreadsheet jockeys: a valuation model is structured estimation under uncertainty. You are not computing the answer; you are organizing your assumptions so that the disagreement between your number and the market's number is legible. If your DCF says $31.50 and the market says $28, the model tells you exactly which beliefs — growth, margins, risk — you'd have to hold for either number to be right. That's the entire point.

Your edge

You already do this. A valuation model is a forward inference with hand-tuned priors: point estimates in, point estimate out, and the honest deliverable is a sensitivity analysis, not a single number. Treat "intrinsic value = $31.50" the way you'd treat a regression prediction with no confidence interval — politely, but skeptically.

A DCF end to end: every single number

Meet Cascade Beverages, our fictional workhorse. It generated free cash flow (FCF) of $100M this year, carries net debt of $200M, has 50M shares outstanding, and trades at $28 per share. FCF is the star of the show: it comes from the statements you built in Section 11 — roughly operating profit after tax, plus depreciation, minus capital expenditure and working-capital growth. It's what's left for the people who financed the business.

Move 2 — the terminal value. Cascade doesn't vaporize after year 5, so we need the value of everything beyond the forecast horizon. The standard tool is the Gordon growth model: assume FCF grows at a modest constant rate g forever, and value that perpetuity. With terminal growth g = 2% and a discount rate (WACC, next subsection) of 10%:

TV = FCF5 × (1 + g) ÷ (WACC − g) = 161.05 × 1.02 ÷ (0.10 − 0.02) = $2,053.4M
TV = terminal value at end of year 5 · g = perpetual growth rate · WACC = discount rate

Move 3 — discount everything to today. The explicit years are already done ($500M). The terminal value sits at the end of year 5, so it gets the year-5 discount factor: 2,053.4 × 0.6209 = $1,275.0M. Adding up gives enterprise value (EV): 500 + 1,275 = $1,775M. Notice the uncomfortable ratio: the terminal value is 72% of the total. Most of a typical DCF is the part you know least about.

Move 4 — from the business to the shareholders. EV belongs to everyone who financed the company. Lenders have first claim, so subtract net debt to get equity value: 1,775 − 200 = $1,575M. Move 5 — per share. Divide by 50M shares: $31.50. Against the $28 market price, our model implies about 12.5% upside — or, said honestly, our assumptions are about 12.5% more optimistic than the market's.

WACC: the price tag on risk

We discounted at 10%. Where did that come from? The discount rate for a whole company is the WACC — weighted average cost of capital. The logic: a company is financed by a mix of debt and equity, each with its own required return, so the business as a whole must clear a blend of the two. It is literally a weighted average — no scarier than that.

WACC = (E ÷ V) × re + (D ÷ V) × rd × (1 − t)
E = market value of equity · D = market value of debt · V = E + D · re = cost of equity · rd = cost of debt · t = tax rate

The cost of equity is the return shareholders demand. The workhorse estimate is the CAPM: re = rf + β × ERP, where rf is the risk-free rate (a long Treasury yield, from Section 8), beta measures how much the stock swings with the market, and the equity risk premium (ERP) is the extra return the market demands over Treasuries. The full story behind CAPM — diversification, why only market risk gets paid — lives in Section 15 (Portfolio Theory); for now, treat it as a pricing formula for risk.

For Cascade: rf = 4%, β = 1.25, ERP = 5.5% → re = 4% + 1.25 × 5.5% = 10.9%. The cost of debt is what lenders charge — say 6% — but interest is tax-deductible, creating a tax shield, so the after-tax cost at a 25% tax rate is 6% × 0.75 = 4.5%. Weight by market values: equity = 50M × $28 = $1,400M; debt = $300M (gross debt; the $200M net debt is $300M debt minus $100M cash). So:

WACC = (1,400 ÷ 1,700) × 10.9% + (300 ÷ 1,700) × 4.5% = 8.98% + 0.79% ≈ 9.8%
We round to 10% for clean arithmetic — and because pretending WACC is precise to two decimals is theater.

Why does riskier mean a higher discount rate? Because a dollar you might not get is worth less than a dollar you will. Discounting a volatile biotech's projected cash flows at 6% quietly asserts they're as dependable as a utility's — and the model will happily reward that lie with a huge valuation. The discount rate is where skepticism about the cash flows gets encoded. (How companies choose their debt-equity mix — and whether WACC can be engineered down — is the subject of Section 14.)

Common pitfall

Weighting WACC with book values from the balance sheet instead of market values, or using yesterday's interest rates for rf. WACC is a current market opportunity cost: what investors could earn elsewhere today, not what the company paid historically.

The embarrassing truth: sensitivity

Now for the part practitioners whisper about. Nudge WACC from 10% to 9% and terminal growth from 2% to 3%, and Cascade's per-share value jumps from $31.50 to $42.22. Push the other way — WACC 11%, growth 1% — and it drops to $25.04. That's a 69% swing from moving two inputs by one percentage point each, inputs nobody can estimate to within a point anyway. The culprit is the terminal value's denominator, WACC − g: it's a small difference of two uncertain numbers, and dividing by a small shaky number is exactly as unstable as it sounds.

Honest analysts respond in two ways. First, the sensitivity table: a grid of valuations across WACC and g, so the reader sees the whole distribution instead of a false point estimate. Second, the football field chart: the valuation range from each method drawn as horizontal bars, with the market price overlaid. Where the bars overlap is where your conviction should live.

Relative valuation: comps and precedents

Relative valuation outsources the hard assumptions to the market. The comps method (comparable companies) runs in five steps. Step 1: choose the peer set — companies with similar business models, growth, margins, and size. This is the step that quietly decides your answer; put a sleepy bottler in a peer set of energy-drink rockets and everything downstream is garbage-in. Step 2: pull each peer's multiples. Step 3: take the median (medians resist the one weird outlier). Step 4: apply it to your company's metric. Step 5: convert to per-share value.

The two workhorse multiples divide the world by capital structure. EV/EBITDA compares the whole business to a pre-interest profit measure (you met EBITDA in Section 12), so it's immune to how much debt each peer carries — ideal when leverage varies across the set, and standard in deal-making. The P/E ratio compares equity price to equity earnings — fine when peers have similar debt loads, and the default for quick public-market comparisons. Mixing them up is a classic rookie tell: never put an equity price over a pre-interest profit measure, or you're comparing numerators and denominators that belong to different claimants.

Worked example

Cascade's EBITDA is $180M. Five peers trade at EV/EBITDA of 9.2×, 10.1×, 10.8×, 11.5×, and 14.0× (median 10.8×). Implied EV = 10.8 × 180 = $1,944M. Subtract $200M net debt → equity $1,744M → ÷ 50M shares → $34.88. Note the same tail-end steps as the DCF: multiples on EV still require the EV → equity → per-share march.

Precedent transactions are comps built from actual acquisitions instead of trading prices. They come out systematically higher because buyers pay a control premium — typically 20–40% over the pre-announcement price — for the right to run the company, fire the CEO, and capture synergies. Use precedents to value a company being sold; use trading comps to value shares you'd merely hold.

Key idea

A multiple is a compressed DCF. EV/EBITDA of 10.8× is shorthand for a whole bundle of growth, margin, and risk assumptions the market is making about the peers. Comps don't avoid assumptions — they borrow the market's, which is great exactly when the market knows more than you and dangerous exactly when it doesn't (see Section 17 on bubbles).

"Walk me through a DCF" — the interview answer

This is the single most common technical question in finance interviews. Here is an answer you can deliver verbatim in about sixty seconds — every clause of which you can now defend:

Say this

  1. "A DCF values a company as the present value of its future free cash flows. I'd start by projecting unlevered free cash flow for five to ten years — revenue growth down to operating profit, taxed, plus depreciation, minus capex and the change in working capital."
  2. "Next I'd estimate a terminal value for everything beyond the projection window, either with the Gordon growth method — final-year FCF times one plus g, over WACC minus g — or an exit multiple on terminal-year EBITDA, and I'd cross-check one against the other."
  3. "Then I'd discount the explicit cash flows and the terminal value back to today at the WACC — cost of equity from CAPM, after-tax cost of debt, weighted by market values. That sum is enterprise value."
  4. "To get to what shareholders own, I'd subtract net debt — and any preferred stock or minority interests — to reach equity value, then divide by diluted shares for a per-share value."
  5. "Finally I'd sensitize it — a WACC-versus-terminal-growth grid — because the terminal value usually carries 60 to 80 percent of the total, and I'd triangulate against trading comps and precedent transactions before trusting the number."

Two follow-ups arrive almost every time. "Why do you subtract net debt?" Because enterprise value belongs to all capital providers and lenders get paid first; equity holders own only what's left. "What dominates the output?" WACC and terminal growth — which is precisely why you sensitize them rather than defend a point estimate. Saying that last part signals you've actually built one.

Where each method breaks — and why analysts triangulate

Every valuation tool has a failure mode, and knowing them is worth more than knowing the formulas. DCF breaks for pre-revenue startups: with negative cash flows for years, essentially 100% of the value sits in a terminal value built on guesses stacked on guesses — the model becomes a random-number generator wearing a suit. Venture investors use staged expected-value thinking and comparables instead. P/E breaks for cyclicals: an automaker looks cheapest (low P/E) at the peak of the cycle, exactly when earnings are about to collapse — the multiple is low because the E is temporarily inflated. Analysts use normalized, mid-cycle earnings instead. Book value breaks for asset-light companies: a software firm's engineers, code, and brand barely appear on its balance sheet (as you saw in Section 11), so price-to-book is nearly meaningless for them.

Banks get their own playbook. For a bank, debt isn't financing — it's raw material (deposits in, loans out), so "enterprise value" and EV/EBITDA stop making sense. Analysts value banks on equity-side measures: P/E, price-to-book (book value actually means something when assets are financial and marked close to reality), and dividend-based models, all disciplined by regulatory capital requirements from Section 6.

Hence the professional habit you saw in the football field: triangulate. Run a DCF for the fundamental logic, trading comps for market context, precedents if a sale is plausible — then interrogate the disagreements. If the DCF says $42 and comps say $30, either your assumptions are heroic or the market is mispricing the sector; both are worth knowing, and the gap itself is the insight. Many investors also demand a margin of safety — acting only when price sits far below the whole range — precisely because they know their estimate has error bars.

Deep dive: two refinements you'll meet in real models

Exit multiples. Instead of Gordon growth, many bankers set the terminal value as a multiple of terminal-year EBITDA — e.g. TV = 10 × EBITDA5 — borrowing today's comps for the far future. It feels more concrete but smuggles relative valuation into your intrinsic model; best practice computes TV both ways and checks that each implies a sane version of the other (a Gordon TV implies an exit multiple, and vice versa).

Mid-year convention. Our model discounts each year's FCF as if it all arrives on December 31, but cash actually dribbles in through the year. Discounting at t = 0.5, 1.5, 2.5… (dividing by (1+WACC)t−0.5) treats flows as arriving mid-year and raises the valuation by roughly half a year of discounting — about 4–5% at a 10% WACC. Small, but interviewers love asking about it.

Check your understanding

1. Your DCF gives an enterprise value of $1,775M. The company has $300M of debt, $100M of cash, and 50M shares. What is the per-share value?

EV belongs to debt and equity holders together. Subtract net debt (300 − 100 = 200) to get equity value of $1,575M, then divide by 50M shares: $31.50. Ignoring debt overstates the answer; ignoring the cash understates it.

2. In a typical 5-year DCF, roughly what share of enterprise value comes from the terminal value?

Terminal value usually carries 60–80% of EV (72% in our Cascade model). That is why WACC and terminal growth dominate the output — and why honest analysts always present a sensitivity grid.

3. You're comping five peers whose debt loads range from zero to heavy. Which multiple keeps the comparison fair?

P/E mixes an equity price with post-interest earnings, so leverage distorts it differently for each peer. EV/EBITDA compares whole-business value to pre-interest profit, neutralizing capital-structure differences.

4. WACC is 8% and terminal growth is 3%. An analyst nudges terminal growth to 4%. What happens to the terminal value?

TV = FCF×(1+g)/(WACC−g). The denominator drops from 0.05 to 0.04 (a 25% boost) and the numerator ticks up too — about a 26% jump in total from a one-point tweak. The WACC−g spread is the most explosive input in the model.

5. Why does a DCF fail for a pre-revenue startup?

The mechanics still run — you can always discount numbers — but with negative explicit-year flows, the answer is nearly 100% terminal value built on guesses. The model outputs false precision rather than insight; comparables and staged expected-value approaches work better.

Key takeaways

  • Price is a fact; intrinsic value is an estimate with error bars. A valuation model organizes your assumptions so disagreement with the market becomes legible.
  • DCF in five moves: project FCF for 5–10 years → terminal value via TV = FCF×(1+g)/(WACC−g) → discount everything at WACC → EV − net debt = equity value → ÷ shares. Cascade: $500M + $1,275M = $1,775M EV → $31.50/share.
  • WACC blends the CAPM cost of equity (rf + β×ERP) with the after-tax cost of debt, weighted at market values. Riskier cash flows get higher rates because a dollar you might not receive is worth less.
  • Terminal value is typically 60–80% of EV, and ±1pp on WACC or g swings the answer by double digits — present sensitivity grids and football fields, not point estimates.
  • Comps borrow the market's assumptions: pick honest peers, use median multiples, EV/EBITDA when leverage differs, P/E when it doesn't. Precedent transactions run 20–40% higher because of the control premium.
  • Every method has a failure mode — DCF for pre-revenue startups, P/E for cyclicals, book value for asset-light firms — and banks get equity-side multiples because their debt is raw material. So professionals triangulate.
  • You can now answer "walk me through a DCF" verbatim — and the follow-ups about net debt and sensitivity that prove you mean it.
