14. Corporate Finance: Decisions Inside the Firm

So far you have watched companies from the outside — reading their statements, computing their ratios, estimating their value. This section walks you through the door: how the people running the firm decide what to build, how to pay for it, and what to hand back to shareholders, using the exact toolkit you have already assembled.

The CFO's three questions

Strip away the org charts and the earnings calls, and a CFO's job reduces to three questions about every dollar the firm touches:

  • What should we invest in? This is capital budgeting — build the factory, fund the R&D, buy the rival, or do nothing.
  • How should we fund it? This is capital structure — borrow money, issue shares, or use retained profits.
  • What should we give back? This is payout policy — dividends, buybacks, or reinvest everything.

Every corporate headline you will ever read maps to one of the three. "Apple announces $110B buyback" — payout. "Microsoft acquires Activision" — capital budgeting (an acquisition is just a very large project). "Tesla raises $5B in new shares" — capital structure. Once you have this map, financial news stops being noise and becomes a stream of labeled training data.

Key idea

This section is where the course converges. Section 1 gave you discounting, Section 11 gave you the statements, Section 12 gave you ROE and ROIC, and Section 13 gave you valuation. Corporate finance is those four tools pointed inward: the CFO runs the same math on the firm's own projects that an analyst runs on the firm's stock.

Capital budgeting I: the NPV rule

Suppose your firm can spend $1,000 today on a project that returns cash over five years. Should it? The gold-standard answer is net present value (NPV): discount every future cash flow back to today at the appropriate rate, add them up (including the negative outlay), and accept the project if the total is positive.

NPV = Σt=0n CFt ÷ (1 + r)t
CFt = cash flow in year t (CF0 is usually negative) · r = discount rate · accept if NPV > 0

Nothing here is new. This is literally the time-value machinery from Section 1 applied to a project instead of a bank deposit, and it is the same present-value engine that powered the DCF in Section 13. A firm is a portfolio of projects; a DCF values the whole portfolio, capital budgeting values one candidate at a time.

Worked example: two rival projects

Project A ("Steady") costs $1,000 and pays $300 a year for five years. Project B ("Moonshot") costs $1,000, pays nothing for four years, then $2,000 in year 5. At a 10% discount rate:

NPVA = −1,000 + 300×(1 − 1.10−5)÷0.10 = −1,000 + 300×3.7908 = +$137
NPVB = −1,000 + 2,000÷1.105 = −1,000 + 2,000×0.6209 = +$242

Both add value, but if they are mutually exclusive, take B: it adds $105 more in today's dollars. Hold that thought — a popular rival metric is about to disagree.

A useful picture is the NPV profile: plot NPV against the discount rate. At r = 0 the curve starts at the raw sum of cash flows; as r rises, distant cash gets crushed and the curve falls; where it crosses zero is a rate with a famous name you'll meet next.

Your edge

NPV is a fold: cashflows.reduce((s, cf, t) => s + cf / (1 + r) ** t, 0). One line of code, and it is the single most consequential formula in corporate life. When you build financial models, most of the complexity is estimating the inputs (the cash flows), not the math.

Capital budgeting II: IRR, payback, and their traps

In practice, executives quote a project's IRR (internal rate of return) more often than its NPV. IRR is the discount rate that makes NPV exactly zero — the project's implied annualized return. The decision rule: accept if IRR exceeds your hurdle rate. It is intuitive ("this project yields 15%") and unit-free, which is exactly why people love it — and exactly where it bites:

  • Scale-blindness. A $1 project returning $2 next year has a 100% IRR and adds about $0.82 of value at r = 10%. A $1,000 project returning $1,300 has a mere 30% IRR and adds $182. IRR picks the first; your bank account prefers the second. Percentages don't pay salaries — dollars do.
  • Multiple IRRs. If cash flows change sign more than once (invest, earn, then pay cleanup costs), the NPV polynomial can have several roots. The flows −100, +230, −132 have IRRs at both 10% and 20% — the number "the" IRR is simply undefined. (Check: at 15%, NPV = −100 + 230/1.15 − 132/1.15² = +0.19, positive between the two roots.)
  • The reinvestment assumption. IRR implicitly assumes every interim cash flow gets reinvested at the IRR itself. A 40% IRR project assumes you can redeploy each payout at 40% — usually fantasy. NPV assumes reinvestment at the discount rate, which is honest.

The third tool you will meet in the wild is the payback period: how long until cumulative cash flows cover the outlay. Project A pays back in 1,000 ÷ 300 ≈ 3.3 years; Project B not until year 5. Payback is a fine quick-and-dirty liquidity check, but it ignores the time value of money and everything after the payback date — by payback logic, a project that returns your money in year 3 and then explodes beats one that pays a fortune in year 6.

Now the main event. Below, Projects A and B from the worked example face off across every discount rate at once. B has the bigger prize but makes you wait; A drip-feeds cash early. B's curve therefore starts higher but falls faster — and the curves cross. Note the paradox: A has the higher IRR (15.2% vs 14.9%), yet at any rate below ~14.4%, B has the higher NPV. IRR and NPV genuinely disagree, and when they do, trust NPV: it measures dollars of value created at your actual cost of capital.

Common pitfall

Private equity marketing decks lean hard on IRR because early cash returns inflate it. A fund that returns your money fast at a modest multiple can post a dazzling IRR while creating less wealth than a slower fund with a bigger multiple. Always ask for the dollar multiple alongside the percentage.

The hurdle rate: WACC and the value-creation test

NPV needs a discount rate — but which one? The firm's money comes from two groups with claims on it: lenders and shareholders. Each demands a return: the cost of debt is observable (it is roughly the yield on the firm's bonds, from Section 8), while the cost of equity is the return shareholders require given the stock's risk. Blend them by their share of the capital pie and you get the WACC — the weighted average cost of capital.

WACC = (E ÷ V) × rE + (D ÷ V) × rD × (1 − t)
E, D = market value of equity, debt · V = E + D · rE, rD = cost of equity, debt · t = tax rate (interest is tax-deductible)

Worked: a firm financed 70% with equity costing 9% and 30% with debt costing 5% pre-tax, at a 21% tax rate. After-tax debt costs 5% × 0.79 = 3.95%, so WACC = 0.70×9% + 0.30×3.95% = 6.3% + 1.185% ≈ 7.5%. That 7.5% is not an accounting cost — it is an opportunity cost. Investors could earn it elsewhere at similar risk, so any project earning less makes them poorer than the alternative.

This closes the loop you started in Section 12. There you learned to compute ROIC, the return a business earns on the capital tied up inside it. Now you have the benchmark to judge it:

Key idea: the value-creation inequality

A firm creates value if and only if ROIC > WACC. Earn 15% on capital that costs 7.5% and every reinvested dollar is worth more than a dollar; earn 6% and growth actively destroys value — the firm is a machine for converting good money into bad projects. Revenue can grow, EPS can grow, the CEO can ring the opening bell, and value still burns. This single inequality explains most of what separates great compounders from value traps in Section 13-style valuations.

In practice firms set a hurdle rate at or slightly above WACC and require project sponsors to clear it. The quiet failure mode: divisions with risky projects using the company-wide WACC, understating their true cost of capital — a subsidy from the boring divisions to the exciting ones.

Capital structure: the debt–equity trade-off

Now the funding question. Debt is cheaper than equity — lenders take less risk (they get paid first) and interest is tax-deductible. So why not fund everything with debt? Because debt is a hard promise, and hard promises turn bad years into fatal ones. The trade-off has two arms.

The prize: the tax shield. Interest is deductible, dividends are not. Borrow $100M at 6% and the $6M interest bill cuts taxable income by $6M, saving $6M × 21% = $1.26M in tax every year — a tax shield. If the debt is permanent, that stream, discounted at 6%, is worth 1.26 ÷ 0.06 = $21M, i.e. tax rate × debt. Real money, courtesy of the tax code.

The price: financial distress. Pile on debt and the probability of missing a payment rises. Financial distress costs are not just bankruptcy lawyers — customers abandon a company that might not honor warranties, suppliers demand cash upfront, star employees leave, and management cancels good projects to hoard cash. Value leaks long before any default.

Between prize and price sits leverage's defining property: it amplifies. Take a firm with $100M of assets. Unlevered, a 12% asset return means 12% ROE. Now fund it with $50M equity + $50M debt at 5% (interest $2.5M, taxes ignored for clarity):

ScenarioAsset returnProfit after interestROE (on $50M equity)
Good year+12% → $12.0M$12.0M − $2.5M = $9.5M+19.0%
Bad year−4% → −$4.0M−$4.0M − $2.5M = −$6.5M−13.0%

Same business, same swings in asset returns — but the equity holder's ride goes from (12%, −4%) to (19%, −13%). Leverage is a gain knob on the whole distribution, exactly the DuPont mechanism from Section 12 seen from the driver's seat. The general formula, with debt costing rD and leverage L = assets ÷ equity: ROE = L × ROA − (L − 1) × rD. Check: 2 × 12% − 1 × 5% = 19%. ✓

One paragraph of theory to frame it all: in 1958, Modigliani and Miller proved that in a perfectly frictionless world — no taxes, no distress costs, no information asymmetries — capital structure is irrelevant: slicing the pizza into different pieces doesn't change how much pizza there is. That sounds like a useless result, but it is the opposite: it tells you that capital structure matters only through the frictions. Taxes, distress, and signaling are not footnotes to the theory — they are the whole game. It is the finance equivalent of a no-free-lunch theorem: it tells you exactly where to look for the lunch.

Payout policy: dividends vs buybacks

When a firm generates more cash than its positive-NPV projects can absorb, holding it is itself a capital-budgeting decision — one that usually earns money-market returns while shareholders' opportunity cost is the WACC. Time for question three: return it. There are two pipes.

A dividend is a direct cash payment per share; once established, markets treat it as a quasi-promise, which is why cuts are rare and brutal for the stock. A buyback (share repurchase) uses the cash to buy the firm's own shares in the market, shrinking the share count so each remaining share owns a bigger slice. Mechanically the two are close cousins; practically they differ in three ways:

  • Taxes. Dividends force a taxable event on every shareholder every quarter. Buybacks tax only those who choose to sell, and only on their gains — everyone else compounds untaxed. This is a big reason US payout has shifted heavily toward buybacks since the 1980s (SEC rule 10b-18 made them safe from manipulation charges in 1982; a 1% excise tax was added in 2023).
  • Flexibility and signaling. A dividend hike says "we are confident this cash level is permanent." A buyback authorization is a maybe — it can be quietly abandoned. Markets price the difference.
  • Price sensitivity. A dividend's value doesn't depend on the stock price. A buyback's does — completely.

Worked example: when buybacks create or destroy value

A firm has 100M shares trading at $50, and $500M of cash it will spend buying back 10M shares. Suppose your analysis (Section 13) says intrinsic value is $70/share — total intrinsic equity $7,000M. After the buyback, remaining intrinsic value is $7,000M − $500M cash out = $6,500M across 90M shares = $72.22/share. Continuing holders gained $2.22 of value per share, transferred from sellers who accepted $50 for $70 of value.

Now flip it: intrinsic value is only $40 (the stock is overpriced at $50). Post-buyback: ($4,000M − $500M) ÷ 90M = $38.89. Management just paid $50 for $40 bills, and continuing holders footed the difference. A buyback is simply an investment in your own stock — the NPV rule applies to it like everything else. The empirical embarrassment: corporate America's buyback volume peaks near market tops and dries up in crashes, i.e. firms systematically buy high. Funding buybacks with debt at cycle peaks compounds the error with leverage.

Neither pipe creates value by itself — moving cash from the company's pocket to yours is plumbing, not alchemy. Value was created earlier, by ROIC > WACC projects. Payout policy decides who holds the cash and what tax bill attaches; the best payout policy is the honest one: return cash when internal projects can't beat the hurdle, retain it when they can.

M&A: buying growth, and why it usually disappoints

M&A is capital budgeting at maximum stakes: instead of building a project, you buy someone else's — typically paying a 20–40% control premium over the market price. Two species of buyer roam this market. A strategic buyer is a company buying a company — it can justify a higher price because it expects synergies: shared costs, cross-sold products, a merged data platform. A financial buyer — typically a private equity fund running a leveraged buyout — buys with borrowed money, applies the leverage amplifier you just played with, and aims to resell in five to seven years.

Synergies deserve your skepticism. The premium is paid on day one, in cash or shares, with certainty; the synergies arrive later, if integration goes well, if key staff stay, if customers don't flee, if the cultures mesh. Study after study finds most deals transfer value to the target's shareholders (who pocket the premium) rather than create it for the acquirer's. And when several bidders compete, the one who wins is the one who overestimated the target most — the winner's curse, straight out of auction theory.

Wall Street's quick screen for a stock deal is accretion/dilution: does the acquirer's earnings per share rise or fall? Here is the arithmetic for an all-stock deal:

LineBigCo (acquirer)SmallCo (target)Combined
Net income$1,000M$200M$1,200M
Shares outstanding500M—500M + 90M new = 590M
Share price / deal price$40 (P/E 20)$3,600M (P/E 18)—
EPS$2.00—$1,200M ÷ 590M = $2.03

BigCo pays $3,600M in its own stock (3,600 ÷ 40 = 90M new shares) and EPS rises 1.7% — accretive. But look at why: BigCo's shares are "worth" 20× earnings while it paid only 18× for SmallCo's. Whenever you buy earnings at a lower P/E than your own using stock, EPS mechanically rises — even if the deal is a disaster. Accretion is arithmetic, not value creation; the NPV question (are the synergies worth more than the premium?) is the only one that matters.

Real world: one funeral, one wedding

AOL–Time Warner (2000): the largest merger ever announced (~$165B, all stock), sold on internet–media synergy at the very peak of the dot-com bubble. AOL paid with wildly overvalued shares, cultures clashed, dial-up collapsed, and in 2002 the company recorded a ~$99B write-down — for years the largest annual corporate loss in US history. Every failure mode in this section in a single deal: peak-cycle timing, phantom synergies, winner's optimism.

Disney–Pixar (2006): $7.4B in stock for a studio Disney distributed but couldn't replicate. The synergy was real and specific — Pixar's creative engine plugged into Disney's merchandising, parks, and distribution machine — and Disney protected it by leaving Pixar's leadership in charge of animation. Frozen, Toy Story 3, and a revived Walt Disney Animation followed; the price looks almost quaint in hindsight. Good deals tend to share this shape: a specific, verifiable synergy and a plan to protect what you bought.

Deep dive: Modigliani–Miller's two propositions, slightly less compressed

Proposition I (the pizza theorem): with frictionless markets, firm value is set by the cash flows the assets generate, not by how claims on them are sliced. The arbitrage proof is elegant — if a levered firm traded at a premium to an identical unlevered one, you could manufacture "homemade leverage" by borrowing personally to buy the cheap unlevered shares, replicating the expensive firm's payoff and pocketing the difference.

Proposition II: the cost of equity rises linearly with leverage: rE = r0 + (D÷E) × (r0 − rD), where r0 is the unlevered cost of capital. Swapping "expensive" equity for "cheap" debt makes the remaining equity riskier and thus costlier, and in a frictionless world the WACC stays exactly flat. So when a CFO claims a debt raise "lowers our cost of capital," the honest version is: only via the tax shield, and only until distress costs eat it. Check your understanding

1. Two mutually exclusive projects: X has IRR 22% and NPV +$3M at the firm's 9% WACC; Y has IRR 15% and NPV +$11M. Which should the firm take?

When rankings conflict, NPV wins: it counts dollars added at the true opportunity cost of capital. IRR is scale-blind and assumes reinvestment at the IRR itself. "Both" fails because the projects are mutually exclusive.

2. A firm's WACC is 8%. It reinvests heavily in projects returning 6%, and its EPS grows every year. What is happening to shareholder value?

ROIC (6%) is below WACC (8%), so each dollar retained turns into less than a dollar of value. Growth amplifies value destruction when returns are below the cost of capital — EPS growth notwithstanding.

3. A firm runs at 3× leverage (assets ÷ equity) with debt costing 5%. Its assets return −2% this year. Roughly what is its ROE?

ROE = L×ROA − (L−1)×rD = 3×(−2%) − 2×5% = −6% − 10% = −16%. The interest on two turns of debt is owed regardless of how the assets performed — that is the downside of the amplifier.

4. Management buys back stock at $80 when a careful intrinsic-value estimate says shares are worth $60. Who wins?

A buyback is an investment in the firm's own stock, so the NPV rule applies: paying $80 for $60 of value transfers the $20 difference from continuing holders to sellers. Higher EPS and a bigger ownership slice don't offset overpaying.

5. An all-stock acquisition is immediately EPS-accretive because the acquirer trades at P/E 24 and paid P/E 16 for the target. What does the accretion prove about value creation?

Swapping high-multiple paper for low-multiple earnings raises EPS by pure arithmetic, even for a value-destroying deal. Whether value is created depends on whether synergies exceed the control premium — a separate, NPV question.

Key takeaways

  • Corporate finance is three questions: what to invest in (capital budgeting), how to fund it (capital structure), and what to return (payout policy). Every corporate headline maps to one.
  • NPV — Section 1's discounting applied to projects — is the gold standard: accept positive-NPV projects, and when rankings conflict, trust dollars over percentages.
  • IRR is scale-blind, can have multiple roots when cash flows change sign twice, and assumes reinvestment at itself; payback ignores time value and everything after the payback date.
  • WACC is the blended opportunity cost of the firm's capital; value is created if and only if ROIC > WACC — growth below the hurdle destroys value even while EPS rises.
  • Debt buys a tax shield but sells insurance against bad years: leverage multiplies ROE in both directions (2× leverage turns +12%/−4% asset returns into +19%/−13% on equity).
  • Modigliani–Miller says capital structure is irrelevant without frictions — so taxes, distress costs, and signaling are precisely where structure matters.
  • Dividends and buybacks are plumbing, not alchemy: buybacks add value only below intrinsic value, and destroy it when firms buy high — which, empirically, is when they buy most.
  • M&A premiums are certain, synergies are hypotheses; accretion is arithmetic, not proof of value; and in competitive auctions, beware the winner's curse.
