7. Equities: The Stock Market

Stocks are the asset class most people mean when they say "the market," and they're where most long-run wealth in public markets has been created — and occasionally vaporized. By the end of this section you'll know exactly what a share is, where equity returns actually come from, how indices are built, and how to read a stock quote like an analyst instead of a tourist.

What a share actually is

A share of stock is a fractional ownership claim on a corporation. If a company has issued 1 billion shares and you own 100 of them, you own 0.00001% of the business — a ten-millionth of every factory, every patent, every dollar of profit it ever earns. Equity is the formal word for this ownership interest.

Two legal features make this claim interesting. First, shareholders are the residual claimants: employees, suppliers, tax authorities, and lenders all get paid first, and shareholders get whatever is left. That's why equity is riskier than debt — and why it can pay so much more. Second, limited liability caps your downside at what you paid. If the company collapses owing billions, your shares go to zero but nobody comes for your house. Unlimited upside, floored downside — that asymmetry is the deal.

Why would a company sell pieces of itself? Because growing costs money, and equity is money you never have to repay. A founder with a great idea but no capital sells 30% of the company to investors, uses the cash to build the product, and keeps 70% of something big instead of 100% of nothing. The alternative — borrowing — creates a fixed obligation that can bankrupt you in a bad year. Equity investors share the pain of bad years, which is exactly why they demand a share of the good ones.

Analogy

Think of a company's claims as a priority queue. Debt holders enqueued first get dequeued (paid) first, with a fixed payout. Shareholders sit at the back holding a claim on whatever remains — which is why the value of a share is so sensitive to small changes in expectations. If a firm is worth 100 and owes 90, equity is worth 10; if the firm's value rises just 10% to 110, equity doubles to 20.

Market cap, share classes, and dilution

The headline size of a public company is its market capitalization ("market cap"): share price × shares outstanding. A $50 stock with 4 billion shares is a $200 billion company; a $500 stock with 20 million shares is a $10 billion one. This is the first myth to kill: the share price alone tells you nothing about how big or "expensive" a company is. Price per share is just market cap divided by an arbitrary share count — the same pizza cut into 8 slices or 80.

Analysts sort companies into buckets: large-cap (>$10B), mid-cap ($2–10B), and small-cap (<$2B). Size matters because it correlates with risk, liquidity, and analyst coverage.

Not all shares are equal. Companies can issue multiple share classes with different voting rights. Alphabet's Class A shares (GOOGL) carry one vote each, Class C (GOOG) carry none, and the founders' Class B shares carry ten — letting insiders keep control while selling most of the economics. Meta and many tech firms use the same structure. When you see "dual-class," read: the founders answer to almost no one.

Your ownership percentage isn't fixed, either. When a company issues new shares — to raise cash, pay employees in stock, or fund an acquisition — every existing share becomes a smaller fraction of the company. That's dilution. Own 10% of a firm with 1 million shares, and if it issues 250,000 new ones, you now own 100,000 of 1,250,000 shares: 8%. Dilution isn't automatically bad — if the cash raised creates more value than the ownership given up, everyone wins — but stock-based compensation that quietly grows the share count 3% a year is a real cost that headline profits can hide. You'll learn to spot it in Section 11 (Financial Statements).

Finally, most companies aren't on the stock market at all. A public company has shares anyone can buy on an exchange and must file audited reports with the SEC; a private company doesn't. The number of US public companies has roughly halved since the mid-1990s (from ~8,000 to ~4,000): regulation post-Enron made being public costlier, and a flood of private equity and venture money means firms can raise billions without ever listing. Companies now go public later, larger, and more reluctantly — which means more of the early growth accrues to private investors before the public ever gets a ticket.

Where returns come from: price + dividends = total return

Owning a stock pays you in exactly two ways. The price can rise (price appreciation), and the company can mail you cash (a dividend). Add them and you get total return — the only return that matters:

Total return = P1 − P0 + DP0 = price return + dividend yield
P0 = starting price · P1 = ending price · D = dividends received over the period

Dividends look small — a 2–3% dividend yield next to double-digit price swings — but reinvested over decades they are enormous, because reinvesting buys more shares, which pay more dividends, which buy more shares. It's the compounding loop from Section 1 running on two engines at once: your per-share price grows at the price return while your share count grows at the dividend yield, so wealth compounds at roughly (1 + g)(1 + d) − 1.

Worked example

$10,000 in a stock returning 5% in price with a 3% yield, dividends reinvested, for 30 years. The compound rate is 1.05 × 1.03 = 1.0815, i.e. 8.15% per year. After 30 years: $10,000 × 1.081530 ≈ $104,900. Price alone: $10,000 × 1.0530 ≈ $43,219. The "small" dividend, reinvested, ends up responsible for more than half of the final wealth — and your share count has grown 1.0330 ≈ 2.43× along the way.

How much do equities pay for all this risk? Over the last century, US stocks have delivered roughly 7% per year after inflation — the long-run equity risk premium over safe government debt has been around 4–6 percentage points. At 7% real, purchasing power doubles about every 10 years (the Rule of 72 from Section 1). No other broadly accessible asset class comes close over long horizons.

The catch is the ride. Equity volatility runs around 15–20% a year, and the market suffers a drawdown of 30–50%+ roughly once a decade: −86% peak-to-trough in 1929–32, about −50% in 1973–74, 2000–02, and 2008–09, and −34% in five weeks in March 2020. The ~7% real average is earned through those crashes, not around them. That's why time horizon is the single most important variable in equity investing: over one year, stocks are close to a coin flip; over 20–30 years, the historical odds of beating cash have been overwhelming. Money you need next year does not belong in stocks.

Common pitfall

Past return is not a promise. The ~7% real figure is a century-long US average that includes surviving two world wars and winning the economic lottery; Japan's market spent three decades underwater after 1989. History tells you what equities can do and what they charge in volatility — it does not guarantee your particular 20 years will match the average.

Indices and index funds: buying the haystack

A stock index is a rule-based basket of stocks used to measure "the market." The ones you'll hear daily:

Almost every serious index is cap-weighted: each company's weight is its market cap divided by the sum of all members' caps. This is just a weighted average with weights wi = capi ÷ Σcap, and it has a beautiful property — the index holds each company in proportion to its actual size, and it rebalances itself for free as prices move. The Dow, by contrast, is price-weighted: it effectively holds one share of each member, so a company's influence depends on its per-share price, which we just established is meaningless. It survives on brand recognition, not merit.

You can't buy an index directly — it's a formula, not a product. The practical vehicles are index funds and ETFs (exchange-traded funds), which hold the index's stocks at the index's weights and charge a tiny expense ratio — as low as 0.03% per year, versus ~1% for typical actively managed funds. That fee gap, compounded over 30 years, eats roughly a quarter of your final wealth, which is why cheap index funds have absorbed trillions of dollars and why Section 17 (Market Efficiency) will explain how hard it is for active managers to earn their fees back. One ETF share can make you a part-owner of 500 (or 8,000) businesses at once — instant diversification, formalized in Section 15 (Portfolio Theory).

Valuation shorthand: EPS, P/E, and earnings yield

Is a stock cheap or expensive? Not by its price — by its price relative to what you get. The workhorse metrics: EPS (earnings per share) is net profit ÷ shares outstanding, and the P/E ratio is what you pay per dollar of annual profit:

P/E = price per shareEPS  ·  earnings yield = EPSprice = 1P/E
A stock at $184.32 with EPS of $7.49 trades at P/E ≈ 24.6, an earnings yield of ≈ 4.1%

The earnings yield (just 1 ÷ P/E) puts stocks in the same units as bonds: a P/E of 25 means each dollar invested currently produces 4 cents of annual profit. That makes cross-asset comparison natural — is 4.1% of growing corporate earnings better than 4.5% of fixed bond coupons? That question is half of finance.

A high P/E is not "overpriced" and a low P/E is not "a bargain" — a P/E is a claim about the future. Paying 45× current earnings only makes sense if you believe earnings will grow fast; paying 11× says you expect stagnation. This is the classic growth versus value framing: growth investors pay up for tomorrow; value investors demand a discount today. Neither is inherently right — the question is always whether the price already reflects the future you expect.

P/E comes with sharp edges. If earnings are negative, P/E is meaningless (a company losing money has no P/E, not a "low" one). If earnings are cyclical, P/E inverts your intuition: a steel maker at the top of a boom shows huge earnings and a deceptively low P/E right before profits collapse, and a sky-high P/E at the bottom of a recession may mark the cheapest moment to buy. One-off items (a lawsuit settlement, an asset sale) can distort a single year's E. This is exactly why full valuation — discounted cash flows, comparables, and adjustments — gets its own treatment in Section 13 (Valuation).

Corporate actions: dividends, buybacks, splits, and going public

Companies return cash to shareholders two ways. Dividends you've met. A buyback (share repurchase) is the mirror image of dilution: the company buys its own shares on the market and retires them, so every remaining share owns a bigger slice. Suppose a firm earns $1B on 500M shares (EPS $2.00) and buys back 25M shares: the same $1B is now spread over 475M shares, so EPS rises to $2.11 — a 5.3% bump with zero change in the underlying business. In a frictionless world buybacks and dividends are economically equivalent ways to hand cash back; in practice buybacks are more tax-efficient in the US and more flexible (cutting a dividend is a scandal, pausing a buyback is a footnote), which is why buybacks now exceed dividends among large US firms.

A stock split changes nothing real. A 4-for-1 split turns one $500 share into four $125 shares; your stake, the market cap, and the P/E are all identical. Splits exist mainly for optics and round-lot convenience. This buries the myth for good: a $500 stock is not "more expensive" than a $50 one, and a split does not make a company "cheaper" — although markets sometimes pop on split announcements anyway, a small irrationality we'll file under Section 17 (Behavioral Finance).

How do companies get onto the market in the first place? The classic route is the IPO (initial public offering): investment banks (Section 6) value the company, drum up institutional buyers, and sell new shares to the public, collecting fees of ~4–7% — and IPOs are famously often "popped" on day one, meaning the sellers left money on the table.

A direct listing skips the banks' underwriting machine: existing shares simply start trading on the exchange (Spotify in 2018, Coinbase in 2021), which raises no new cash but avoids underpricing and most fees.

A SPAC (special purpose acquisition company) is a publicly listed shell full of cash that hunts for a private company to merge with, taking it public through the back door. SPACs boomed in 2020–21 with lighter disclosure than IPOs, and the average post-merger SPAC investor lost badly — a useful reminder that innovations in market plumbing often redistribute money toward whoever designed the plumbing.

Reading a stock screen like an analyst

Open any quote page and you'll face a wall of numbers. Here's the decoder ring. OHLC is the day's open, high, low, and close — the atomic unit of price data (and, as a time series, the raw material for everything in Section 18). Volume is how many shares traded; a price move on heavy volume carries more conviction than the same move on a trickle. The 52-week range instantly locates today's price in its recent history.

Beta measures how the stock moves with the overall market: beta 1.2 means it tends to move 1.2% for every 1% market move — it's literally the slope coefficient from regressing the stock's returns on the market's, a calculation you could run in three lines of Python (and will, in Section 15). Float is the share count actually available to trade after excluding insiders' locked-up holdings; a small float plus heavy demand is the recipe for violent price moves (see: meme stocks, 2021).

Your edge

Every field on that screen is a column in a dataset. OHLCV time series are freely downloadable, beta is a linear regression, cap weighting is a normalized vector, and total return is a cumulative product. When we hit Section 18 (Quant Finance), you'll compute all of this from raw data — most finance professionals can read the screen; far fewer can rebuild it.

Deep dive: why the share count on a screen can lie to you

"Shares outstanding" comes in flavors. Basic shares count what exists today; diluted shares add everything that could become shares — employee options, restricted stock units, convertible bonds. A company with 100M basic shares and 12M in-the-money options is really a 112M-share company for valuation purposes, and careful analysts always divide by the diluted count. The gap between basic and diluted EPS on an income statement (Section 11) is a quick read on how much of the company is being quietly handed to employees each year.

Float can diverge wildly from shares outstanding. A newly listed company might have 90% of its shares locked up by insiders for six months post-IPO; when the lockup expires, supply floods the market. Checking the float and the lockup calendar is standard diligence before touching a recent IPO.

Check your understanding

1. Stock A trades at $500 with 20 million shares outstanding; Stock B trades at $50 with 4 billion shares. Which company is bigger?

Size is market cap = price × shares. B: $50 × 4B = $200B. A: $500 × 20M = $10B. Price per share alone tells you nothing — it depends on an arbitrary share count.

2. You invest $10,000 at a 5% annual price return with a 3% dividend yield, reinvesting dividends, for 30 years. Your wealth compounds at approximately…

Reinvested returns compound multiplicatively: (1.05)(1.03) − 1 = 8.15%. The extra 0.15% over the naive 8% seems tiny but adds up — the exact 30-year result is ~$104,900 versus $43,219 from price alone.

3. In a price-weighted index like the Dow, which company has the most influence?

Price weighting is effectively holding one share of each member, so influence scales with per-share price — an arbitrary number. A $500 small company outweighs a $50 giant, which is why serious indices are cap-weighted.

4. A steel producer reports record profits at the peak of a construction boom and trades at a P/E of 6. The most careful interpretation is:

Cyclical companies show their lowest P/Es when earnings peak, right before profits fall. A low P/E on boom-time earnings can be more dangerous than a high P/E on trough earnings.

5. A company does a 4-for-1 stock split. What happens to the value of your holding?

A split changes the unit of account, not the business. Market cap, your ownership percentage, and the P/E are all unchanged. Contrast with a buyback, which does change per-share economics by shrinking the share count.

Key takeaways

  • A share is fractional ownership: a residual claim on profits and assets, with losses capped by limited liability — last in line, but with all the upside.
  • Company size is market cap (price × shares), never price per share; splits and share counts are just slicing choices.
  • Total return = price appreciation + dividends. Reinvested dividends compound your share count and can account for over half of long-run wealth.
  • Equities have paid roughly 7% real per year over the last US century — but through 30–50% drawdowns roughly once a decade. The premium is payment for surviving the ride, and past averages are not promises.
  • Serious indices (S&P 500, Russell 2000, MSCI World) are cap-weighted; the price-weighted Dow is a legacy oddity. Index funds and ETFs are the cheap, practical way to own the whole basket.
  • P/E = price per dollar of earnings; its inverse, the earnings yield, makes stocks comparable to bonds. A high P/E is a bet on growth — and P/E breaks on negative or cyclical earnings.
  • Buybacks and dividends both return cash; buybacks shrink the share count and lift EPS. Dilution does the opposite. IPOs, direct listings, and SPACs are just three doors into the public market.
  • On a stock screen, check internal consistency: price ÷ EPS should equal the P/E, beta is a regression slope, and float — not shares outstanding — is what actually trades.
