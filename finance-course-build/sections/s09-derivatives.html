9. Derivatives: Futures, Options and Swaps

Derivatives are contracts whose value is computed from something else — a stock, a bond, a barrel of oil, an interest rate. Master this section and you will be able to read an option chain, price out a hedge, and explain why the same instrument that saves a farmer's season can vaporize a hedge fund.

A contract about a price, not the thing itself

A derivative is a legal agreement whose payoff is a function of some other price. That other price — the stock, the commodity, the interest rate — is called the underlying. You never need to own the underlying to trade the derivative, any more than you need to own a database to write a query against it.

Analogy

Think of a derivative as a derived column: it stores no data of its own, just a formula like payoff = max(price − 100, 0) evaluated against a live feed. All the machinery in this section is about who gets paid when that formula is evaluated, and when.

Two kinds of people show up to trade these contracts, and both are necessary. A hedger already carries a risk — a farmer exposed to wheat prices, an airline exposed to jet fuel — and uses the contract to shed it. A speculator has no such exposure and takes the other side hoping to profit. Speculators get the bad press, but without them hedgers often couldn't find a counterparty at a fair price. Speculators are the liquidity providers of the risk-transfer business.

Two properties make derivatives different from the assets in Section 7 (Equities) and Section 8 (Fixed Income):

They are zero-sum before costs. Every derivative has two sides, and one side's gain is exactly the other side's loss. A stock can make both buyer and (earlier) seller money over time because the company itself creates value; a futures contract cannot. After commissions and spreads, the average derivative trader is slightly underwater by construction. That is fine — for a hedger, the "loss" is an insurance premium happily paid.

They embed enormous leverage. A few thousand dollars of collateral can control a six-figure exposure. Leverage scales your P&L in both directions with the same multiplier: a 3% move in the underlying can be a 60% move in your account. Derivatives are professional risk-transfer tools first; the retail lottery-ticket use case came later, and most retail option buyers lose money. Keep that ordering in mind for the rest of this section.

Key idea

Derivatives don't create or destroy risk — they relocate it, from someone who doesn't want it to someone who is paid to hold it. Pricing a derivative is pricing that relocation.

Forwards and futures: locking a price today

The oldest derivative is a promise. A forward is a private contract: I agree today to sell you 50,000 bushels of wheat in December at $6.00 per bushel, whatever the market price is then. No money changes hands now; we just shake on the price.

Worked example: the farmer and the flour mill

A farmer will harvest 50,000 bushels in December and needs $5.20/bu to cover costs. Today's December price is $6.00. She sells forward at $6.00, locking in revenue of 50,000 × $6.00 = $300,000 and a profit of $40,000.

If December spot wheat is $5.00: she sells her crop for $250,000, but her forward position gains ($6.00 − $5.00) × 50,000 = $50,000. Total: $300,000. If spot is $7.00: crop fetches $350,000, forward loses $50,000. Total: $300,000 again. The hedge doesn't make her rich — it makes her predictable. The airline hedging jet fuel runs the same trade in mirror image, buying forward to cap its costs.

Forwards have two problems: you must find a counterparty who wants your exact size and date, and you must trust them to pay up in December — counterparty risk. A futures contract solves both. It is a forward that has been standardized (fixed size, fixed delivery months, fixed quality specs) and listed on an exchange like the CME, with a clearinghouse inserted in the middle so that neither side ever faces the other directly.

The clearinghouse protects itself with two mechanisms. First, both sides post margin — a good-faith deposit, typically 3–12% of the contract's value. Second, positions are marked to market every day: each evening, that day's gain or loss is moved in cash between the two margin accounts. Losses are never allowed to pile up unpaid. If your balance falls below the maintenance margin, you get a margin call: top up or be liquidated. Figure 9.1 walks through a week of this for our farmer.

That Wednesday row is the hidden cost of hedging with futures: even a hedge that is guaranteed to work out at delivery can demand cash today. Being "right eventually" doesn't help if you can't fund the margin calls along the way. The practical differences in one line each: futures are standardized, exchange-traded, clearinghouse-guaranteed, marked to market daily, and easy to exit; forwards are custom, private, carry counterparty risk, settle once at the end, and are hard to exit early. Most physical commodity futures are closed out before delivery — hedgers usually want the price protection, not the truckloads of wheat (more on commodities in Section 10).

Options: the right, but not the obligation

A future obligates both sides. An option is asymmetric: the buyer gets a right, the seller takes on an obligation. A call is the right to buy the underlying at a fixed price; a put is the right to sell. That fixed price is the strike, the deadline is the expiry, and the price you pay up front for the right is the premium. The seller — called the writer — pockets the premium and must perform if the buyer exercises. US equity options cover 100 shares per contract, so a quoted premium of $3.02 costs $302.

Because the buyer will only ever exercise when it helps them, option payoffs are kinked — the famous hockey sticks in Figure 9.2. A call buyer's loss is capped at the premium while the upside is open-ended; the call writer's profile is the exact mirror, capped gain and open-ended loss. Zero-sum, always.

An option's value at any moment splits into two parts. Intrinsic value is what exercising immediately would earn: for a call, max(S − K, 0). Time value is everything above that — payment for the possibility that things improve before expiry. Related jargon: moneyness. A call with the stock above the strike is in the money (ITM); stock near the strike is at the money (ATM); stock below the strike leaves the call out of the money (OTM) — pure time value, pure hope.

Worked example: leverage cuts both ways

Buy the 100-strike call at $3.02 ($302 per contract). If the stock closes at $110 at expiry, the call is worth $10.00 — a gain of $6.98 per share, or +231%, versus +10% for the stock. If the stock closes at $100 (unchanged!), the call expires worthless: −100% versus 0% for the stock. The option didn't just lever the direction — it added a deadline. You now need to be right about what, how much, and by when. That triple hurdle is why most retail option buyers lose money even when their directional view is decent.

What an option is worth: volatility and the Greeks

Why is the ATM call above worth $3.02 rather than $1 or $8? Intuitively: an option is worth the probability-weighted average of its payoffs. Anything that widens the distribution of possible stock prices — more time, more volatility — raises the value of a contract that keeps the upside and discards the downside. Practitioners summarize an option's sensitivities with the Greeks, and if you know calculus you already know what they are: partial derivatives of price with respect to each input.

Delta (∂V/∂S) is the slope: a 0.53-delta call gains about $0.53 when the stock gains $1. Delta also doubles as a rough probability of finishing ITM — see the chain above, where the deep ITM call has delta 0.79 and the OTM call 0.27. Theta (∂V/∂t) is time decay: the option bleeds value every day the stock fails to move, and the bleed accelerates near expiry (Figure 9.3). Vega (∂V/∂σ) is sensitivity to volatility itself.

Of the pricing inputs — stock price, strike, time, interest rate, volatility — only one is not directly observable: future volatility. So volatility is the thing option markets actually trade. Quote an option price and I can invert the pricing model to recover the volatility that price implies — the implied volatility in the chain above. When traders say options are "expensive," they mean implied volatility is high relative to what they expect to be realized.

The model everyone inverts is Black-Scholes. You do not need its derivation (that lives in Section 18 (Quant Finance)); you need to know it exists, what goes in, and that it is trivially implementable.

Deep dive: Black-Scholes in ten lines
C = S·N(d1) − K·e−rT·N(d2)
C = call price · S = stock price · K = strike · r = risk-free rate · T = years to expiry · N = standard normal CDF
d1 = ln(S ÷ K) + (r + σ²/2)·Tσ·√T   d2 = d1 − σ·√T
σ = annualized volatility · put price via put-call parity: P = C − S + K·e−rT

As a CS person you can ship this before lunch: N(x) = 0.5 * (1 + erf(x / sqrt(2))) using the error function in any math library, then the two formulas above — about ten lines of Python. Sanity check: S=100, K=100, T=30/365, r=4%, σ=25% gives C ≈ $3.02 and P ≈ $2.69, matching the option chain earlier. Implied volatility is the inverse problem: given a market price, root-find for σ (bisection or Newton's method — the vega is the derivative you need). Note what the buyer's edge must be: the formula assumes volatility; if you pay 25% implied and the stock only realizes 18%, you overpaid even if the model is perfect.

Black-Scholes also gives closed-form Greeks: the call delta is simply N(d1) — which is where the 0.79 / 0.53 / 0.27 column in the chain came from.

Strategies: composing hockey sticks

Options are payoff Lego. Because each leg is a simple kinked function, you can compose positions whose expiry P&L has almost any shape — and you should always look at that shape before trading. Three canonical compositions:

A covered call = own the stock + write a call. You collect the premium as income but surrender any gains above the strike. A protective put = own the stock + buy a put: an insurance policy with the premium as the deductible-plus-premium cost and the strike as the floor. A straddle = buy a call and a put at the same strike: you don't know direction, you're betting on magnitude — a volatility trade. And the mirror image of insurance is the cash-secured put: sell someone else downside protection and keep the premium if it isn't needed.

Common pitfall: selling options is not free money

Short option strategies win small amounts very often — the writer keeps the premium in most months — which makes the P&L history look like a beautiful, smooth, upward line. Right up until it isn't. The distribution is extremely skewed: frequent small gains, rare catastrophic losses. Traders call it picking up pennies in front of a steamroller. Funds that sold volatility this way (see the February 2018 "Volmageddon" episode, and Section 17 for the general pattern) lost years of gains in days. If a strategy's backtest never loses, ask what tail it is short — your statistics background is exactly the edge here: always look at the full distribution, not the mean.

Swaps: trading cash-flow streams

A swap is an agreement to exchange one stream of cash flows for another. The workhorse is the interest-rate swap: one side pays a fixed rate, the other pays a floating rate that resets with the market — today usually SOFR, the benchmark that replaced LIBOR. Both legs are computed on a notional amount that is never actually exchanged — it is just the number the percentages multiply. Headlines about "hundreds of trillions in derivatives outstanding" are quoting notionals; the actual net cash at risk is a tiny fraction of that.

Worked example: fixing a floating loan

TechCo borrowed $100M from its bank at SOFR + 1.25%, floating. The CFO can't budget around a rate that resets constantly, so TechCo enters a swap: pay 4.00% fixed, receive SOFR, on a $100M notional. Only the net moves each period:

Year 1, SOFR averages 3.50%: TechCo owes 4.00% and is owed 3.50%, so it pays the dealer the net 0.50% = $500,000. Year 2, SOFR averages 4.50%: now TechCo receives the net 0.50% = $500,000. Combine the swap with the loan and the SOFR terms cancel: TechCo's all-in cost is 4.00% + 1.25% = 5.25% fixed, forever, no matter what the Fed does. Same trick, reversed, is how banks and insurers manage the rate risk in their bond portfolios.

The other swap you must know is the credit default swap (CDS): insurance on a bond. The protection buyer pays a periodic fee (say 1% of notional per year); if the referenced borrower defaults, the seller covers the loss. Useful in moderation — a lender can hedge a loan without ending the relationship, and CDS spreads are a real-time market vote on credit risk, often faster than rating agencies.

Real world: CDS and 2008

Two features made CDS the star of the 2008 crisis. You can buy protection on bonds you don't own — like insuring your neighbor's house — so total CDS notional grew to a multiple of the underlying debt. And because CDS were then private, uncleared contracts with little margin, sellers could write essentially unlimited protection. AIG's financial products unit sold protection on mortgage securities against which it held almost no reserves; when housing cracked, collateral calls arrived it could not meet, and the US government committed roughly $182 billion so AIG's failure wouldn't cascade through every counterparty. Post-crisis reforms pushed standard swaps into clearinghouses with daily margin — futures-style plumbing, precisely because that plumbing worked. The full story is in Section 17 (Crises).

Your edge

A swap is just two projected cash-flow vectors netted against each other, and its fair value is the difference of their present values — discounting you already know from Section 1. Swap desks literally hire people to build and maintain those discount curves in code. If you can write clean numerical Python, this corner of finance is one of the most natural entry points (see Section 20).

Check your understanding

1. A farmer shorts wheat futures at $6.00 to hedge her harvest, and by delivery wheat trades at $6.80. Which statement is right?

The hedge locks the price in both directions: the $0.80 futures loss is offset by an extra $0.80 on the physical crop. She gave up the upside — that is the cost of certainty, not a mistake. And no one is exempt from margin.

2. You are long one crude oil futures contract (1,000 barrels) bought at $80. Today's settlement price is $78. What happens to your margin account today?

Mark-to-market is unconditional: a $2 drop × 1,000 barrels = $2,000 leaves your account this evening. Whether you then get a margin call depends on where that leaves your balance relative to maintenance margin.

3. You paid $6 for a call with a $100 strike. At expiry the stock is at $104. Which is true?

Moneyness and profit are different questions. Intrinsic value at expiry is max(104 − 100, 0) = $4, so you exercise (worthless it is not) — but you paid $6 for it, so P&L = 4 − 6 = −$2. Breakeven was $106.

4. A strategy of selling out-of-the-money puts has been profitable 34 of the last 36 months. What is the most statistically sound reaction?

Short-option P&L is heavily skewed — the mean of the distribution can be negative even with a 94% win rate if the rare losses are large enough. "Picking up pennies in front of a steamroller" is a statement about tails, and hit rate ignores tails.

5. A firm pays 3% fixed and receives SOFR on a $50M notional swap. SOFR averages 2% this year. What cash actually moves?

Swaps settle net: 3% owed minus 2% received = 1% of $50M = $500,000 paid by the firm. The notional is only a reference number. And paying fixed while receiving floating hurts when floating rates are low — the fixed payer wins when rates rise.

Key takeaways

  • A derivative's value is a formula evaluated against an underlying price. It relocates risk from hedgers to speculators — both roles are essential — and it is zero-sum before costs.
  • Futures lock in a price today via a standardized, clearinghouse-guaranteed contract; daily mark-to-market and margin calls mean even a "correct" hedge can demand cash along the way.
  • Options are asymmetric: buyers pay a premium for capped-loss, open-ended-gain payoffs; writers accept the mirror image. Value = intrinsic + time value, and the deadline is half the bet.
  • The Greeks are just partial derivatives: delta is the slope versus the stock, theta the daily time decay (which accelerates near expiry), vega the sensitivity to volatility — the one input the market actually trades, via implied volatility and Black-Scholes.
  • Strategies compose kinked payoffs: covered calls trade upside for income, protective puts buy a floor, straddles bet on magnitude rather than direction. Judge every strategy by breakeven, max gain, max loss.
  • Selling options harvests frequent small gains against rare huge losses — a skewed distribution that a win rate cannot summarize. Most retail option buyers lose money; these are professional hedging tools first.
  • Swaps exchange cash-flow streams on a notional that never moves; an interest-rate swap converts floating debt to fixed. CDS are default insurance whose unmargined excesses helped detonate 2008 — and why swaps now clear like futures.
